## Object Description

A collection of **first-generation carbon-filament incandescent electric lamps** displayed in a three-tiered mahogany rack (445 × 265 mm), acquired almost entirely at the **First International Exposition of Electricity, Paris, August–November 1881**, with a small number of Siemens lamps added in 1882. The ~20 bulbs represent the five principal competing manufacturers of the era: Thomas A. Edison, Joseph Wilson Swan, St. George Lane Fox-Pitt, Hiram Stevens Maxim, and Siemens & Halske (Berlin).

Lamps were purchased and subsequently tested by Teylers Museum's curator of the Physical Cabinet, **Elisa van der Ven** (1833–1909), who measured light output, temperature, and electrical resistance and published his findings in **1882** — the **first published scientific study of incandescent lamps in the Netherlands**. In 1883 Van der Ven used the lamps to propose electric lighting for the new Lecture Hall, a proposal rejected by museum leadership.

At the 1881 exposition, a testing committee (including William Crookes) ranked lamp efficiency: 1st Edison, 2nd Lane-Fox, 3rd Swan, 4th Maxim. The rack assembles exactly these five competing systems.

## Key Findings

### 1. "H.P. MAXIM" IS THE WRONG PERSON

The catalog credits "H.P. Maxim" as the lamp maker. **Hiram Percy Maxim** (1869–1936) — the H.P. — was 12 years old in 1881. The Paris exhibitor was his father **Hiram Stevens Maxim** (1840–1916), who held 17 U.S. lamp patents and represented the United States Electric Lighting Company at the exposition. The wrong member of the Maxim family has been entered in the record.

### 2. "G. LANE-FOX" — WRONG SURNAME FORM AND TRUNCATED GIVEN NAME

The catalog uses "G. Lane-Fox." The inventor's correct name at the time of the 1881 exhibition is **St. George Lane Fox-Pitt** (1856–1932) — he adopted the hyphenated surname "Lane Fox-Pitt" in May 1880, before the exposition. "Lane-Fox" is the pre-1880 form. "G." truncates his distinctive given name "St. George." The record should read "St. George Lane Fox-Pitt."

### 3. "VON W. SIEMENS" IS ANACHRONISTIC AND MISATTRIBUTES THE LAMPS

The catalog lists "von W. Siemens" as maker. The "von" particle was granted to Werner Siemens only in **1888** by Emperor Friedrich III — seven years after the Paris exposition. In 1881–1882 he was **Ernst Werner Siemens**, and the lamps were a product of the firm **Siemens & Halske** (Berlin), not a personal invention. The anachronistic "von" and personal attribution to Werner rather than the company are both errors.

### 4. "VAN DER E. VEN" — GARBLED NAME ENTRY

The catalog renders the researcher's name as "van der E. Ven" — placing the initial in the middle of the surname, producing a nonsensical string. The correct name is **Elisa van der Ven** (1833–1909): given name "Elisa" (initial "E."), surname "van der Ven." The Teylers Museum's own topstuk page uses "Eliza van der Ven."

### 5. VAN DER VEN'S ROLE UNDERSTATED AS "ONDERZOEKER" (RESEARCHER)

Van der Ven was the **curator of the Physical Cabinet** who **personally acquired** the lamps at the Paris 1881 exposition, systematically tested them, and **published the comparative results in 1882**. His role was threefold: institutional acquirer, systematic tester, and author of the first Dutch scientific study of incandescent lamps. "Researcher" omits the crucial provenance fact that he is the acquisition agent.

### 6. NO PROVENANCE / ACQUISITION NARRATIVE

The Adlib catalog contains no provenance. The museum's own topstuk page documents that Van der Ven purchased "nearly all" the lamps at the **First International Exposition of Electricity, Paris, 1881** — entirely absent from the formal record.

### 7. NO DESCRIPTION

No descriptive text: number of bulbs (~20), shapes (spherical, pear, cylindrical, faceted), filament types (carbon loop, hairpin, straight), rack construction, or maker labels. The record cannot function as a standalone document.

### 8. 1881 PARIS EXPOSITION CONTEXT AND TEST RANKINGS ABSENT

The 1881 exposition was the defining context for this collection. The testing committee's efficiency ranking (1st Edison, 2nd Lane-Fox, 3rd Swan, 4th Maxim) explains why exactly these five makers' lamps were assembled together. This is entirely absent.

### 9. VAN DER VEN'S 1882 PUBLICATION NOT CITED

Van der Ven published his comparative test results in 1882 — the first published Dutch scientific study of incandescent lamps. The catalog does not cite or reference this publication, which is directly related to the object.

### 10. "J.W. Swan" gives only initials

Full name: **Sir Joseph Wilson Swan** (1828–1914). Minor incompleteness rather than factual error.
