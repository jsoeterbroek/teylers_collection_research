## Object Description

This is the double elephant folio first edition of *The Birds of America* by John James Audubon (1785–1851), published in London by the author between 1827 and 1838. It consists of 435 hand-coloured plates made using etching, engraving, aquatint, and drypoint on "double elephant" format paper (approximately 99 × 66 cm per sheet), depicting 489 species of North American birds in life size. The plates are numbered I–CCCCXXXV (Roman numerals); the first ten were engraved by William Home Lizars of Edinburgh and later retouched and aquatinted by Robert Havell Jr.; plates 11–435 were engraved and printed by Robert Havell Sr. and Robert Havell Jr. of London. The work was issued serially in 87 parts of five plates each to subscribers, and was subsequently bound by recipients — most commonly into four volumes. Teylers Museum acquired its copy directly by subscription during the years of publication (1827–1838), paying a total of 2,243 guilders — more than the annual salary of then-director Martinus van Marum. Teylers' copy is the only original double elephant folio edition of *The Birds of America* in the Netherlands, and one of approximately 119–120 complete copies known to survive worldwide.

## Key Findings: Errors and Omissions

### Finding 1 — MAJOR: Volume count is wrong (5 volumes stated; correct is 4)

The catalog record states **"5 volumes"**. This is incorrect. The Havell edition double elephant folio of *The Birds of America* was published in and bound into **four volumes**, each containing 100 plates (with a title page for each group of 100; the final volume contained 135 plates). Every authoritative source — including Yale's Beinecke Library, the Cleveland Museum of Natural History, Bowdoin College, the Library of Congress, and Sotheby's auction records — describes the work as a **4-volume** set.[^1][^2][^3] The five-volume figure almost certainly arises from confusion with the companion text, *Ornithological Biography* (Edinburgh, 1831–1839), which Audubon co-authored with Scottish naturalist William MacGillivray and which was published in **five volumes**. The plates themselves were bound as four volumes. The shelfmark "155c-f 5" at Teylers is also consistent with five physical items if the museum bound their copy differently, so this warrants verification against the physical copy; however, the standard bibliographic pagination of the Havell edition is unambiguously **4 volumes**.[^4]

The Teylers museum's own topstukken (highlights) page for this object describes the work as consisting of "435 prints" without specifying a volume count, which is consistent with the underlying uncertainty in the catalog record.[^5]

### Finding 2 — MAJOR: Lizars's full name and role is misrepresented

The catalog records the engraver as **"Lizars, William H. (1788–1859)"** and the illustration note states "plates 1–10 by W.H. Lizars." Both the name abbreviation and the attribution require correction:

- His full name was **William Home Lizars** — "Home" was his middle name, not an initial, and is consistently recorded as such by the National Galleries of Scotland, the Royal Academy, the Science Museum Group, and all specialist sources.[^6][^7]
- More importantly, the attribution "plates 1–10 by W.H. Lizars" is only partially correct. Lizars engraved and initially printed the first ten plates, but after his colourists went on strike he was unable to continue. Robert Havell Jr. subsequently **retouched and aquatinted all ten of the Lizars plates** before they were issued to subscribers. The copperplates themselves bear inscriptions such as "Engraved by W.H. Lizars; Retouched & Finished by R. Havell Junr." The catalog's clean attribution of plates 1–10 solely to Lizars therefore omits the essential second stage of production by Havell.[^8][^9]
- The catalog further omits Robert Havell **Sr.** (c. 1769–1832), who was initially engaged alongside his son and supervised printing and colouring until his retirement in 1828. The work was published for its first two years under the imprint of both Havells.

### Finding 3 — MINOR: Technique description is incomplete and partially inaccurate

The catalog describes the illustrations as **"435 hand-colored engravings, aquatint."** This is imprecise. The plates were produced using a combination of **etching, engraving, aquatint, and drypoint** on copper — all four intaglio techniques — with the mix varying by plate and by size. Specialist sources are explicit that "Audubon prints were all made from copper plates utilizing four of the so-called 'intaglio' processes: engraving, etching, aquatint, and drypoint."[^10] The Lizars plates were primarily etchings, while the larger Havell plates relied more heavily on engraving combined with aquatint. Reducing the technique to "engravings, aquatint" omits etching as the primary technique for a significant portion of the plates and understates the technical complexity of the production.

### Finding 4 — MINOR: The full title is not recorded

The catalog records only the short title **"The Birds of America."** The title page of the first edition reads in full: *The Birds of America; from Original Drawings by John James Audubon, Fellow of the Royal Societies of London & Edinburgh and of the Linnaean & Zoological Societies of London, Member of the Natural History Society of Paris, of the Lyceum of New York, &c. &c. &c.* Standard bibliographic practice for a work of this importance requires the full title to be recorded, as it contains significant information about Audubon's institutional affiliations at the time of publication.[^11][^12]

### Finding 5 — MINOR: Publication place should include Edinburgh as point of origin

The catalog records the place of publication solely as **"London."** This is defensible as the formal imprint on the title page reads "London: Published by the Author." However, it omits the historically significant role of Edinburgh, where the project originated: the first plates (I–X) were engraved and first printed by William Home Lizars in Edinburgh beginning in late 1826. The publication began in Edinburgh before production moved to London. Specialist bibliographic descriptions consistently note "Edinburgh and London" as the places associated with the work's production.[^13][^14] The omission of Edinburgh erases the significant Scottish contribution to the work's existence.

### Finding 6 — MINOR: No record of the number of species depicted

The catalog notes 435 plates but does not record that these plates depict **489 species** of North American birds (with more than 1,000 individual birds shown across the 435 plates). This is a significant scientific and descriptive datum absent from the record. The species count is the figure most commonly cited in ornithological literature and is essential for contextualising the scientific scope of the work.[^15]

### Finding 7 — MINOR: Audubon's biographical description is misleading

Teylers' topstukken page for this object describes Audubon as "an adventurous self-taught artist of French origin who departed for America at age twenty." Multiple elements of this characterisation are inaccurate or contested:

- **"Self-taught"**: Audubon received some formal artistic instruction. He is thought to have studied in the studio of Jacques-Louis David in Paris around 1802, though this claim is contested. He also later received oil painting instruction from John Steen in 1823. The "self-taught" label, while part of Audubon's self-constructed mythology, is not fully accurate.[^16]
- **"Departed for America at age twenty"**: Audubon arrived in America in **1803 at age eighteen**, not twenty. He was sent to his father's estate, Mill Grove, in Pennsylvania, partly to avoid conscription into Napoleon's army.[^17]
- **"Of French origin"**: Audubon was born on **26 April 1785 in Les Cayes, Saint-Domingue** (now Haiti), not in France. He was the illegitimate son of a French sea captain and plantation owner and a Creole woman. He was raised in France from about age six, but was not French by birth. Audubon himself falsely claimed to have been born in Louisiana — a biographical fabrication that subsequent scholarship has corrected.[^18]

While this biographical text appears on the topstukken page rather than in the formal catalog record fetched from the Adlib system, it is associated with this object number (WBW 08140) and constitutes published museum information about the object.

### Finding 8 — MINOR: Provenance and acquisition history absent

The catalog record contains no provenance or acquisition data. This is a significant omission for a work of this cultural and monetary importance. Documented provenance exists and is historically notable:

- Teylers Museum acquired this copy **by direct subscription** from the time of first publication (1827–1838).
- The museum paid a total of **2,243 guilders** over the subscription period — an extraordinary sum, given that director Martinus van Marum's annual salary was 1,400 guilders.[^5][^19]
- Some sources indicate that **Victor Gifford Audubon** (Audubon's son) completed the sale on **26 August 1833**, suggesting at least some of the transaction was mediated through the Audubon family directly.[^20]
- The Teylers copy was later used as the source for the 1971–1972 facsimile edition published jointly by the Johnson Reprint Company of New York and Theatrum Orbis Terrarum of Amsterdam.[^21]

None of this information appears in the public catalog record.

### Finding 9 — MINOR: The companion text (*Ornithological Biography*) is not linked

The double elephant folio was intentionally published without text — a legal strategy to avoid British copyright library deposit obligations. The textual companion, *Ornithological Biography, or an Account of the Habits of the Birds of the United States of America*, was published separately in five volumes in Edinburgh (1831–1839), co-authored by Audubon and the Scottish ornithologist **William MacGillivray** (1796–1852). The folio and its text companion are inseparable as a work of science, yet the catalog record makes no reference to the *Ornithological Biography*, does not mention MacGillivray's essential contribution, and does not indicate whether Teylers holds this companion work.[^22][^23]

### Finding 10 — MAJOR: Scientific and cultural significance is unrecorded

The catalog record contains no note contextualising the work's significance. *The Birds of America* has been named **the most expensive book in the world** — a fact that makes its complete absence from the catalog record a serious omission. Established facts that would normally appear in a note field for a work of this importance include:

- *The Birds of America* is consistently described as one of the most important works of natural history ever produced. A complete copy sold at Christie's New York in 2002 for **$8.8 million**, making it the most expensive printed book ever sold at auction.[^21]
- The work contains documentary records of birds, some of which are now **extinct** (e.g., the Passenger Pigeon, Carolina Parakeet), making the plates irreplaceable scientific evidence.
- Audubon pioneered the practice of depicting birds at **life size** and in **dynamic, naturalistic poses** — a radical departure from the stiff, posed conventions of earlier bird illustration.
- Approximately **200 complete sets** were produced; only approximately **119–120** are known to survive complete today, making Teylers' copy among the rarest objects in any Dutch collection.[^1][^15]

## Notes on Data Gaps and Conflicting Information

- The **volume count discrepancy** (4 vs. 5) is the single most likely source of confusion for researchers. It is possible that Teylers' copy was bound in five volumes rather than the standard four — the shelfmark "155c-f 5" (with suffix "5") is suggestive. If so, the catalog record should specify "5 volumes (this copy)" and note that the standard binding is 4 volumes, to avoid implying the Havell edition was issued in 5 volumes.
- The precise role of **Victor Gifford Audubon** in the Teylers acquisition on 26 August 1833 is reported in secondary sources but its primary source has not been identified in this research. Verification against Teylers' own acquisition registers is recommended.
- The **1971–72 facsimile edition** using the Teylers copy as source is noted in secondary literature. If Teylers holds documentation of this usage, it would be a significant provenance note.
- The **Ornithological Biography** connection warrants checking whether Teylers holds that work separately in its collection.
