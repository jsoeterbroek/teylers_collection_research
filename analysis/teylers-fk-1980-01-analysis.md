## Object Description

This is a **Gregorian reflecting telescope** made by the Van der Bildt workshop in Franeker, Friesland — one of the most prolific telescope-making dynasties in 18th-century Europe. The patriarch **Jan Pieters van der Bildt** (1709–1791) manufactured over **550 reflecting telescopes** during his career, rivaling the output of James Short of London. According to a Teylers Museum article, this instrument is a remarkable **dual-system telescope** that can function in both Gregorian mode (concave secondary) and Cassegrain mode (convex secondary), with a brass tube of 630 mm (780 mm with extension), a primary mirror of 108 mm diameter in speculum metal, and Huygens eyepieces including a micrometer eyepiece. It is displayed in vitrine VIII of the Oval Room.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. MANUFACTURING DATE MISSING — "(1663)" IS MISLEADING

The title includes "(1663)" which refers to **James Gregory's telescope design date**, not the date of this instrument. The catalog provides **no manufacturing date**. Based on the maker's active period:

- If by **Jan Pieters van der Bildt** (the elder): c. **1745–1791**
- If by **Jan van der Bildt the younger** (who also signed "J. van der Bildt"): c. **1768–1780**
- An antique dealer has dated a comparable Van der Bildt Franeker telescope to c. 1745–1760

The "(1663)" in the title could mislead a reader into thinking the telescope is from 1663 — over 80 years before the Van der Bildt workshop existed.

### 2. THE VAN DER BILDT DYNASTY: PROLIFIC TELESCOPE MAKERS

The catalog says only "J. v.d. Bildt, Franeker." This was one of Europe's most important telescope-making workshops:

**Jan Pieters van der Bildt** (1709–1791):
- Born in Vrouwenparochie (Het Bildt), Friesland; son of a carpenter
- Self-taught in watchmaking and lens/mirror grinding
- Became **instrument maker and conservator at Franeker University**
- Manufactured **over 550 reflecting telescopes** — an enormous output
- His telescopes resembled those of **James Short** of London, the foremost Gregorian maker of the era
- Enjoyed the patronage of **Stadhouder Willem IV**
- Signed instruments "J. VAN DER BILDT FRANEKER"

**His sons and grandson continued the tradition:**
- **Jan van der Bildt the younger** (1736–c. 1780): Made telescopes independently from c. 1768; known numbers 20–63
- **Lubbertus van der Bildt** (active c. 1760–1780): Known examples up to No. 143
- **Bauke Eisma van der Bildt II** (1753–1831): Grandson; appointed **professor of physics and astronomy at Franeker University** in 1806; perfected lens production methods

### 3. DUAL GREGORIAN/CASSEGRAIN SYSTEM NOT MENTIONED

According to a Teylers Museum article (DocPlayer), this telescope can function in **both Gregorian and Cassegrain modes**:

- **Gregorian mode**: Concave secondary mirror placed beyond the primary's focal point → erect image
- **Cassegrain mode**: Convex secondary mirror placed inside the focal point → inverted image, shorter tube

This dual capability makes the instrument more significant than a standard Gregorian reflector. The catalog makes no mention of this feature.

### 4. JAMES GREGORY (1638–1675): BIOGRAPHY NOT PROVIDED

The catalog references "James Gregory (1663)" without context. Gregory was:

- Scottish mathematician and astronomer, born in Drumoak near Aberdeen
- Published *Optica Promota* (1663) at age 24, describing the reflecting telescope design
- Discovered Taylor series 40+ years before Taylor; proved differentiation and integration are inverse operations
- First Regius Professor of Mathematics at the University of St Andrews (1668)
- Discovered diffraction using a bird feather — the first diffraction grating
- Died of a stroke in 1675, aged 36, while demonstrating Jupiter's moons to students

The first working Gregorian telescope was built by **Robert Hooke** in 1673–1674.

### 5. MATERIALS AND DIMENSIONS NOT LISTED

The catalog provides no materials or dimensions. From the Teylers Museum article:
- **Tube**: Brass, 630 mm (780 mm with extension)
- **Primary mirror**: Speculum metal, 108 mm diameter
- **Secondary mirrors**: Speculum metal (concave for Gregorian, convex for Cassegrain)
- **Eyepieces**: Huygens type, including a micrometer eyepiece
- **Mount**: Azimuthal

### 6. FRANEKER'S REMARKABLE SCIENTIFIC CULTURE

Franeker (population ~13,000) produced two extraordinary contributions to astronomy in the 18th century:
- The **Van der Bildt telescope dynasty** (550+ telescopes)
- **Eise Eisinga's planetarium** (1774–1781), the oldest working planetarium in the world, now **UNESCO World Heritage** (inscribed 2023)

Van der Bildt Jr.'s telescope No. 20 is housed in the Eisinga Planetarium. Both families were part of the same small-town scientific culture. This context is absent from the catalog.
