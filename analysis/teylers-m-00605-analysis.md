## Object Description

M 00605 is a substantial malachite specimen (409.6 g) from the Ural Mountains of Russia, collected during the 18th century and now displayed in the Oval Hall (Ovale Zaal) of Teylers Museum. Malachite is a copper carbonate hydroxide mineral with the formula Cu₂(CO₃)(OH)₂, crystallising in the monoclinic crystal system, with a hardness of 3.5–4 on the Mohs scale. It is characterised by its vivid green colour with distinctive concentric banding patterns formed by the alternating growth of botryoidal or stalactitic masses.

The catalog image shows a spectacular specimen displaying the mineral's characteristic vivid green colour with concentric banding in lighter and darker green, visible on a polished cross-section that occupies most of the specimen's upper surface. The base is brownish-red, likely a gossan or iron oxide host rock typical of Ural copper deposits. The specimen is visually striking and representative of the finest-quality malachite extracted from the Ural Mountains during the 18th century.

The specimen has a documented and distinguished provenance. It was originally part of the mineral collection of the Dutch naturalist **Johannes le Francq van Berkhey** (1729–1812), a physician, painter, poet, and natural historian best known for his *Natuurlyke Historie van Holland* (4 vols., 1769–1776). Van Berkhey's collection was illustrated by **Martinus Houttuyn** (1720–1798), who included an engraving of this very malachite specimen in his encyclopedic work *Natuurlyke Historie of uitvoerige Beschryving der Dieren, Planten en Mineraalen, volgens het Samenstel van den Heer Linnaeus* (37 volumes, 1761–1785). When van Berkhey's collection was forced onto the market following his dismissal from Leiden University in 1795, Teylers Museum director **Martinus van Marum** (1750–1837) acquired the specimen for the museum. It has remained in the Teylers collection ever since and is displayed in the Oval Hall.

## Key Findings

### 1. GEOGRAPHIC CLASSIFICATION IS WRONG: "SIBERIA" IS INCORRECT

The catalog classifies the collection location as **"Asia \| Russia \| Siberia \| Ural Federal District \| Yekaterinburg."** The Ural Mountains are not in Siberia. They form the boundary between Europe and Asia, and the Ural Federal District (where Yekaterinburg is located) is geographically and administratively distinct from Siberia, which lies to the east. The Ural mountain range runs roughly north-south through Russia and is considered the eastern boundary of Europe; the primary malachite-producing mines (at Nizhny Tagil, Gumeshki near Yekaterinburg, and elsewhere in the region) are in the **Ural region**, not in Siberia.

The correct classification should be: **Europe/Asia boundary \| Russia \| Ural Mountains (Uralskiy Federal'nyy Okrug)**, or simply: **Russia, Ural Mountains**. The use of "Siberia" as a parent geographic category for an Ural specimen is geographically incorrect and will mislead researchers.

### 2. PROVENANCE IS ENTIRELY ABSENT

The catalog provides no provenance information. The documented history of this specimen is significant and should be recorded:
- Original collection: **Johannes le Francq van Berkhey** (1729–1812), Dutch naturalist
- Illustrated in Houttuyn's *Natuurlyke Historie* (published 1761–1785) — an engraving was made of this specimen
- Acquired for Teylers Museum by director **Martinus van Marum**, following the forced sale of van Berkhey's collection after 1795
- Now on permanent display in the Oval Hall

This provenance chain, spanning from at least the 1760s–1780s (when Houttuyn illustrated it) through to the present, is a significant part of the specimen's cultural and scientific value and is entirely absent from the catalog record.

### 3. DESCRIPTION IS ENTIRELY MISSING

No description is provided. The following information should appear in the catalog:
- Mineral species: malachite, copper carbonate hydroxide
- Chemical formula: Cu₂(CO₃)(OH)₂
- Crystal system: monoclinic
- Hardness: 3.5–4 (Mohs)
- Habit: botryoidal/stalactitic with characteristic concentric banding
- Colour: vivid green with lighter and darker green banding
- Physical description of this specimen: polished upper surface showing banding, matrix/host rock
- Locality: Ural Mountains, Russia (18th-century provenance, probable Gumeshki or Nizhny Tagil mine)
- Geological context: secondary copper mineral formed by weathering of copper sulphide ore deposits

### 4. "PRODUCTION PERIOD: 18th CENTURY" IS MISLEADING

The "production period" of a mineral specimen is not the 18th century — malachite forms over geological time scales. The 18th century refers to the **period of collection/acquisition**, not the formation of the mineral. The Ural malachite deposits are geologically ancient (formed during Hercynian-age copper mineralisation). Using "production period" for the collection date conflates two entirely different time references. This field should either be relabeled as "acquisition/collection period" or the 18th century should be clarified as the date of extraction/collection.

### 5. RELATED DOCUMENTATION NOT FULLY IDENTIFIED

The catalog lists the related documentation as "Natuurlijke historie of uitvoerige beschrijving der dieren, planten en mineraalen, volgens het Samenstel van den Heer Linnaeus" — this is Houttuyn's encyclopedic natural history work. However, the catalog does not specify:
- The author (Martinus Houttuyn, 1720–1798)
- The publication date range (37 volumes, 1761–1785)
- The specific volume and plate number where this malachite specimen is illustrated
- That an engraving of this specific specimen exists in the work

This cross-reference is highly significant for provenance purposes and should be fully cited with volume and plate number.

### 6. MINERAL DATA ABSENT

Standard mineralogical catalog data is absent:
- Chemical formula (Cu₂(CO₃)(OH)₂)
- Crystal system (monoclinic)
- Strunz classification (5.BA.10 — Carbonates with additional anions)
- Hardness (3.5–4 Mohs)
- Specific gravity (approximately 3.6–4.0)
- Streak (light green)

These are standard fields for a mineral specimen catalog and are all missing.

### 7. ONLY WEIGHT IS GIVEN AS A DIMENSION

The only dimensional data is weight (409.6 g). No length, width, or height measurements are given. For a specimen on public display in the Oval Hall, basic physical dimensions should be recorded.

### 8. RELATED OBJECT: LABEL NOT DESCRIBED

The catalog notes a related object "Etiket met opschrift" (Label with inscription) but provides no information about what the label says. Original 18th-century mineral labels are primary sources for provenance and should be described or transcribed. The mineralogy.eu article on Teylers mineral labels confirms that the museum holds original handwritten labels from van Marum and Le Francq van Berkhey's collections.
