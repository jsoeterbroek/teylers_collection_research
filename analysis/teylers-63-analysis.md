## Object Description

This is a bilingual Greek-and-Latin edition of two ancient philosophical texts on fate, determinism, and free will, printed by Thomas Roycroft in London in 1658. It combines Alexander of Aphrodisias's *De Fato* (On Fate, addressed to the emperors Septimius Severus and Caracalla, c. 198–209 AD) with Ammonius Hermiae's commentary on the second section of Aristotle's *De Interpretatione* (c. 5th century AD). The volume is in duodecimo format with a parchment binding.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. ALEXANDER OF APHRODISIAS: MAJOR PHILOSOPHER, NOT JUST "WRITER"

The catalog classifies this under "Griekse en Latijnse schrijvers" (Greek and Latin writers). Alexander of Aphrodisias (fl. c. 200 AD) was far more significant: he was **the most celebrated ancient commentator on Aristotle**, known simply as "the Commentator" (ho exēgētēs) throughout late antiquity and the Arabic philosophical tradition. He held an imperially endowed chair of Peripatetic philosophy in Athens. His *De Fato* is one of the most important surviving ancient works on determinism and free will — some scholars consider him the first philosopher to articulate something resembling the modern concept of free will. The subject classification is reductive.

### 2. "AD IMPERATORES" CONTEXT NOT EXPLAINED

The "Ad Imperatores" (To the Emperors) in the title refers to **Septimius Severus and Caracalla**, who co-ruled 198–209 AD and appointed Alexander to his philosophical chair. The dedication was a political-philosophical act: arguing against Stoic fatalism before rulers who had the power to favor or suppress philosophical schools. This context is absent from the catalog.

### 3. AMMONIUS HERMIAE NOT LISTED AS AUTHOR

The catalog lists only Alexander Aphrodisiensis as author, but the volume also contains a substantial work by **Ammonius Hermiae** (c. 440–520 AD), a Neoplatonist philosopher from Alexandria. His commentary on Aristotle's *De Interpretatione* chapter 9 — the famous "sea battle" argument — is a seminal treatment of future contingents. The two texts are thematically paired: both address fate, necessity, and human freedom from different philosophical traditions (Peripatetic vs. Neoplatonist). Ammonius should be listed as a second author.

### 4. THOMAS ROYCROFT: SIGNIFICANT PRINTER

Thomas Roycroft (c. 1616/17–1677) was one of the most important London printers of the 17th century:
- He printed **Brian Walton's London Polyglot Bible** (1654–1657) — a monumental six-volume work in nine languages, considered the typographical achievement of the century
- Appointed **"King's Printer of Oriental Languages"** by Charles II as reward
- This 1658 edition, with parallel Greek and Latin text, was printed immediately after the Polyglot Bible project, using the multilingual typographic capacity Roycroft had developed

This printer's significance is not mentioned in the catalog.

### 5. THEMATIC COHERENCE OF THE VOLUME

The catalog does not explain why two apparently separate texts appear in one volume. They are deliberately paired around the philosophical problem of **determinism vs. free will**:
- Alexander argues against Stoic universal necessity, defending human agency
- Ammonius addresses whether statements about future events make those events necessary (the "sea battle" problem), arguing divine foreknowledge does not abolish contingency

### 6. DIGITIZED COPIES EXIST ELSEWHERE

The catalog notes "not digitized due to parchment binding." While this is understandable for conservation reasons, digitized copies of this same edition exist on **Google Books** (IDs: 2Uw-AAAAcAAJ, K6VbAAAAcAAJ) and in the **Austrian National Library** (via Europeana, ref. ABO 2BZ186216508). Cross-referencing to these digital surrogates would benefit researchers.

### 7. ARISTOTLE'S ROLE UNDERSPECIFIED

The catalog lists "Aristoteles" as a related person but does not specify his role. Aristotle is the subject of Ammonius's commentary, not an author of this volume. The relationship should be clarified.
