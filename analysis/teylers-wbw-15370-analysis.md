## Object Description

One of the most celebrated botanical publications of the nineteenth century. *Jardin de la Malmaison* documents the exotic plant collection assembled by **Empress Joséphine de Beauharnais** at Château de Malmaison, west of Paris. Text by **Étienne Pierre Ventenat** (1757–1808), French botanist and former director of the Sainte-Geneviève library, engaged directly by Joséphine. Illustrated by **Pierre-Joseph Redouté** (1759–1840), the pre-eminent botanical illustrator of the era, later appointed official flower painter to the Empress.

Published in Paris by Imprimerie de Crapelet, issued in **20 fascicles of 6 plates each**, April 1803 – November 1805. Title pages carry Republican calendar dates An XI–XII (1803–1804), but the complete work extended to November 1805. Large folio (c.520 × 338 mm), two volumes bound as one. Contains **120 color-printed stipple-engraved plates** after Redouté watercolors, executed *à la poupée* (colors applied directly to the copperplate) and finished by hand by ~15 engravers. Only approximately **200 copies** were printed, most distributed as imperial presentation gifts to European monarchs and dignitaries. Ventenat described species new to science; the Malmaison gardens contained ~200 plant species flowering in France for the first time, including 46 Australian species.

## Key Findings

### 1. ILLUSTRATION TECHNIQUE MISIDENTIFIED AS LITHOGRAPHY

The catalog notes "6 pl. **lith.** et col." — describing the plates as lithographs. This is factually wrong. The plates are **stipple engravings** (intaglio/taille-douce), printed from copperplates engraved with dots (*à la poupée*), then finished by hand. Lithography was a distinct process, invented by Alois Senefelder in 1796 and not adopted by Redouté for this work. Every authoritative source (Sotheby's, Arader Galleries, the Hunt Institute, Biodiversity Heritage Library, academic literature) consistently describes the plates as stipple engravings. The "lith." abbreviation is an outright factual error that fundamentally misrepresents the medium.

### 2. PLATE COUNT OF 6 IS CATASTROPHICALLY WRONG — COMPLETE WORK HAS 120

The catalog records "6 pl. … avec une feuille texte." This matches the format of a **single fascicle** — one of 20 fascicles, each containing 6 plates and one text leaf. The description of the full work (implied by "2 tom. dans 1 vol.") requires **120 plates**. Either: (a) the Teylers copy is incomplete (only 1 of 20 fascicles), a fact that is not disclosed; or (b) the description was copied from a fascicle wrapper rather than the full bound work. Either way, the record is seriously misleading — it describes 5% of the complete work's plates.

### 3. PUBLICATION DATE TRUNCATED — WORK COMPLETE IN 1805, NOT 1804

The catalog records "1803-1804" (and the note repeats "An XI-XII; (1803-1804)"). While the title pages carry dates corresponding to An XI–XII (Gregorian: 1803–1804), actual publication of all 20 fascicles ran from April 1803 to **November 1805**. The authoritative Sotheby's lot entry catalogs this as "An XI–1803–[1805]." The terminal date 1804 is incorrect by a year for the complete publication.

### 4. Empress Joséphine absent as patron and dedicatee

The work was commissioned and financed by **Empress Joséphine de Beauharnais** (1763–1814), wife of Napoleon Bonaparte. The dedication leaf addresses her directly. The catalog contains no mention of the patron, dedicatee, or imperial context — information essential to understanding the object's provenance and historical positioning.

### 5. Language field absent

The work is written entirely in French. The language field is missing from the record.

### 6. Edition and rarity not stated

This is the first and only edition, with only approximately **200 copies** printed — most distributed as imperial presentation gifts. The record contains no edition statement and no note on rarity, standard requirements for a rare book of this importance.

### 7. Named engravers absent

The plates were executed by ~15 professional engravers (including J.B. Dien, P.F. Legrand, L.J. Allais). None are named in the record.

### 8. No subject headings or botanical context

No subject terms recorded. The work documents ~200 plant species new to France, 46 Australian species, and species described for the first time by Ventenat — information entirely absent from the record.
