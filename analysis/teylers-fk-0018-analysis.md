## Object Description

This is an 18th-century physics demonstration apparatus for teaching pulley mechanics, made by the Amsterdam instrument makers **J. Kampman and J.C. Pelletier**. It consists of a mahogany stand with two brass pulleys, used to demonstrate mechanical advantage — the principle that a small force applied over a greater distance can move a larger weight. Such apparatus was standard equipment in 18th-century physics cabinets (*fysische kabinetten*) and derives from designs published by **Willem Jacob 's Gravesande** in his influential textbook *Physices Elementa Mathematica* (1720). The instrument belongs to Teylers' **Fysisch Kabinet**, one of the oldest institutional physics collections in the Netherlands, assembled from the 1780s onward by director **Martinus van Marum**.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. KAMPMAN WORKSHOP: A SUCCESSION OF PARTNERSHIPS

The catalog lists makers "J. Kampman, J.C. Pelletier" without context. Research reveals that J. Kampman ran an Amsterdam instrument-making workshop across two distinct partnerships spanning approximately 50 years:

- **Kampman & Adam Steitz** (active 1750–1774): Made FK 0027 (inclined plane) and other instruments
- **Kampman & J.C. Pelletier** (active 1775–1799): Made FK 0018 (this pulley apparatus)

The transition around 1775 suggests Steitz retired or died and Pelletier succeeded him. Adam Steitz is independently documented as an accomplished maker who built a Gregorian reflector telescope (~1750) for Amsterdam merchant Jacobus van der Wall, notable enough to be photographed in 1961 at Leiden Observatory with astronomers Jan Oort and Henk van de Hulst. J.C. Pelletier (French name, suggesting possible Huguenot background) is listed in the DWC/KNAW database of Dutch instrument makers but remains otherwise poorly documented.

All three are recorded in **Maria Rooseboom's** *Bijdrage tot de Geschiedenis der Instrumentmakerskunst in de Noordelijke Nederlanden* (1950), the foundational reference on Dutch instrument makers.

### 2. PART OF A SUITE OF 'S GRAVESANDE APPARATUS

The catalog treats this instrument in isolation. In fact, the Kampman workshop produced an entire coordinated suite of demonstration apparatus for the same collection, all based on 's Gravesande designs:

| Object | Instrument | Makers | Date |
|--------|-----------|--------|------|
| FK 0014 | Projectile trolley | Kampman & Steitz | 1750–1774 |
| FK 0027 | Inclined plane | Kampman & Steitz | 1750–1774 |
| FK 0073 | Cycloidal track | Kampman (& Steitz?) | 1750–1774 |
| **FK 0018** | **Pulley stand** | **Kampman & Pelletier** | **1775–1799** |
| FK 0053 (area) | Centrifugal machine | Adam Steitz | c. 1750–1774 |

This suite represents a systematic acquisition of Newtonian mechanics demonstration equipment. The catalog should cross-reference these instruments.

### 3. 'S GRAVESANDE'S SIGNIFICANCE UNEXPLAINED

The catalog title says "naar Willem Jacob 's Gravesande" (FK 0027) but never explains who he was. **Willem Jacob 's Gravesande** (1688–1742) was:

- **Professor of Mathematics and Astronomy at Leiden University** from 1717
- Pioneer of **demonstration-based physics teaching** — he was the first on the continent to teach Newtonian physics through systematic experiments
- Author of *Physices Elementa Mathematica, Experimentis Confirmata* (1720), "easily the most influential book of its kind, at least before 1750"
- The 1742 third edition contained **127 folded copper plates** illustrating apparatus — these plates served as blueprints for instrument makers across Europe
- He worked closely with instrument maker **Jan van Musschenbroek** (1687–1748) to design and build the apparatus depicted in successive editions

The pulley apparatus demonstrates the "simple machines" — one of the core topics in 's Gravesande's Volume I on mechanics. His treatment covers the lever, pulley, wheel and axle, inclined plane, and screw/wedge.

### 4. PHYSICAL PRINCIPLES NOT DESCRIBED

The catalog says only "demonstratie van de werking van de katrol" (demonstrating the workings of the pulley). An 18th-century pulley stand demonstrates:

- **Mechanical advantage**: A single movable pulley halves the required force
- **The "golden rule of mechanics"**: What is gained in force is lost in distance
- **Equilibrium conditions**: Balancing forces through compound pulley arrangements
- **Block and tackle** systems showing increasing mechanical advantage with more pulleys

These are fundamental principles of Newtonian mechanics (Newton's *Principia*, 1687) that 's Gravesande made accessible through demonstration.

### 5. MUSEUM BOERHAAVE HOLDS THE ORIGINALS

The Teylers instruments are Amsterdam-made copies of designs originating in Leiden. **Museum Boerhaave** (Rijksmuseum Boerhaave) holds the original 's Gravesande–Musschenbroek collection, which includes "balances, levers, and tackles [pulley systems] that demonstrate laws of statics." These originals were:

- Built by **Jan van Musschenbroek** in close collaboration with 's Gravesande
- Purchased by Leiden's governors in 1742, making it "the largest collection of instruments in the world"
- Cataloged by **C.A. Crommelin** (1926 and 1951) and **Peter de Clercq** (1997, *The Leiden Cabinet of Physics*)
- Formally transferred from Leiden University to Rijksmuseum Boerhaave in February 2025

The relationship between the Teylers copies and the Leiden originals — illustrating the dissemination of Newtonian experimental physics across the Netherlands — is historically significant and unmentioned.

### 6. DATING CAN LIKELY BE NARROWED

The dating 1775–1799 is based on the Kampman & Pelletier partnership period. However, **Martinus van Marum** became director of Teylers in **1784** and was the principal acquirer of physics instruments. If FK 0018 was a Van Marum acquisition (likely, given it belongs to the Fysisch Kabinet he established), the date can be narrowed to **c. 1784–1799**. Van Marum is known to have acquired instruments from "a series of private collection sales that occurred in Holland in the late 1780s."

### 7. NO DIMENSIONS RECORDED

The catalog provides no dimensions for this instrument. Physical measurements would aid comparison with comparable apparatus at Museum Boerhaave and other collections.

### 8. REFERENCE CATALOG NOT CITED

The definitive reference for Teylers' instrument collection is **G. L'E. Turner and T.H. Levere, *Martinus van Marum: Life and Work, Volume IV: Van Marum's Scientific Instruments in Teyler's Museum*** (Leiden: Noordhoff International, 1973), 401 pp. This published catalog should be cross-referenced.
