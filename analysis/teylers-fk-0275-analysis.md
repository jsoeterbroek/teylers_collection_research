## Object Description

A **phonautograph** (*fonautograaf*) — the **world's first practical device to record sound as a visible waveform**, predating Edison's phonograph by 20 years. Invented by French typographer **Édouard-Léon Scott de Martinville** (25 April 1817 – 26 April 1879), patented in France on **25 March 1857** (French patent no. 17,897/31,470), and manufactured by Prussian-born Paris instrument maker **Karl Rudolph Koenig** (1832–1901). Koenig began manufacturing phonautographs from 1859; the Smithsonian acquired a comparable example from Koenig in 1866 for 500 francs.

**Operating principle:** Sound enters a large conical horn (white-enameled interior, brass/zinc body), causing a taut membrane at the narrow end to vibrate. A boar's bristle attached to the membrane traces a wavy line — a *phonautogram* — in lampblack soot on a rotating cylinder driven by a hand crank. The device **cannot play back sound**; it produces only a visual record of sound waves. This is the critical functional distinction from Edison's phonograph (1877).

**Physical appearance:** Large rectangular wooden base (840 × 730 mm). Dominant feature: large conical horn with white-enameled interior, supported by ornate cast-iron brackets. Hand crank drives the horizontal recording cylinder via a pulley wheel. Cast-iron A-frame support. Fine-grained hardwood base.

Teylers acquired this instrument in **1865**, the same year as its listed date.

## Key Findings

### 1. PRODUCTION DATE CONFLATES ACQUISITION YEAR WITH MANUFACTURE DATE

The catalog records "1865 (exact)" as production date, then adds: *"1865 is jaar van aanschaf (collectie Teylers)"* (1865 is the year of acquisition). This is circular and contradictory: the date is simultaneously the stated manufacture year and the admitted acquisition year. Koenig manufactured phonautographs continuously from **1859 onward**. There is no independent evidence this particular instrument was made in 1865 rather than any year between 1859 and 1865. The catalog used the acquisition year as a proxy for production year without verification and the date note acknowledges this without resolving it. Manufacture date and acquisition date must be recorded separately.

### 2. MATERIALS LIST IS CRITICALLY INCOMPLETE — HORN MATERIAL ABSENT

The catalog lists only "hout" (wood) and "ijzer" (iron). Visual analysis reveals the dominant component — the large conical horn — has a **white-enameled interior**, almost certainly zinc or tin-plate, consistent with Koenig's early-1860s redesign which used a "paraboloid sound collector made of zinc." The horn is the most visually prominent part of the instrument and its material is entirely missing. Brass, steel, the lampblack-coated recording paper, and the membrane/bristle assembly are also unrecorded.

### 3. NO DESCRIPTION — CATALOG CONTAINS ZERO EXPLANATORY TEXT

No description field. The record provides no information that this is the world's first sound-recording device, that it records sound visually but cannot play it back, what scientific principle it demonstrates, or why it is historically significant. A researcher encounters only "Fonautograaf" with dimensions and two materials — nothing else.

### 4. MAKER'S FIRST NAME TRUNCATED — "R. KOENIG" INSUFFICIENT

The maker is listed as "R. Koenig, Parijs" without full given name, dates, or nationality. Full name: **Karl Rudolph Koenig** (1832–1901), Prussian-born acoustic instrument maker, Paris. The truncation "R. Koenig" is insufficient for unambiguous scholarly identification and records none of the biographical context essential for a research catalog.

### 5. INVENTOR'S GIVEN NAME TRUNCATED; PATENT DATE (1857) ENTIRELY ABSENT

The inventor is "E.L. Scott de Martinville" without full given name (Édouard-Léon), dates (1817–1879), profession (typographer/printer), or nationality (French). Most critically, the **patent date of 25 March 1857** — which precedes the catalog's listed date by eight years and established priority for the invention of sound recording — is completely absent. This makes it impossible to understand the relationship between invention, manufacture, and acquisition.

### 6. Invention patent date (1857) not recorded

The gap between invention (1857 patent), commercial production (from 1859), and acquisition (1865) is invisible in the current record.

### 7. No provenance

Likely purchased directly from Koenig's Paris workshop (as the Smithsonian did in 1866), but nothing is documented.

### 8. No bibliography

Extensive scholarly literature exists including David Pantalony's *Altered Sensations: Rudolph Koenig's Acoustical Workshop* (Springer, 2009) and the FirstSounds.org research project. None referenced.
