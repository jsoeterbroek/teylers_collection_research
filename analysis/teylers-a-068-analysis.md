## Object Description

A drawing in red chalk (with stylus underdrawing and possible graphite annotations) on paper (203 x 224 mm) by **Raphael** (Raffaello Sanzio, Urbino, 1483 – Rome, 1520), dated c. 1517–1518. The title — "Study for the half-length figures of two angels" — connects the sheet to the preparatory process for the **Transfiguration** (Pinacoteca Vaticana), Raphael's last major painting, commissioned c. 1516–1517 by Cardinal Giulio de' Medici. Part of Teylers' collection of **twelve drawings attributed to Raphael** — the largest Raphael drawings collection in the Netherlands and among the most significant in the world. Acquired in 1790 as part of the same Odescalchi purchase that brought the Michelangelo drawings to Haarlem. Designated one of Teylers' **50 Topstukken**.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. PAINTING CONNECTION NOT STATED

The catalog does not identify **which painting or commission** this is a preparatory study for. Based on the date (c. 1517–1518), the connection is to the **Transfiguration** (Pinacoteca Vaticana), Raphael's last major work, commissioned by Cardinal Giulio de' Medici (later Pope Clement VII) around 1516–1517 for Narbonne Cathedral. This is the most important contextual fact about a preparatory drawing — it is entirely absent.

### 2. "SASSOFERRATO" INSCRIPTION — FORMER ATTRIBUTION NOT EXPLAINED

The inscription "Sassoferrato" refers to **Giovanni Battista Salvi, called Sassoferrato** (1609–1685), the Italian Baroque painter. Its presence indicates a **former misattribution** of the drawing to Sassoferrato rather than to Raphael. This is entirely plausible because:

- Sassoferrato was an obsessive copyist of Raphael — producing large numbers of copies that were frequently confused with Raphael originals
- His works were "long taken to be by some follower of Raphael"
- He worked in Rome during Queen Christina's lifetime, overlapping with the period when the Odescalchi collection was assembled

The catalog transcribes the inscription but provides no explanation. The history of attribution (Raphael → Sassoferrato → back to Raphael) is significant scholarly context.

### 3. "PENCIL" MEDIUM — POTENTIALLY ANACHRONISTIC OR MISLEADING

The catalog states "stylus, red chalk, pencil on paper." In the context of a c. 1517–1518 drawing, "pencil" (Dutch: *potlood*) is potentially misleading. True graphite pencils were not in common use in the early 16th century. The term may refer to:

- A lead or metalpoint stylus (used for underdrawing)
- Later annotations added to the sheet by a different hand
- A translation artifact from Dutch catalog terminology

If the "pencil" refers to actual graphite, this should be noted as a later addition and not attributed to Raphael's hand.

### 4. "A 95?" — OLD INVENTORY NUMBER NOT EXPLAINED

The inscription "A 95?" most likely represents an **old inventory number** from a previous cataloguing system — either from the Odescalchi collection or an early Teylers inventory. The discrepancy between "A 95" and the current number A 068 suggests either a superseded early Teylers inventory or a numbering from Rome before the 1790 sale. The question mark indicates partial illegibility. This should be explained rather than left as a bare transcription.

### 5. PROVENANCE CHAIN INCOMPLETE — QUEEN CHRISTINA ABSENT

As with the Michelangelo drawings (same 1790 acquisition), the catalog states "purchased from Odescalchi heirs, Rome, 1790" but omits the full provenance chain:

1. Raphael's workshop/estate (c. 1520)
2. Various Roman collectors (16th–17th century)
3. **Queen Christina of Sweden** (Rome, 1626–1689)
4. Cardinal Decio Azzolino (bequeathed 1689)
5. Marchese Pompeo Azzolino → Principe Livio Odescalchi (c. 1692–1696)
6. Odescalchi heirs
7. Purchased by **Willem Anne Lestevenon** for Teylers, 1790

### 6. TEYLERS' TWELVE RAPHAEL DRAWINGS NOT CONTEXTUALISED

The catalog does not indicate that Teylers holds **twelve drawings attributed to Raphael** — the largest Raphael collection in the Netherlands. In 2012, three drawings previously attributed to Raphael's workshop were upgraded to autograph Raphael by curators Michiel Plomp (Teylers) and Achim Gnann (Albertina), published in the exhibition catalogue *Raphael and His School* (W Books/Waanders, 2012).

### 7. TITLE MORE DESCRIPTIVE IN ENGLISH SCHOLARSHIP

The Dutch title "Studie voor twee engelfiguren" translates as "Study for two angel figures." English-language scholarship refers to it more precisely as "Study for the half-length figures of two angels" — which better describes what the drawing actually shows (the angels are depicted from the waist up, not full-length).

### 8. KEY LITERATURE NOT CITED

The catalog cites no scholarly references. For a Raphael drawing of this importance, the following should be cited:

- **Joannides, Paul**: *The Drawings of Raphael* (1983) — the conservative catalogue raisonne
- **Knab, Mitsch & Oberhuber**: *Raphael: Die Zeichnungen* (1983)
- **Van Tuyll van Serooskerken, Carel**: *The Italian Drawings of the Fifteenth and Sixteenth Centuries in the Teyler Museum* (2000) — definitive catalogue of the Teylers collection
- **Gnann & Plomp**: *Raphael and His School* (W Books/Waanders, 2012)
