## Object Description

This is a set of five graduated block-and-tackle configurations mounted on a frame, demonstrating mechanical advantage from 1:1 through 5:1. Each tackle adds one more supporting rope segment and one more pulley, progressively showing the **"golden rule of mechanics"**: what is gained in force is lost in distance. The apparatus belongs to a **three-part pedagogical series** of pulley demonstrations in the Teylers Fysisch Kabinet (FK 0018 → FK 0020 → FK 0022), progressing from individual pulleys to assembled blocks to complete graduated tackle systems. All three date to 1775–1799 and derive from designs by **Willem Jacob 's Gravesande** (1688–1742).

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. NO MAKER LISTED — PROBABLE ATTRIBUTION TO KAMPMAN & PELLETIER

The catalog provides **no maker** for this instrument. Given the identical date range (1775–1799), identical materials (mahogany and brass), identical collection (Mechanics), and the fact that the two companion pulley instruments FK 0018 and FK 0020 are both attributed to **J. Kampman and J.C. Pelletier, Amsterdam**, this instrument almost certainly comes from the same workshop. The attribution gap should be addressed.

### 2. CAPSTONE OF A THREE-PART PEDAGOGICAL SERIES

The catalog treats FK 0022 in isolation. It is the culminating piece of a clear pedagogical progression:

| Object | Title | Dutch Term | Function | Level |
|--------|-------|-----------|----------|-------|
| FK 0018 | Statief met twee poelies | **poelie** (pulley wheel) | Stand with 2 individual pulleys | Elementary |
| FK 0020 | Katrollen | **katrol** (pulley block) | Set of pulley blocks with cord | Intermediate |
| **FK 0022** | **Vijf takels** | **takel** (tackle system) | **Five graduated tackle configurations on frame** | **Advanced** |

The Dutch terminology itself encodes the progression: poelie (wheel) → katrol (block) → takel (complete system). This series demonstrates the full curriculum of pulley mechanics in 's Gravesande's *Physices Elementa Mathematica* (1720), from the individual component to the complete system.

### 3. THE FIVE TACKLES: MA 1 THROUGH 5

The number five represents five levels of integer mechanical advantage:

| # | Configuration | Mechanical Advantage | Principle |
|---|--------------|---------------------|-----------|
| 1 | Single fixed pulley | 1:1 | Redirects force only |
| 2 | Single movable pulley | 2:1 | Halves effort required |
| 3 | Gun tackle (3 parts) | 3:1 | Three supporting rope segments |
| 4 | Double tackle (4 parts) | 4:1 | Four supporting rope segments |
| 5 | Compound tackle (5 parts) | 5:1 | Five supporting rope segments |

Displayed side by side on a frame ("[raam]" in the catalog description), the five tackles provide a **complete visual proof** of the golden rule: as mechanical advantage increases from 1 to 5, the rope pull distance increases proportionally. Five is a practical pedagogical limit — beyond 5:1, friction losses in 18th-century brass pulleys would erode the theoretical advantage, making the demonstration less clean.

### 4. "[RAAM]" NOTATION UNEXPLAINED

The description includes "[raam]" (frame) in square brackets, suggesting the five tackles are mounted on or displayed in a wooden frame. This is consistent with comparable apparatus: Benjamin Martin shipped a "Compleat Sett of Mechanicall Pullies with a Large square Frame in Mahogany, & brass hooks for hanging the Pullies on" to Harvard College in 1765. The square brackets suggest the notation may be an editorial addition, not part of the original description.

### 6. PHYSICAL PRINCIPLES NOT DESCRIBED

The catalog says only "demonstratie van de werking van takels." The apparatus demonstrates:

- **Progressive mechanical advantage** (MA 1:1 through 5:1)
- **The golden rule of mechanics**: force gained = distance lost
- **Conservation of work**: work input ≈ work output (minus friction)
- **Practical applications**: cranes, ship rigging, building construction — directly relevant to 18th-century Dutch maritime and engineering technology

### 7. NO DIMENSIONS RECORDED

As with FK 0018 and FK 0020, no physical dimensions are provided.

### 8. HARVARD PARALLEL

The closest documented parallel is Harvard's **"Compleat Sett of Mechanicall Pullies"** (1765, by Benjamin Martin, London), with mahogany frame and brass fittings — the same concept, materials, and period. This comparison is absent from the catalog.
