## Object Description

A set of **distillation vessels** (*destilleerketels*) made of **Wedgwood Queen's Ware** (cream-coloured earthenware / creamware), used in the chemistry laboratory of **Martinus van Marum** (1750–1837), founding director of Teylers Museum. Van Marum established the **first dedicated chemistry laboratory in the Netherlands** in 1790 in a building adjacent to the museum, using it to demonstrate and extend Lavoisier's oxygen-based chemistry to Dutch audiences.

Queen's Ware is the refined creamware body perfected by **Josiah Wedgwood I** (1730–1795) from c.1765, named after Queen Charlotte (who granted permission to use the name from 1767). Wedgwood was a well-documented supplier of laboratory apparatus — retorts, crucibles, evaporating dishes — to leading 18th-century chemists including Priestley and Lavoisier. These vessels were almost certainly acquired for van Marum's 1790 laboratory programme.

## Key Findings

### 1. MATERIAL FIELD CONTRADICTS THE TITLE — DROPS "QUEEN'S WARE" SPECIFICATION

The title specifies "Wedgwood Queensware aardewerk" but the material field records only the generic "aardewerk" (earthenware). Queen's Ware is a **specific, historically named type** of creamware with a refined body (including Cornish clay) and characteristic cream-coloured lead glaze — not simply generic earthenware. The material field directly contradicts the more specific identification already claimed in the title. Queen's Ware was chosen for laboratory use for its specific properties (acid resistance, non-porous glaze, heat tolerance) superior to ordinary earthenware for distillation.

### 2. NO DESCRIPTION — NUMBER OF VESSELS, FORM, AND MARKS UNKNOWN

No descriptive text whatsoever. The title uses the plural (*destilleerketels*) but the record gives no information on: how many vessels are in the set, the shape and form of each (alembic, cucurbit, receiver, condenser?), individual sizes, Wedgwood marks or stamps, physical condition, or how the set functioned as a complete distillation apparatus. For a multi-part set this is a critical omission.

### 3. NO DIMENSIONS RECORDED

No dimensions for any vessel in the set. Without dimensions the record cannot support comparative study, condition monitoring, or display/storage planning.

### 4. NO PROVENANCE RECORDED

No acquisition history. Key unanswered questions: purchased directly from Wedgwood by van Marum? Ordered via agent? Mentioned in van Marum's extensive notebooks or correspondence? The definitive scholarly source — Turner & Levere, *Martinus van Marum: Life and Work* Vol. IV (1967) — should be consulted but is not cited.

### 5. NO BIBLIOGRAPHY — KEY SCHOLARLY LITERATURE ABSENT

No references recorded. The definitive catalog of van Marum's instruments is: G. L'E. Turner & T. H. Levere, *Martinus van Marum: Life and Work, Vol. IV — Van Marum's Scientific Instruments in Teyler's Museum* (Haarlem, 1967). Additional relevant literature includes Levere's work on van Marum's chemistry programme and the Science History Institute's documentation of Wedgwood's laboratory apparatus supply network. None cited.

### 6. Maker "J. Wedgwood" — ambiguous between Josiah I and II

"J. Wedgwood" without full given name or dates is ambiguous. In 1790, **Josiah Wedgwood I** (1730–1795) was still alive and directing the firm; attribution to him is almost certainly correct but the record should specify "Josiah Wedgwood I (1730–1795)" to eliminate ambiguity with his son Josiah Wedgwood II (1769–1843), who ran the firm after 1795.

### 7. Production place "Stoke-on-Trent" is anachronistic

Stoke-on-Trent did not exist as an administrative unit until **1910**. In 1790, the correct location for Wedgwood's works was **Etruria Works, near Burslem, Staffordshire** (opened 1769). The catalog's place name is a modern retroactive label inapplicable in 1790.

### 8. Production date "1790" likely conflates inventory date with manufacture date

The "1790" in the catalog number FK **1790**.01 almost certainly reflects the year these items were **registered in the Fysisch Kabinet inventory** when van Marum set up the chemistry laboratory. Wedgwood had been supplying laboratory apparatus from at least 1772. The catalog presents the inventory year as the production year without independent verification.

### 9. Van Marum described only as "gebruiker" (user)

Van Marum was far more than a user: he was the founding director of Teylers Museum, the founder of the Fysisch Kabinet, the commissioner and architect of the 1790 chemistry laboratory, and the person who introduced Lavoisier's chemistry to the Netherlands. "gebruiker" drastically understates his relationship to this object.
