## Object Description

This is a Klankanalysator (Sound Analyzer), after Rudolph Koenig — the world's first acoustic spectrum analyzer. The instrument uses a bank of Helmholtz resonators connected to manometric flame capsules and a rotating mirror to make the harmonic components of complex sounds visible. When a sound (voice, musical instrument, etc.) is directed at the resonators, each one tuned to a different frequency vibrates in sympathy with its corresponding partial tone. These vibrations modulate gas flames via thin membranes, and the resulting flame fluctuations — invisible to the naked eye due to their speed — become visible as saw-toothed patterns of light when observed in a rotating four-sided mirror. The instrument is, in essence, the 19th-century equivalent of an oscilloscope or modern spectrum analyzer.

## Key Findings

### 1. RUDOLPH KOENIG (1832–1901): FULL BIOGRAPHY

Karl Rudolph Koenig was born on 26 November 1832 in Königsberg, East Prussia (now Kaliningrad, Russia). His father, Johann Friedrich Koenig, was a professor of mathematics and physics who had studied under the astronomer Friedrich Bessel. This academic background shaped his son's lifelong dedication to precision and scientific rigor.

**Training under Vuillaume (1851–1858):** In 1851, at age 19, Koenig moved to Paris and took a position as an apprentice in the workshop of Jean-Baptiste Vuillaume (1798–1875), the most celebrated violin maker of the 19th century. This seven-year apprenticeship gave Koenig exceptional skills in woodwork, metalwork, and — crucially — an intimate understanding of the acoustics of stringed instruments. The precision and aesthetic refinement that characterized all his later scientific instruments can be traced directly to this training.

**Establishing his atelier (1858):** In 1858, Koenig opened his own workshop at 26 (later 30) Rue Hautefeuille on the Left Bank in Paris, in the heart of the Latin Quarter near the Sorbonne. This atelier was simultaneously a workshop, laboratory, showroom, and meeting place for scientists, musicians, artisans, and teachers. It remained his base for over forty years until his death.

**Relationship with Helmholtz:** Around 1860, Koenig began corresponding with Hermann von Helmholtz (1821–1894), the towering German physicist and physiologist who had published *Die Lehre von den Tonempfindungen als physiologische Grundlage für die Theorie der Musik* (On the Sensations of Tone as a Physiological Basis for the Theory of Music) in 1863. This work provided the theoretical foundation for acoustic science — particularly the theory that complex sounds consist of a Fourier series of pure tones (harmonics). After working alongside Helmholtz, Koenig became the principal maker and seller of Helmholtz's instruments, including the famous Helmholtz resonators. Koenig did not merely copy Helmholtz's designs; he improved, extended, and refined them with an instrument maker's practical genius.

**The Helmholtz–Koenig dispute:** Their relationship eventually soured over a scientific disagreement about the nature of *combination tones* (Tartini tones). Helmholtz argued these were subjective phenomena occurring in the ear. Koenig, based on his meticulous experiments, argued they were objective physical phenomena present in the air. This dispute had geopolitical dimensions: German scientists supported Helmholtz, while English and American scientists sided with Koenig. After the Franco-Prussian War of 1870–1871 and the ongoing scientific disputes, Koenig became increasingly isolated from the German scientific establishment, though he remained highly regarded internationally.

**International recognition:** At the London International Exposition of 1862, Koenig displayed his manometric flame apparatus (invented that year) and won a gold medal. At the 1876 Philadelphia Centennial Exhibition, he displayed a vast collection of apparatus — the centerpiece being his Grand Tonomètre of over 670 tuning forks — and again won a gold medal. The exhibition jury stated: "There is no other [exhibit] in the present International Exhibition which surpasses it in scientific interest."

**Publications and catalogs:** Koenig published five catalogs of his instruments: in 1859, 1865, 1873, 1882, and 1889. The final 1889 *Catalogue des appareils d'acoustique* ran to one hundred pages with descriptions of 272 instruments in French, English, and German.

**Death and legacy:** Koenig died on 2 October 1901 in Paris. After his death, portions of his workshop equipment went to the Conservatoire national des arts et métiers in Paris. His instruments survive in major collections worldwide: the Smithsonian Institution, Harvard University, University of Toronto, Teylers Museum, University of Coimbra, University of Rome, Musées de la civilisation (Quebec), and many others. He is the subject of a major scholarly biography: David Pantalony, *Altered Sensations: Rudolph Koenig's Acoustical Workshop in Nineteenth-Century Paris* (Springer, 2009), which includes a comprehensive catalogue raisonné of his entire instrument line.

### 2. THE KLANKANALYSATOR: WHAT IT IS AND HOW IT WORKS

The Klankanalysator (Dutch for "sound analyzer") is Koenig's *Appareil pour l'analyse du timbre des sons à flammes manométriques* — the apparatus for analyzing the timbre of sounds using manometric flames. It is recognized as the **world's first spectrum analyzer**.

**Principle of operation:** The instrument is a physical realization of Helmholtz's theory that every complex sound (voice, musical instrument, etc.) can be decomposed into a series of pure tones (the fundamental and its harmonics). Each resonator in the instrument is tuned to respond to a specific frequency. When that frequency is present in the sound being analyzed, the resonator vibrates in sympathy (resonance). This vibration is transmitted through a rubber tube to a manometric capsule — a small chamber divided by a thin elastic membrane. On one side is the acoustic vibration; on the other, a flow of illuminating gas. The membrane modulates the gas flow, causing the flame at the gas burner to fluctuate at the frequency of the resonating tone. Observed in the rotating four-sided mirror (acting as a stroboscope), each active flame appears as a saw-toothed band of light, while inactive flames appear as smooth luminous stripes. By observing which flames are "toothed," the experimenter can see which harmonic components are present in the sound.

**Two versions existed:**
- **No. 242a (c. 1865, the original model):** Eight fixed resonators tuned to the fundamental UT2 (128 Hz) and its first seven harmonics, covering UT2 to UT5 (128–1024 Hz). This was a demonstration instrument showing the concept of timbre analysis in a limited range.
- **No. 242 (c. 1865–1872, the improved model):** Fourteen *universal* (adjustable-volume) resonators in a cast-iron frame, connected to eight manometric capsules. The adjustable resonators could be set to any frequency in a range of 65 notes from Sol1 to Mi5 (approximately 96–1,280 Hz, or in some sources 194–1,300 Hz). This was the research-grade instrument developed by Koenig specifically for his vowel studies.

The Teylers instrument, dated c. 1872, corresponds to the period when Koenig was developing and producing the improved model (No. 242). Its height of 895 mm is consistent with the dimensions of known examples: the Toronto specimen is 920 mm tall, and the ENS Éditions source describes a frame 1,150 mm in height with a rotating mirror of 800 mm. The Dartmouth Hood Museum example is 880 mm (34 5/8 inches).

### 3. KOENIG'S CATALOG AND PRICES

In Koenig's 1889 *Catalogue des appareils d'acoustique*, the sound analyzer appears as:

- **No. 242:** Improved sound analyzer with universal (adjustable) resonators — **650 francs** (approximately €2,200 in modern purchasing power)
- **No. 242a:** Original sound analyzer with eight fixed resonators — price lower (exact figure not confirmed in available sources)

The 650 francs price places the sound analyzer among Koenig's more expensive instruments. For comparison, individual Helmholtz resonators (Nos. 53–55) cost 190–380 francs for a set.

The 1889 catalog was the final and most comprehensive edition, but earlier catalogs (1873, 1882) also listed versions of the analyzer. The page reference for No. 242 in the 1889 catalog is p. 86.

### 5. THE DATE C. 1872: CAN IT BE VERIFIED?

The date "c. 1872" is significant and defensible. Here is the evidence:

- Koenig developed the **first (fixed-resonator) sound analyzer around 1865**.
- He developed the **improved (adjustable-resonator) sound analyzer for his vowel studies between 1865 and 1872**. Multiple scholarly sources (University of Toronto, Sound & Science project, ENS Éditions) confirm this 1865–1872 window.
- The improved model first appears in Koenig's **1873 catalog**, suggesting it was finalized by 1872–1873.
- The instrument was thus a relatively recent product when Koenig displayed his collection at the **1876 Philadelphia Centennial Exhibition**.

The date c. 1872 likely places this instrument at the **transition point** between Koenig's vowel research phase and the commercial availability of the improved analyzer. Whether this specific date derives from Teylers' archival purchase records, an inscription on the instrument, or a stylistic dating by a curator cannot be determined from publicly available sources. However, the date is consistent with the known production timeline and is more precise than the broader ranges assigned to comparable instruments (e.g., Toronto's "c. 1870–1890").

**A question remains:** Is the Teylers instrument the improved No. 242 model (with adjustable resonators) or the earlier No. 242a (with fixed resonators)? The c. 1872 date and the "total height 895 mm" suggest the improved model, which would make it one of the **earliest examples of the improved analyzer** — potentially from Koenig's own vowel research period.

### 6. CONNECTION TO TEYLERS MUSEUM: ACQUISITION HISTORY

The object number "FK 1996.01" is revealing:

- **"FK"** stands for **Fysisch Kabinet** (Physics Cabinet), Teylers Museum's scientific instrument collection.
- **"1996"** is the accession number within the Fysisch Kabinet's sequential numbering system.
- **".01"** indicates this is the first sub-item of accession 1996.

**Critical finding:** The FK numbering does NOT refer to the year 1996. The Fysisch Kabinet uses a sequential numbering system running from FK 0001 through FK 2000+. Instruments with nearby FK numbers (e.g., FK 1994.03) are not from the year 1994 — they are simply the 1994th accession. This is confirmed by examining other FK numbers: FK 0002 is a c. 1750 measuring ruler, FK 0814 is a gramophone, FK 1041 is a Helmholtz resonator, etc. — all numbered sequentially, not by acquisition year.

**However**, there is evidence from a web source (a Prezi presentation about the klankanalysator of Rudolf Koenig at Teylers Museum) and from search results suggesting that the klankanalysator was **"purchased by the Teylers Museum to complement their Koenig collection in 1996."** If true, this would mean the object number and the acquisition year are coincidentally aligned — or that the museum's numbering had reached the 1996 range around the time of actual 1996 acquisitions.

**Teylers' existing Koenig holdings:** Teylers already possessed multiple Koenig instruments, including:
- FK 0322: Ronddraaiende spiegel, naar Koenig (Rotating mirror, after Koenig) — the very mirror component used with manometric flame instruments
- FK 0283: Manometric capsule
- FK 1041: Klankbodem met Helmholtz resonator (Soundboard with Helmholtz resonator)
- FK 0782: Opslaand tongwerk in buis met resonator (Reed in tube with resonator)
- Various other acoustic instruments by or after Koenig

The sound analyzer would have been a natural and prestigious addition to this existing acoustics collection. It is described as being displayed in the **Instrumentenzaal** (Instruments Hall) at Teylers.

The museum's complete financial archives survive from 1778, so the exact acquisition date, source, and purchase price could be confirmed through archival research.

### 7. THE 1876 PHILADELPHIA CENTENNIAL EXHIBITION

Koenig's display at the 1876 Philadelphia Centennial Exhibition was one of the most celebrated scientific exhibits of the 19th century.

**What was displayed:**
- The **Grand Tonomètre**: 670+ tuning forks covering 800 tones from 16 to 4,096 Hz, arranged in orderly rows
- **Wave sirens**: the first model was shown at Philadelphia
- **Manometric flame apparatus**: which particularly impressed Alexander Graham Bell, who sought out Koenig at the exhibition
- Various other acoustic instruments from Koenig's catalog

**Was the sound analyzer part of the exhibition?** The exhibition included manometric flame apparatus, and Koenig brought a comprehensive collection. While the specific inclusion of the sound analyzer is not confirmed in sources reviewed, it is highly probable: the analyzer was Koenig's flagship demonstration instrument for visualizing sound, and by 1876 both the original (No. 242a, c. 1865) and improved (No. 242, c. 1872) versions existed. The exhibition judges' praise — "There is no other [exhibit] in the present International Exhibition which surpasses it in scientific interest" — would have been informed by such a dramatic visual demonstration.

**Aftermath:** The collection did not sell easily in America. A "splendid collection" was retained at the Stevens Institute of Technology under a promise of payment that was never fulfilled. Eventually, parts were distributed: the Grand Tonomètre went to West Point (U.S. Military Academy), and portions went to the University of Toronto (purchased by James Loudon in 1878 with $12,000 from the Province of Ontario) and back to Paris.

### 8. HELMHOLTZ'S THEORY OF SOUND AND THE ANALYZER'S ROLE

Hermann von Helmholtz's *Die Lehre von den Tonempfindungen als physiologische Grundlage für die Theorie der Musik* (1863; English translation *On the Sensations of Tone*, 1875) was the foundational work of physiological acoustics. Its core arguments:

1. **Fourier analysis of sound:** Every complex sound (musical note, vowel, etc.) consists of a fundamental tone plus a series of upper partials (harmonics/overtones) at integer multiples of the fundamental frequency.
2. **Timbre is determined by overtone structure:** The perceived "quality" or "color" of a sound (why a violin and a flute sound different playing the same note) depends on the relative intensities of these partials.
3. **The ear as analyzer:** Helmholtz proposed that structures in the inner ear (the basilar membrane in the cochlea) act as resonators, each tuned to different frequencies, performing a mechanical Fourier analysis.
4. **Resonators as proof:** Helmholtz demonstrated his theory using spherical brass resonators held to the ear. Each resonator amplified a specific frequency, allowing the listener to "hear out" individual partials from a complex sound.

**The sound analyzer's relationship to this theory:** Koenig's sound analyzer is the **visual, objective demonstration** of Helmholtz's theory. Where Helmholtz relied on the subjective experience of holding a resonator to the ear — leaving "an unwelcome element of subjectivity in acoustic experimentation" — Koenig made the analysis visible and public. The bank of resonators performs the same frequency-selective function as Helmholtz's ear resonators, but instead of activating the listener's perception, they activate gas flames that everyone in the lecture hall can observe simultaneously.

The instrument was also central to Koenig's own research on **vowel sounds** — his attempt to determine which combination of harmonics distinguishes one vowel from another (e.g., "ah" from "ee" from "oo"). This work was a direct extension of Helmholtz's Chapter V on vowel quality.

Ironically, the same instrument was also used in the **dispute** between Helmholtz and Koenig. The precision of Koenig's apparatus allowed him to conduct experiments whose results contradicted some of Helmholtz's theoretical predictions about combination tones, fueling their scientific disagreement.
