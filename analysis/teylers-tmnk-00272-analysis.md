## Object Description

This is a **prinsendaalder** — a large silver coin struck in 1584 at the Dordrecht mint, bearing the portrait of **William the Silent (Willem van Oranje)** with a sword over his shoulder. It is the **only official Dutch coin to depict William of Orange**, and the 1584 date places it at the exact historical fulcrum of his assassination on **10 July 1584** by Balthasar Gerard. The coin was designed as political propaganda by the States of Holland, intended to facilitate William's formal inauguration as sovereign — which never took place. Dies were engraved by **Gerard van Bylaer**, one of the most important medallists of the Dutch Revolt, and the coin was produced under mint master **Jacob Janszoon de Jonge** at the Dordrecht mint, which had operated continuously since 1367.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. THIS COIN MAY BE A POSTHUMOUS PORTRAIT

The catalog does not note the extraordinary chronological significance: William of Orange was assassinated on **10 July 1584**. This coin is dated 1584. Coins struck after 10 July 1584 are **de facto posthumous issues** — they depict a dead man as if he were still alive and about to be inaugurated as sovereign. The coin was originally propaganda for a living leader; William was killed "a few days before his investiture."

Minting continued through 1585 in the first period, and the portrait was deliberately reissued in a second minting period (1591–1602) as a national symbol. The catalog should note this assassination context.

### 2. COIN TYPE NOT IDENTIFIED — THIS IS A "PRINSENDAALDER"

The catalog calls this a "gehelmde rijksdaalder" (helmeted rixdaalder) but does not use the widely recognized numismatic name **prinsendaalder** — the standard term in Dutch numismatics. This is important because:

- The **prinsendaalder** (1583–1585, 1591–1602) is a specifically Holland provincial issue
- It is **not** a leicesterrijksdaalder (1586–1597), which was a different coin introduced by the States-General under Robert Dudley, Earl of Leicester
- The prinsendaalder was struck to approximate the German Reichstaler standard (29.03 g, 0.885 fineness)
- It was valued at 42 stuivers (raised to 45 stuivers from 1586)

### 3. INSCRIPTION NOT TRANSLATED OR EXPLAINED

The full inscription reads: **MO NO ARG COMIT HOL ZEL VIGILATE DEO CONFIDENTE 1584**

- **MO NO ARG COMIT HOL ZEL** = *Moneta Nova Argentea Comitatus Hollandiae et Zelandiae* = "New Silver Coin of the County of Holland and Zeeland"
- **VIGILATE DEO CONFIDENTE** = "Watch and Trust in God" — the **official motto of the States of Holland**, expressing spiritual determination during the Dutch Revolt against Spain

The motto remains the official motto of the province of **Zuid-Holland** today. The inscription's claim to represent both Holland **and** Zeeland reflects the political alliance between these two maritime provinces at the core of the revolt. The catalog provides no translation or context.

### 4. GERARD VAN BYLAER: MAJOR MEDALLIST OF THE DUTCH REVOLT

The catalog lists "Gerard van Bylaer (±1540–1617)" without context. He was born **c. 1553 in Barneveld** (the catalog's birth date of ±1540 appears too early), was of **Mennonite** faith, and became one of the most important medallists of the Dutch Revolt era:

- Appointed **die cutter at the Dordrecht Mint** on 16 July 1580
- Cut dies for **all triumph medals ordered by the States-General** until his death
- Notable works include medals commemorating the **Siege of Leiden** (1574), the **rejection of Philip II** (1583), the **assassination of William I** (1584), and the **defeat of the Spanish Armada** (1588)
- Also worked at Middelburg (1586–1601) and Harderwijk (1592–1593)

The catalog's birth date of "±1540" may need revision — multiple sources give c. 1553.

### 5. JACOB JANSZOON DE JONGE: MINT MASTER CONTEXT

The catalog lists the mint master without context. **Jacob Jansz. de Jonge** (also Van Velzen/Van Haarlem) was appointed mint master at Dordrecht on **30 April 1580** and served until his death in **1607** — a 27-year tenure spanning the critical period of the Dutch Revolt. He oversaw production of the first gold ducats (1583), the prinsendaalders (1583–1585), and various denominations through the early Republic. His son Jacob Jacobsz. de Jonge succeeded him.

### 6. WEIGHT ANALYSIS: EXCELLENT PRESERVATION

The catalog records the weight as 28.8316 g. The standard weight for a prinsendaalder was **29.03 g**. This specimen has retained **99.3%** of its original standard weight — a deficit of only 0.68%, indicating the coin saw limited circulation. Numista lists 28.62 g as a typical recorded weight for surviving prinsendaalders, making this specimen **above average** in preservation.

### 7. JEAN HENRI CORONEL NOT IDENTIFIED

The provenance states "purchased 31 January 1902 from Jean Henri Coronel (1846–1934)" without identifying him. Coronel was a coin dealer associated with The Hague; the **Coronel** surname is a well-known **Sephardic Jewish** family name in the Netherlands. He appears to have been a recognized figure in the Dutch numismatic trade at the turn of the 20th century, possibly associated with the Koninklijk Nederlands Genootschap voor Munt- en Penningkunde.

### 8. DORDRECHT MINT NOT CONTEXTUALIZED

The Dordrecht mint operated from **1367 to 1806** — nearly 440 years, one of the longest-running mints in the Low Countries. It struck the first Dutch gold ducat (1583) and the first guilder (1375), a denomination that survived until the Euro in 2002. The mint building still stands at Munt 5, Dordrecht.
