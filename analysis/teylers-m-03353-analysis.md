## Object Description

M 03353 is one of the most historically significant geological specimens in any museum collection in the world: a fragment of rock chipped from the highest ice-free exposed rock outcrop at the summit of Mont Blanc on 3 August 1787, by the Genevan scientist Horace-Bénédict de Saussure (1740–1799) during his ascent. The rock outcrop from which it was taken, the Rocher de la Tournette, stands at approximately 4,677 metres on the summit ridge of Mont Blanc. The specimen weighs 147.7 grams and shows an irregular shape consistent with having been chipped from an exposed rock face.

The specimen is a **biotite granite** — specifically a leucocratic monzogranite — the dominant rock type forming the Mont Blanc massif. The granite consists of quartz, potassium feldspar (sometimes large white rectangular crystals), plagioclase, and black biotite mica, with the biotite partially replaced by greenish chlorite as a metamorphic alteration product. Both catalog images confirm this: one shows the fresh broken surface with a granitic speckled texture in grey-pink tones; the other shows the rougher, slightly more weathered outer surface with visible greenish chlorite patches and a museum label reading "W47 / M3353."

De Saussure's ascent (3 August 1787) was the third overall ascent of Mont Blanc and the second by a scientist. It was a major scientific expedition: he brought 18 guides, scientific instruments including barometers, thermometers, a hygrometer, a magnetometer, a cyanometer (to measure sky blueness), and an electrometer. At the summit he made 17 hours of observations, measured barometric pressure to calculate summit altitude (arriving at 4,775 m — within 30 m of the modern value of 4,805.59 m), and collected rock samples. His findings were published in his multi-volume *Voyages dans les Alpes* (1779–1796).

The specimen was acquired by Teylers Museum in 1802, purchased by director Martinus van Marum from de Saussure's son Nicolas-Théodore de Saussure, three years after Horace-Bénédict's death. It is displayed in the Oval Room alongside the Exchaquet maquette (M 12369).

## Key Findings

### 1. "CREATOR" FIELD MISATTRIBUTES DE SAUSSURE'S ROLE

The catalog lists Horace-Bénédict de Saussure as **"onderzoeker" (researcher)** in the creator field. While accurate in that he was a researcher, applying the "creator" field to a natural rock specimen is a cataloging category error. De Saussure did not create the rock — he **collected** it. The appropriate field would be "Collector" or "Verzamelaar," and it should be combined with the collection date. The current structure implies de Saussure somehow authored or manufactured the specimen, which is misleading.

### 2. ROCK TYPE DESCRIPTION IN SCIENTIFIC NAME FIELD IS IMPRECISE

The "scientific (common) name" field reads: **"Graniet overtrokken met chloriet (v. d. hoogste top van Mont Blanc)"** — "granite coated with chlorite (from the highest summit of Mont Blanc)." This is descriptive rather than scientific. The correct petrological classification is:

- **Rock type:** Biotite granite (leucocratic monzogranite)
- **Formal petrology:** The Mont Blanc massif is composed of a late Hercynian (Variscan) granitic intrusion emplaced approximately 300 million years ago during the late stages of the Variscan orogeny.
- **Mineralogy:** Quartz + potassium feldspar (orthoclase/microcline) + plagioclase + biotite ± muscovite, with chlorite as a secondary replacement of biotite.
- The "chlorite coating" is not a coating — it is **in situ chloritisation of biotite**, a metamorphic replacement process. Calling it a coating ("overtrokken met chloriet") implies a surface deposit, which is petrologically misleading.

### 3. ALTITUDE OF COLLECTION MISSING

The catalog states the specimen was collected at the top of Mont Blanc but gives no altitude. The rock was collected from the Rocher de la Tournette at **4,677 m** — not the absolute summit snowfield (4,805.59 m), which is permanently ice-covered. This distinction matters scientifically and historically: de Saussure collected from the highest *exposed rock*, not from the absolute highest point. This nuance is entirely absent from the catalog.

### 4. DE SAUSSURE'S DATES MISSING

The catalog names the creator but gives no dates for Horace-Bénédict de Saussure. He lived 17 February 1740 – 22 January 1799. He is recognised as a founder of alpine geology, an early user of the term "geology," and the originator of systematic Alpine natural history research.

### 5. ACQUISITION HISTORY OMITTED

The catalog has no provenance information beyond the date of collection (1787). The specimen's journey from de Saussure's personal collection to Teylers is unrecorded in the catalog:

- De Saussure retained the specimen until his death in 1799
- In 1802, Martinus van Marum purchased it from de Saussure's son Nicolas-Théodore de Saussure
- This provenance chain is scientifically and historically significant and is entirely absent

### 6. "PRODUCTION DATE: 1787" IS CATEGORY-INAPPROPRIATE

The date 1787 refers to the date of collection on the mountain. This should be identified as the **collection date** rather than a "production date." Granite is a geological material 300 million years old, not something "produced" in 1787. The catalog's use of "production date" across the mineral and rock collection consistently misrepresents the nature of natural specimens. A preferred structure would distinguish:

- **Geological age:** c. 300 million years (Late Carboniferous, Hercynian orogeny)
- **Date collected:** 3 August 1787
- **Date acquired by Teylers:** 1802

### 7. PUBLICATION REFERENCE MISSING

De Saussure documented the 1787 ascent and geological observations in detail in *Voyages dans les Alpes*, vol. 4 (1796). The specimen is directly connected to this foundational work in Alpine geology. No bibliographic reference appears in the catalog.

### 8. SCIENTIFIC SIGNIFICANCE OF THE ASCENT UNDERSTATED

The free-text date field provides minimal context. De Saussure's 1787 ascent was historically significant as:

- One of the first three ascents of Mont Blanc (the highest peak in the Alps and Western Europe)
- A landmark event in the history of geology, combining mountaineering with systematic scientific observation for the first time at extreme altitude
- The source of barometric data that confirmed the altitude of Mont Blanc
- An object of direct study as a natural history specimen in 19th-century mineralogy

None of this appears in the catalog.

### 9. RELATED LABEL OBJECT NOT INTEGRATED

The catalog references a "Label van het topje van de Mont Blanc" as a related object. This label, noted in the mineralogy.eu archive as a manuscript label by Martinus van Marum, reads: "the specimen of the top of the Mont Blanc as collected by de Saussure on August 3, 1787." This primary source documentation is related but not linked in any detail. The manuscript label's text and significance should be integrated into the main record.
