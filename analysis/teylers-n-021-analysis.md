## Object Description

This is a red chalk drawing by Hendrick Goltzius (1558–1617), depicting the famous equestrian statue of Marcus Aurelius on the Capitoline Hill in Rome. It was made during Goltzius' transformative Italian journey of 1590–1591. The drawing is one of approximately 125 Goltzius sheets held by Teylers Museum — the largest collection of Goltzius drawings in the world. It was acquired in 1790 from the Odescalchi heirs in Rome, as part of a purchase of approximately 1,700 drawings that had passed through the collection of Queen Christina of Sweden.

## Visual Analysis of the Drawing

The drawing depicts the equestrian statue of Marcus Aurelius in red chalk (sanguine) on white paper, viewed from a slightly off-center frontal position, with the horse's head turning slightly to the right.

**The rider (Marcus Aurelius):**
- Shown in imperial Roman garb: a flowing toga/paludamentum draped over the left shoulder and gathered at the waist
- Right arm extended in a gesture of command or clemency (the "adlocutio" pose)
- Curly hair and short beard rendered with careful attention to the bronze original's texture
- Sandaled feet visible, with decorative military boots (calcei)
- The figure is powerfully modeled, with warm flesh-like tones giving life to what is actually a bronze statue

**The horse:**
- A robust, muscular horse in a walking pose, left foreleg raised
- The mane is depicted with fine parallel strokes suggesting the bronze's stylized rendering
- The horse's expressive face — large eye, flared nostril — shows Goltzius' characteristic vitality
- The decorative horse blanket (saddlecloth) with fringed tassels is carefully recorded
- Three hooves on the ground, one raised — accurately representing the original bronze

**The pedestal:**
- Lightly sketched at the bottom of the composition, far less finished than the statue itself
- This suggests Goltzius was primarily interested in the sculpture, not its architectural setting

**Paper condition:**
- The corners have been **clipped** (octagonal trimming), a practice common in 18th-century collector mounting
- Age foxing/spots visible, particularly on the left side and edges
- The sheet appears to be in overall good condition for its age (over 430 years old)

**Technique:**
- The red chalk is used with extraordinary skill, ranging from light, transparent strokes for highlights to dense, blended passages for deep shadows
- Goltzius used the "reuselen" (blending/stumping) technique to smooth transitions, creating an almost painterly effect
- The warm reddish-brown tones of red chalk naturally evoke the bronze patina of the original statue — a conscious artistic choice

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. EXTRAORDINARY PROVENANCE: QUEEN CHRISTINA OF SWEDEN

The provenance note "aangekocht van de Odescalchi erfgenamen te Rome, 1790" is accurate but incomplete. The full provenance chain is:

1. **Hendrick Goltzius** (created 1590–1591 in Rome)
2. **Queen Christina of Sweden** (1626–1689) — the drawing entered her collection in Rome after her 1654 abdication and relocation to Italy. Christina assembled a major art collection including approximately 275 paintings and ~2,000 drawings
3. **Prince Livio Odescalchi** (1652–1713), Duke of Bracciano, nephew of Pope Innocent XI — purchased Queen Christina's entire collection *en bloc* on **6 January 1692**, three years after her death. He significantly expanded the drawing collection
4. **Odescalchi heirs** at the Palazzo Odescalchi, Piazza dei Santi Apostoli, Rome
5. **Teylers Museum** — purchased approximately **1,700 drawings** from the Odescalchi heirs in **1790**, through Willem Anne Lestevenon

The Queen Christina provenance is a significant detail absent from the catalog record. This 1790 purchase was one of the most important acquisitions in Teylers' history, instantly giving the young museum (founded 1778) a world-class Old Master drawing collection including works by Michelangelo, Raphael, Bernini, the Carracci, Guido Reni, and Guercino — alongside this extraordinary group of 125 Goltzius drawings.

### 2. GOLTZIUS' ITALIAN JOURNEY: FULLER CONTEXT

The catalog notes "ontstaan tijdens Goltzius' bezoek aan Rome" but provides no further detail. Goltzius departed Haarlem in **October 1590**, traveling incognito (reportedly posing as a Dutch cheese merchant to avoid social obligations). He traveled with the silversmith **Jan Matthijsz Ban** and antiquary **Philips van Winghe** via Munich and Venice, arriving in Rome in **January 1591**. He returned to Haarlem by late 1591.

This journey was transformative — it moved Goltzius from Northern Mannerism toward a classicizing style. The Marcus Aurelius drawing belongs to a remarkable series of classical sculpture studies made in Rome, including:
- Farnese Hercules (front and back views, both at Teylers)
- Alexander and Bucephalus (Quirinal horse tamers)
- Belvedere Torso, Antinous, Laocoon
- Trophy of Marius (two versions)
- Michelangelo's Moses

### 3. THE MARCUS AURELIUS STATUE: WHAT GOLTZIUS SAW

In 1590–91, the Piazza del Campidoglio was **mid-construction** according to Michelangelo's 1536 design:
- The statue stood on **Michelangelo's pedestal** (installed 1538–1539) at the center of the piazza
- The Palazzo Senatorio had its new staircase, but the Palazzo Nuovo **had not yet been built**
- The Cordonata (monumental staircase) had been completed in 1582
- Pope Sixtus V had just placed the "Trophies of Marius" on the balustrade in 1590
- The famous oval pavement pattern was **not yet laid** (completed only in the 20th century)

This drawing thus documents the statue in an interim state of its surroundings — the Campidoglio partly realized, 50 years after Michelangelo began the redesign.

### 4. THE "N" PREFIX EXPLAINED

The "N" prefix most likely stands for **"Nederlands" (Dutch/Netherlandish)**, distinguishing drawings by Northern artists from Italian drawings (which use other prefixes such as "K"). This is consistent with the museum's practice of publishing separate catalogs for Italian and Netherlandish drawings.

### 5. RED CHALK SIGNIFICANCE

Goltzius' choice of red chalk (sanguine) for this drawing is artistically significant:
- The warm reddish-brown tones **evoke the patina of the bronze** original
- Goltzius used red and black chalk separately for sculpture studies — never mixing them — a distinctive practice
- His "reuselen" blending technique creates painterly, three-dimensional effects that give the statue a living, breathing quality
- As Karel van Mander noted, Goltzius insisted that "flesh parts in particular had to be colored with cryons" — the red chalk transforms cold bronze into warm life

### 6. GOLTZIUS AND HAARLEM

The catalog does not mention the profound connection between Goltzius and Haarlem. Goltzius lived and worked in Haarlem for **40 years** (1577–1617), making the city his adopted home. He co-founded the informal "Haarlem Academy" with Karel van Mander and Cornelis Corneliszoon van Haarlem (c. 1586–1591). He died in Haarlem on 1 January 1617 and was buried in the Grote Kerk. Teylers Museum, also in Haarlem, is thus the natural institutional home for his work — holding 125 of his drawings, the largest collection worldwide.

### 7. DIMENSION NOTE

The catalog field appears to render the height as "411 cm" in the raw data, though this must be **411 mm** (41.1 cm). A drawing of 4.11 meters would be impossible. The dimensions of 411×310 mm are plausible for a large sculpture study sheet. For comparison, the Farnese Hercules drawing at Teylers measures 360×210 mm.

### 8. SCHOLARLY REFERENCES

The three scholarly references mentioned in the catalog likely correspond to:
1. **E.K.J. Reznicek, *Die Zeichnungen von Hendrick Goltzius***, 2 vols., Utrecht, 1961 (catalogue raisonné, 449 drawings; supplement 1993)
2. **Yvonne Bleyerveld and Ilja M. Veldman, *The Netherlandish Drawings of the 16th Century in Teylers Museum***, Primavera Pers, Leiden, 2016 (257 drawings cataloged, including 125 Goltzius sheets)
3. Possibly a volume from the Teylers drawings catalog series, or the 2015 exhibition catalog *Drawn from the Antique: Artists & the Classical Ideal*

### 9. THE ONLY SURVIVING EQUESTRIAN STATUE FROM ANTIQUITY

The Marcus Aurelius is the **only complete equestrian bronze surviving from Roman antiquity** — approximately 22 such monuments originally existed in Rome. It survived because it was long misidentified as Emperor Constantine, the first Christian emperor, and was thus spared the melting-down that destroyed all others. The original is now **inside the Palazzo dei Conservatori** (Musei Capitolini) since 1981; a replica stands in the piazza. Goltzius' drawing thus records one of the most important works of ancient art.
