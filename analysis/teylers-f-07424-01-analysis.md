## Object Description

F 07424-01 is a jaw fragment of *Mosasaurus hoffmanni* (Mantell, 1829), part of the historically decisive specimen cataloged as TM 7424 at Teylers Museum. This specimen — consisting of three partial jaw fragments along with cervical vertebrae and a femur — represents the first scientifically documented remains of a mosasaur ever recovered anywhere in the world. The fossils were extracted from a chalk quarry in the Sint Pietersberg near Maastricht, Netherlands, in 1764, making them foundational to both vertebrate palaeontology and to the concept of biological extinction.

The specimen was acquired by Teylers Museum in 1784, when director Martinus van Marum purchased the palaeontological collection of Jean-Baptiste Drouin, a lieutenant colonel in the French army who had collected the remains from the Sint Pietersberg in 1766. Unlike the more famous second Maastricht skull (now MNHN AC 9648 in Paris, seized by French forces in 1795), TM 7424 was protected from confiscation because it belonged to Teylers Museum, which retained legal protections. The specimen remains on permanent display in Teylers' fossil hall.

The jaw fragment is preserved in its natural white chalk matrix, as visible in the catalog image: multiple elongated jaw bones are embedded in a cream-coloured limestone block, with the characteristic pleurodont teeth of the mosasaur clearly visible. The Maastrichtian chalk in which it is embedded is itself the type sediment for the Maastrichtian Stage of the Late Cretaceous (72–66 million years ago), making the specimen doubly significant — both the founding fossil of mosasaur science and a type locality specimen of the geological stage in which it lived.

## Key Findings

### 1. SPECIES EPITHET SPELLING IS INCORRECT

The catalog uses **"Mosasaurus hoffmannii"** (double-i). This was proposed as a correction by Konishi et al. (2014), who argued that Mantell's 1829 original spelling with double-i should be restored as a Latinisation of "Hoffmann." However, a 2024 peer-reviewed nomenclatural note published in the *Netherlands Journal of Geosciences* — authored in part by Anne S. Schulp of **Teylers Museum itself** — presents historical evidence against this and recommends under ICZN Article 33.2.3.1 that the correct spelling is **hoffmanni** (single-i), which has been in exclusive prevailing use since Mantell (1851). The article concludes that Hoffmann himself did not Latinise his name (as shown in three surviving letters from 1771, 1774, and 1775), that Latinisation of surnames had gone out of fashion in Dutch academic circles by the late 18th century, and that the spelling "hoffmanni" therefore represents prevailing use and must be retained.

The correct name to use is: ***Mosasaurus hoffmanni* Mantell, 1829** — not *hoffmannii*.

### 2. DESCRIPTION CRITICALLY INCOMPLETE: "KAAK" (JAW) IS INADEQUATE

The entire description field consists of the single Dutch word "kaak" (jaw). This is wholly insufficient for a specimen of this global historical importance. The catalog does not state:
- Which jaw element(s) are preserved (partial skull, mandible fragments, quadrate?)
- That the specimen consists of three partial jaw fragments plus cervical vertebrae and a femur
- That this is the **first mosasaur specimen ever collected**, predating the famous Paris specimen
- That TM 7424 is the founding material for the genus *Mosasaurus*
- That Cuvier used the specimens in his development of the concept of biological extinction
- That the chalk matrix is itself from the **Maastrichtian Stage** (type locality)

### 3. ACQUISITION HISTORY ENTIRELY ABSENT

The catalog provides no provenance or acquisition information. The documented history is:
- Discovered in chalk quarry, Sint Pietersberg, Maastricht, **1764**
- Collected by French army officer **Jean-Baptiste Drouin**, 1766
- Drouin corresponded about the specimen with naturalists including Petrus Camper and Johann Leonhard Hoffmann (the person honoured in the species name)
- Purchased by **Martinus van Marum** for Teylers Museum in **1784** as part of Drouin's palaeontological collection
- Protected from French confiscation in **1795** (unlike the Paris specimen, TM 7424 remained in Haarlem)
- Now permanently displayed in the First Fossil Hall

None of this appears in the catalog record.

### 4. GEOLOGICAL FORMATION NOT SPECIFIED

The catalog records the production period as "72–66 million years ago (Maastrichtian)" which is a reasonable age range, but does not name the geological formation. The specimen comes from the **Maastricht Formation** (Maastrichtian chalk), the type section of the Maastrichtian Stage. This is a fundamental omission: the specimen's significance is partly geological, not only palaeontological.

### 5. COLLECTION PLACE ERRORS AND ANACHRONISM

The catalog lists the collection place as including "ENCI quarry." The ENCI cement quarry (ENCI = Eerste Nederlandse Cement Industrie) was not established until **1926**. In 1764, the specimen came from a **subterranean chalk mine** (an underground gallery system), not from an open-pit quarry. The application of the ENCI quarry name to an 18th-century discovery is an anachronism. The correct locality description is: Sint Pietersberg (Mount Saint Peter), Maastricht, Netherlands; a chalk mine.

### 6. NO DIMENSIONS RECORDED

The catalog records no dimensions for the specimen. For a fossil of this significance — the earliest known mosasaur material, a centrepiece of the First Fossil Hall — this is a major omission. Published literature (Schulp et al. 2013; Pieters et al. 2012) documents the specimen's dimensions and could be used to populate this field.

### 7. NO BIBLIOGRAPHY

No literature references are provided in the catalog record. The specimen is discussed in a substantial body of scientific literature spanning two centuries, including:
- Camper, A.G. (1800) — first scientific description of the genus
- Conybeare (1822) — establishment of genus *Mosasaurus*
- Mantell (1829) — establishment of species *hoffmanni*
- Cuvier (1808, 1812) — use of the specimen in extinction theory
- Bardet & Jagt (1996) — history of the specimen
- Pieters et al. (2012) — provenance and acquisition
- Schulp et al. (2013) — anatomy and rare specimens
- Schulp et al. (2024, *Neth. J. Geosciences*) — nomenclatural note on the species epithet

### 8. CANONICAL STATUS NOT NOTED

The Mosasaurus is one of four Teylers specimens designated as part of the **Canon van Nederland** (Canon of the Netherlands), reflecting its status as a nationally and internationally significant cultural heritage object. This is not noted anywhere in the catalog record.
