## Object Description

An oil sketch on prepared cardboard (29 x 41.3 cm) by **George Hendrik Breitner** (Rotterdam, 1857 – Amsterdam, 1923), depicting two young girls in the dunes, painted en plein air during his period living in **Loosduinen**, near The Hague (c. 1884–1886). Brown cardboard shows through the paint in places; preliminary chalk lines are still visible. Breitner told his friend Willem Witsen he intended to develop approximately twenty such studies into *"een groot ding"* — a grand large-scale work that was apparently never completed. Acquired in **1968** with support from the Olga Heldring Fonds, under the Fundatie Van den Santheuvel, Sobbe. Designated one of Teylers' **50 Topstukken**.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. SUBJECT METADATA INCONSISTENT WITH TITLE

The catalog tags the subject as "Mens, Vrouw" (Human, Woman). The painting is titled *Kinderen in het duin* (Children in the Dunes), and the museum's own text describes "twee meisjes" (two girls). Cataloging children under "Vrouw" (Woman) rather than "Kind" (Child) is a **metadata inconsistency**.

### 2. PLEIN AIR POCHADE STATUS NOT EXPLAINED

The catalog records the medium as "oil on prepared cardboard" but does not contextualise this as a **plein air pochade** — a field oil sketch made on-location as preparatory material. Key technical observations from the museum's own description are absent from the catalog fields:

- Brown cardboard shows through the paint where areas are left unpainted
- Preliminary sketch/chalk lines remain visible beneath the paint
- Radical snapshot-like cropping

These features are characteristic of Breitner's known interest in **photography** as a compositional tool. The pochade format should be explicitly identified.

### 3. THE "GRAND THING" — CONTEXT OF THE SERIES NOT NOTED

The catalog does not mention that this is one of approximately twenty on-location studies Breitner planned to synthesise into a large-scale composition. His own statement to **Willem Witsen** — that he intended to develop the series into *"een groot ding"* — is documented and of significant art-historical interest. The planned final work was never completed, making these studies the only surviving evidence of Breitner's dune ambitions from this period.

### 4. LOOSDUINSE SCHOOL CONTEXT ABSENT

The catalog does not place the work within the context of the **Loosduinen painters' colony**. During his residence on Beeklaan, Loosduinen (1883–1886), Breitner was working near an informal group of painters including Akkeringa, Dankmeijer, Koning, and later De Zwart — sometimes called the "Loosduinse School." This regional context is relevant for dating and situating the work.

### 5. PROVENANCE BEFORE 1968 NOT GIVEN

The painting was created c. 1884–1886 and acquired by Teylers in 1968 — an 82-year gap for which no ownership history is provided. For a designated Topstuk, this provenance gap is a significant omission.

### 6. FUNDATIE VAN DEN SANTHEUVEL, SOBBE NOT EXPLAINED

The credit line names the Fundatie Van den Santheuvel, Sobbe but does not identify it. The Fundatie is a Dutch philanthropic umbrella foundation based in Amsterdam (established 28 April 1905), founded by Cornelia Petronella van den Santheuvel-Sobbe and her brother Carel Leendert Sobbe. The **Olga Heldring Fonds** is one of twelve sub-funds under this umbrella, established in 1930 by Olga Heldring (1880–1958); its statutes were broadened in 1958 to include support for art in general.

### 7. BREITNER'S SIGNIFICANCE NOT INDICATED

The catalog provides no context for the artist:

- **Leading figure of Amsterdam Impressionism** — styled himself *"le peintre du peuple"* (the painter of the common people)
- The Dutch expression **"Echt Breitnerweer"** (typical Breitner weather) is still used to describe grey, rainy Amsterdam days
- **Auction record**: USD 1,080,430 (EUR 760,250) for a canal scene (Christie's Amsterdam, 2007)
- His *De Gele Rijders* (1886) was the **first modern painting purchased by the Rijksmuseum**
- Briefly worked alongside **Vincent van Gogh** in The Hague, 1882
