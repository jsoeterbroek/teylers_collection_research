## Object Description

This is one of the most important fossils in any museum worldwide — the so-called **"Haarlem Archaeopteryx,"** described by Teylers' own Collection Manager as **"the Mona Lisa of the museum's fossil collection."** Found in 1855 near Jachenhausen, Bavaria, it was the **chronologically first Archaeopteryx specimen ever discovered** — predating the famous London specimen by six years — but was misidentified as a pterosaur (*Pterodactylus crassipes*) by Hermann von Meyer in 1857. It remained misidentified for over a century until **John Ostrom** recognized feather impressions during a visit to Teylers in September 1970. In **2017**, it was reclassified again: Foth & Rauhut demonstrated it is not *Archaeopteryx* at all, but a new genus — **Ostromia crassipes** — the first anchiornithid dinosaur found outside China. In May 2024, the fossil was secretly transported to the Diamond Light Source synchrotron in the UK for cutting-edge X-ray analysis.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. DATE "165-45 MILLION YEARS AGO" IS WRONG

The catalog states "165-45 million years ago." This is **incorrect**. The Painten Formation, from which this specimen derives, dates to the **Early Tithonian stage of the Late Jurassic**, approximately **150 million years ago** (149–152 Ma). The catalog's range of "165-45" spans from the Middle Jurassic through the Late Cretaceous — a period of 120 million years that makes no geological sense for a single specimen. This may be a data entry error (perhaps "165-145" with a missing "1"), but even that would be too broad.

**Correct age: approximately 150 million years old (Late Jurassic, Early Tithonian).**

### 2. TWICE MISIDENTIFIED: FULL TAXONOMIC HISTORY ABSENT

The catalog title says "Ostromia voorheen Archaeopteryx" without explaining this extraordinary history of misidentification:

| Year | Classification | By whom | Significance |
|------|---------------|---------|-------------|
| **1857** | *Pterodactylus crassipes* | Hermann von Meyer | Misidentified as a pterosaur — feather impressions unrecognized |
| **1970** | *Archaeopteryx* sp. | **John Ostrom** | Recognized feather impressions during a visit to Teylers; identified as the 4th known Archaeopteryx and retroactively the **first ever found** (1855) |
| **2017** | **Ostromia crassipes** | Foth & Rauhut | Reclassified as a new genus — NOT Archaeopteryx but an anchiornithid, the first found outside China |

This triple identity — pterosaur, then Archaeopteryx, then Ostromia — is itself one of the great stories in the history of paleontology. The catalog reduces it to four words.

### 3. JOHN OSTROM NOT IDENTIFIED

The genus name *Ostromia* honors **John Harold Ostrom** (1928–2005), the Yale paleontologist who:

- Discovered **Deinonychus** (1964/1969), triggering the "dinosaur renaissance" — the revolution that reimagined dinosaurs as active, warm-blooded animals
- Identified this very fossil as a feathered theropod during his September 1970 visit to Teylers
- In 1973, formally proposed that **birds evolved directly from theropod dinosaurs** — now the scientific consensus

The catalog does not explain the name's origin or Ostrom's significance.

### 4. THE 2017 RECLASSIFICATION: GLOBAL SCIENTIFIC IMPACT

Foth & Rauhut's 2017 paper in *BMC Evolutionary Biology* showed that the Haarlem specimen:

- **Differs markedly in proportions** from all Archaeopteryx specimens (metacarpal III/I ratio 2.21 vs. 2.99–3.33 in Archaeopteryx)
- Possesses **longitudinal furrows on manual phalanges** — absent in Archaeopteryx, present in the Chinese genus *Anchiornis*
- Has a **strongly flexed pubic shaft** with triangular pubic boot, unlike Archaeopteryx

The specimen was placed within **Anchiornithidae**, a clade previously known only from China. This means:
- The number of known Archaeopteryx specimens was reduced from 12 to 11
- **Anchiornithids were not restricted to eastern Asia** — Ostromia proves they reached Europe
- Evidence for an **explosive radiation of maniraptoran dinosaurs from eastern Asia** during the Late Jurassic, with multiple dispersal events westward

### 5. PROVENANCE: ACQUIRED 1860, VAN BREDA

The catalog provides no provenance history. The chain of custody:

1. **1855**: Discovered near Jachenhausen, Bavaria, in Painten Formation limestones
2. **1857**: Described by Hermann von Meyer as *Pterodactylus crassipes*
3. **March 1860**: Purchased from von Meyer by **Jacob Gijsbertus Samuël van Breda** (1788–1867), director of Teylers' Palaeontological and Mineralogical Cabinet
4. **1860–present**: Continuously at Teylers Museum (main slab TM 6928, counterslab TM 6929)

Van Breda's role is significant — he actively built the paleontological collection through his personal network and in-depth knowledge, acquiring this specimen three years after its initial description.

### 6. 2024 SYNCHROTRON RESEARCH NOT MENTIONED

In **May 2024**, the fossil was secretly transported under high security to the **Diamond Light Source** synchrotron facility in the UK — the first time it was studied with synchrotron X-ray techniques. Researchers used:
- X-ray fluorescence imaging
- X-ray absorption spectroscopy
- Microfocus X-ray technology

They investigated feather coloration (melanin), keratin in claw sheaths, and elemental composition. The research was led by Edgar Mulder (Utrecht University). Results are expected in a forthcoming paper.

### 7. PARALLELS WITH HOMO DILUVII TESTIS

The catalog does not note the remarkable parallel with Teylers' other famous fossil, the *Homo diluvii testis* (F 00001):

| | Homo diluvii testis | Ostromia / "Haarlem Archaeopteryx" |
|---|---|---|
| Originally identified as | Human (flood victim) | Pterosaur |
| Correctly identified by | **Georges Cuvier** (1811) | **John Ostrom** (1970) |
| Actually is | Giant salamander | Feathered theropod dinosaur |
| Years misidentified | ~85 years (1726–1811) | ~113 years (1857–1970) |
| Then reclassified again | — | As Ostromia, not Archaeopteryx (2017) |

Both fossils demonstrate how scientific understanding evolves — a theme central to Teylers' identity as a museum.

### 8. TYPE "FOSSIEL VAN OERVOGEL" IS NOW INCORRECT

The catalog classifies this as "fossiel van oervogel" (ancient bird fossil). Since the 2017 reclassification, Ostromia is classified as an **anchiornithid maniraptoran theropod dinosaur** — closely related to birds but not classified as a bird itself. The Dutch "oervogel" (ancient bird/Archaeopteryx) is no longer accurate for this specimen.
