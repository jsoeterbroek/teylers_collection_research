## Object Description

M 12369 is a large, three-dimensional terrain relief model of the Mont Blanc massif, created in 1787 by the Swiss engineer and cartographer Charles-François Exchaquet (6 December 1746 – 1792). The model depicts the Mont Blanc summit and the surrounding Chamonix valley in painted relief at a scale of approximately 1:15,000, measuring approximately 100 × 64 × 35 cm. It is constructed from nine glued wooden blocks carved in Swiss stone pine (arolla pine), shaped with chisels to the correct topography, finished with plaster, and covered with ground quartz to represent the glacial snowfields. It was painted, with snow cover reflecting August conditions (the prime climbing season).

The catalog image shows the front face of the model as seen from the Chamonix valley side. The high central peak (Mont Blanc summit) is visible at upper right with white-painted glaciers and snow, while the valley and rock faces are rendered in brown-ochre tones. The green-painted base is visible at the lower edge. The model is extraordinarily detailed for its period, made by a man who had personally explored the massif. Exchaquet served as Directeur général des Mines et Fonderies du Haut-Faucigny from his base in Servoz, near Chamonix, from 1780 until his death in 1792.

Exchaquet made or commissioned multiple copies of this maquette; as of recent scholarship, five copies were known to have been made, of which two definitive survivals are documented: one at Teylers Museum and one at the Muséum d'histoire naturelle de la Ville de Genève. The Teylers copy was acquired in 1799 — either purchased by director Martinus van Marum for nine guilders, or donated by Amsterdam merchant Govert Lups (sources differ on this point). It is displayed in the Oval Room of Teylers Museum alongside the De Saussure rock specimen (M 03353), with a dotted line tracing the 1787 ascent route.

## Key Findings

### 1. EXCHAQUET'S DATES ABSENT FROM CATALOG

The catalog lists Charles François Exchaquet as maker but gives no dates. He was born **6 December 1746** in Court, Canton of Berne, Switzerland, and died in **1792** in Servoz, Haute-Savoie. He was a Swiss engineer, cartographer, and mountaineer who served as Directeur général des Mines et Fonderies du Haut-Faucigny in Servoz from 1780 until his death. His early death at approximately 46 means he never saw the international recognition his maquette received.

### 2. DIMENSIONS ABSENT FROM CATALOG

The catalog record contains no dimensions. This is a significant omission for a large three-dimensional object. The known dimensions, established by independent scholarship, are approximately **100 × 64 × 35 cm** at a scale of **1:15,000**. Without dimensions, the object cannot be properly cataloged, conserved, or compared to other known copies of the maquette.

### 3. MATERIALS NOT SPECIFIED

The catalog record contains no information about what the maquette is made of. The materials are:

- **Structure:** Nine glued blocks of Swiss stone pine (Pinus cembra, Dutch: arolla den or cembersen den)
- **Surface modeling:** Plaster applied over the carved wood
- **Glaciers/snow:** Ground quartz applied to simulate snow and ice
- **Painted finish:** Paint applied over the plaster and quartz, with snow cover based on August alpine conditions
- **Base:** Green-painted

None of this appears in the catalog.

### 4. SCALE NOT RECORDED

The approximate cartographic scale of the model (1:15,000) is absent from the catalog. This is a fundamental specification for any terrain model or map-related object.

### 5. PROVENANCE IS CONTESTED AND UNRESOLVED IN CATALOG

The catalog contains no acquisition provenance. The historical record presents two conflicting accounts:

- **Version A:** In 1799, Van Marum purchased the maquette for **nine guilders**
- **Version B:** In 1799, the maquette was **donated by Amsterdam merchant Govert Lups**

Both accounts appear in the literature and both place the acquisition in 1799. The contradiction has not been resolved or even acknowledged in the catalog. The truth may be that Govert Lups donated it to Van Marum personally, who then formally acquired it for the museum for nine guilders — but the catalog gives no information whatsoever.

### 6. NUMBER OF SURVIVING COPIES UNADDRESSED

The catalog does not mention that multiple copies of this maquette exist. Exchaquet made or commissioned at least five copies. Knowing the number and locations of surviving copies is important for:

- Authenticating the Teylers specimen
- Understanding the intellectual history of the object
- Contextualising the Teylers copy within the broader survival of Exchaquet's work

The other confirmed surviving copy is in the Muséum d'histoire naturelle de la Ville de Genève.

### 7. CONNECTION TO DE SAUSSURE'S 1787 EXPEDITION NOT MADE EXPLICIT

The catalog lists M 12369 with related objects including "Mont Blanc climbing records from 1787" and M 03353 (the De Saussure rock specimen). However, no narrative explanation is given for why these objects are related or what the significance of their co-display is. The maquette was created the same year as De Saussure's ascent; the two objects together represent the scientific and cartographic record of the most important year in early Alpine exploration. This story is absent.

### 8. SCHOLARLY CONTEXT UNRECORDED

The Teylers library holds the publication by Paul Guichonnet and Charles Morand, *Charles-François Exchaquet (1746–1792) et les plans et relief du Mont-Blanc* (catalog number 22G-2 at Teylers), which is the definitive scholarly source on Exchaquet and his maquette. This reference is not cited in the M 12369 catalog record. A direct connection between the object record and the relevant scholarly publication held in the same institution should be standard practice.

### 9. "SCIENTIFIC (COMMON) NAME" FIELD USED FOR A MAN-MADE OBJECT

The "scientific (common) name" field contains "Maquette Mont Blanc" — the same as the title. This field is structurally designed for natural history specimens (mineral species, fossil taxa). Its application to a man-made terrain model demonstrates how the Adlib catalog system's mineral/fossil object template is being misapplied to objects that do not fit those categories. A terrain model should be cataloged with fields appropriate to three-dimensional scientific instruments or cartographic objects: maker, scale, materials, technique of construction, and provenance.

### 10. IMAGE OBJECT NUMBER DISCREPANCY

The image in the catalog is fetched with the value "F 50001.jpg" rather than "M 12369.jpg," indicating a dual-numbering situation (or numbering inconsistency) in the image management system. The number "50001" appears in Teylers website URLs for this object as well. The catalog database and the image server may be using different identifiers for the same object.
