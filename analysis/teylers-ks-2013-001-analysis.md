## Object Description

A large oil painting on canvas (93 x 137 cm) by **Jacobus van Looy** (Haarlem, 1855 – Haarlem, 1930), depicting a lush garden in full summer bloom. A woman in a hat kneels among nasturtiums, California poppies, and sunflowers. Painted in the summer of **1893** en plein air in the backyard of Van Looy's house on Rustenburgerstraat in Amsterdam's De Pijp neighbourhood, with his wife **Titia van Gelder** as the figure in the garden. Acquired in November 2013 and immediately designated one of Teylers' **50 Topstukken**.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. LOCATION OF THE GARDEN INCORRECTLY IMPLIED (AMSTERDAM, NOT HAARLEM)

The catalog describes Van Looy as a Haarlem artist and the painting is exhibited in a Haarlem museum, likely leading visitors to assume the garden is in Haarlem. **It is not.** The garden depicted is the small backyard at **Rustenburgerstraat, De Pijp, Amsterdam**, where Van Looy and his newly married wife Titia van Gelder lived in 1893. Van Looy did not move back to Haarlem until 1913 (Kleine Houtweg 103). His later Haarlem garden paintings are a separate chapter.

### 2. IDENTITY OF THE WOMAN NOT STATED

The catalog describes a "kneeling woman wearing a hat" but does not identify her. The woman is Van Looy's wife, **Titia van Gelder** (married 28 January 1892). This biographical identification adds significant personal meaning to the work — Van Looy painted his own domestic world in the first full summer after his marriage.

### 3. VAN LOOY'S DUAL CAREER AS PAINTER AND WRITER NOT EXPLAINED

The catalog gives no context for the artist. Van Looy was one of the most exceptional figures in Dutch cultural history:

- **Prix de Rome** winner (shared with Jan Dunselman), 1884 — funded travel through Italy, Spain, and Morocco
- One of the central voices of the **Tachtigers** (Movement of Eighty), the progressive literary movement of the 1880s
- A painter and writer with genuine distinction in both fields — the posthumous **Jacobus van Looy Prize** (awarded every five years since 1985) honours artists who excel at both writing and painting
- His literary masterwork: the autobiographical trilogy **Jaapje** (1917), **Jaap** (1923), **Jakob** (1930) — describing his childhood years in the Haarlem Municipal Orphanage after being orphaned at age five

### 4. PARIS INFLUENCE NOT MENTIONED

Van Looy had just returned from a visit to **Paris** where French Impressionism deeply impressed him when he painted "De Tuin." The painting was his direct response to Monet and the French Impressionist treatment of garden light. One contemporary critic called it "a dream painting, more beautiful than many a work by Monet or Manet." The Vereniging Rembrandt notes it "anticipates modernist approaches to color and light seen in Claude Monet's later work."

### 5. CRITICAL RECEPTION OMITTED

When "De Tuin" was exhibited at **Arti et Amicitiae in 1894**, it was poorly received by critics — but enthusiastically praised by fellow artists. **Isaac Israels** reportedly said: "It's the best painting there is [in Arti]." This reception history is significant.

### 6. THIS IS ONE OF ONLY FIVE LARGE FLOWER PAINTINGS

The catalog provides no rarity context. "De Tuin" (1893) was Van Looy's first large flower painting — he created only **five** such works in total. This makes it the founding work of a small, significant group.

### 7. CALIFORNIA POPPIES MISSING FROM DESCRIPTION

Multiple scholarly sources identify three main flowers in the composition: nasturtiums (*Oost-Indische kers*), **California poppies** (*slaapmutjes / goudpapaver*), and sunflowers. The catalog omits the California poppies.

### 8. "WHITE BUTTERFLIES" — UNVERIFIED DETAIL

No art-historical source consulted mentions butterflies in the painting. Sources consistently describe the nasturtiums, California poppies, and sunflowers. The "white butterflies" detail in the catalog may be an over-interpretation of paint marks or may reflect actual elements that specialists simply do not emphasize. This should be verified against the physical painting.

### 9. STICHTING ROODENBURG VAN LOOY — UNEXPLAINED CO-FUNDER

The credit line names the Stichting Roodenburg van Looy as a co-funder but does not explain what it is. It is a cultural ANBI (public benefit organisation) affiliated with Teylers Museum, which has placed coins, medals, and paintings with Teylers. It is a **separate entity** from the better-known Stichting Jacobus van Looy (founded 1949; dissolved May 2019 after donating ~220 Van Looy paintings and drawings to the municipality of Haarlem).
