## Object Description

This is a standard measuring ruler (standaardmaatlat) made by Benjamin Ayres (active 1731–c. 1775), an English-born instrument maker established in Amsterdam. The ruler is a conversion tool comparing multiple concurrent European measurement systems — English, French, Rhineland, Amsterdam, and Holland measures — in mahogany with brass fittings. Made for or by appointment to the Prince of Orange and the Admiralty of Amsterdam, it represents the chaotic state of European metrology before the adoption of the metric system.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. BENJAMIN AYRES: SIGNIFICANT HISTORICAL FIGURE

Benjamin Ayres (died c. 1775) was far more important than a simple "instrument maker from Amsterdam." He was:

- **English-born**, trained in London
- **Brother-in-law of Jonathan Sisson** (1690–1747), the renowned London instrument maker who invented the modern theodolite. Sisson married Sarah Ayres, Benjamin's sister.
- Moved to **Amsterdam in 1743**, bringing cutting-edge English instrument-making techniques
- **Appointed in 1749** as instrument maker to the Stadtholder and the Admiralty of Amsterdam
- **Dismissed in 1752 due to misconduct**, though his instruments remained in use
- Sold **octants to the VOC** (Dutch East India Company)
- His instruments incorporated technical innovations introduced by Sisson

This biography is entirely absent from Teylers' record. Ayres represents a significant link between English and Dutch precision instrument-making traditions.

### 2. THE PRINCE OF ORANGE IDENTIFIED

The inscription refers to **William IV, Prince of Orange** (1711–1751), the first hereditary Stadtholder of all seven United Provinces (appointed 1747). Ayres was appointed instrument maker in **1749**, during William IV's reign. After William IV's death in 1751, the appointment would have continued under the regency for **William V** (1748–1806). Teylers does not identify which Prince of Orange is referenced.

### 3. THE ADMIRALTY IDENTIFIED

The "Edle Mogend Collegie ter Admiralitijt" was the **Admiraliteit van Amsterdam** — the largest of five Dutch admiralties, responsible for equipping the Republic's naval fleets, managing trade taxes, and overseeing shipbuilding specifications. In 1653, all Dutch Admiralties adopted the **Amsterdam foot** as the standard measurement for naval construction, making accurate conversion rulers essential. Teylers does not identify which Admiralty is meant.

### 5. MEASUREMENT SYSTEMS CONTEXT

The ruler documents six concurrent measurement systems that coexisted in the 18th-century Netherlands:

| System | Modern Equivalent | Usage |
|--------|-------------------|-------|
| English foot | 304.8 mm | Trade with England |
| Amsterdam foot (Amsterdamse voet) | 283.1 mm | Local Amsterdam standard; adopted by all Dutch Admiralties for naval construction (1653) |
| French foot (Pied du Roi) | 324.8 mm | French trade, diplomacy |
| Rhineland foot (Rijnlandse voet) | 314.0 mm | Most widely used Dutch/German foot since 1621 |
| Amsterdam ell (Amsterdamse el) | c. 688 mm | Textile trade |
| 2/3 English yard | c. 610 mm | English textile/trade measure |

The "palm" subdivisions reflect both the Amsterdam palm (c. 94 mm) and the Holland palm (later standardized at 100 mm in 1817 as part of Dutch metrication). This object is a remarkable document of pre-metric European measurement chaos.

### 6. HISTORICAL SIGNIFICANCE UNDERSTATED

The ruler's significance extends well beyond its function as a measuring tool:

- It documents the **pre-metric measurement landscape** of the Netherlands
- It demonstrates the **transversal division technique** — the primary precision technique before the vernier scale, where diagonal lines allow readings finer than main scale divisions
- It represents the intersection of **commerce, navigation, and statecraft**
- It was made obsolete by the metric system adoption (Netherlands, 1799/1817), making it historically valuable
- It connects to the broader story of **Enlightenment-era standardization**

### 9. PRODUCTION DATE COULD BE NARROWED

The date range "c. 1750 – c. 1774" is broad. Given that:
- Ayres was appointed in **1749**
- He was dismissed in **1752**
- The inscription prominently advertises his appointment

The ruler was most likely made **c. 1749–1752**, during the period when the appointment was current and Ayres was actively using this credential. After his dismissal, he likely would not have continued advertising the appointment.
