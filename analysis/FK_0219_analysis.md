## 1. What the Object Is

FK 0219 is a gasometer (gas-measuring and gas-storage apparatus) designed by Martinus van Marum and built by instrument maker Frederik Willem Fries at Teylers Museum, Haarlem, in late 1790 to early 1791. The instrument was designed specifically to demonstrate and investigate the combustion of hydrogen in oxygen, thereby confirming Lavoisier's analytic chemistry principle that water is a compound of hydrogen and oxygen — not an element.

The device is van Marum's deliberate simplification and improvement of Lavoisier's original gasometer design (as published in Lavoisier's *Traité élémentaire de chimie*, 1789). Lavoisier's original gasometer, built by Paris instrument maker Pierre Mégnié and later redesigned by Jean-Baptiste Meusnier, was notoriously expensive and complex, placing it out of reach of most chemists. Van Marum's version was designed to make the water-decomposition/synthesis demonstration affordable and reproducible for scientific institutions worldwide. According to contemporary records, upon its completion van Marum received requests for copies from academies across the world.

The apparatus is constructed of glass, brass (messing), and mahogany (mahonie). The glass components store and measure the gas volumes; brass fittings control gas flow; the mahogany base provides structural support. Physically, it is a late 18th-century precision chemical instrument of significant historical importance, representing the diffusion of Lavoisier's chemical revolution into the Netherlands.

Van Marum described the instrument and related experiments in his 1798 treatise: *"Beschryving van eenige nieuwe of verbeterde chemische Werktuigen behoorende van Teylers Stichting"* (Description of Some New or Perfected Chemical Instruments Belonging to Teyler's Foundation and of Experiments Carried Out with These Instruments), published in Volume 10 of the *Verhandelingen uitgegeven door Teyler's Tweede Genootschap*.

The FK prefix stands for Fysisch Kabinet (Physical Cabinet), the collection founded by van Marum at Teylers Museum.

**Scientific principle demonstrated:** Combustion of hydrogen in oxygen produces water; water is a compound (H₂O), not an element — the cornerstone of Lavoisier's new chemistry and the refutation of phlogiston theory.

## 3. Errors and Omissions

---

### Error 1 — Maker's name is a language-variant inconsistency that risks identity confusion
**Classification: Minor**

The catalog records the instrument maker as **"Frédéric Guillaume Fries"** (French rendering). The standard scholarly name used in the primary literature is **"Frederik Willem Fries"** (Dutch rendering). Both refer to the same individual. The authoritative source — Martin Paul Michael Weiss, *The Masses and the Muses: A History of Teylers Museum in the Nineteenth Century* (Leiden University dissertation, 2013, p. 110, citing "Notulen Tweede Genootschap", 19.11.1790, Haarlem, ATS, vol. 1382) — uses "Frederik Willem Fries" throughout.

The name "Frédéric Guillaume" does not appear in any scholarly literature located during this research. Since Fries was of Swiss origin (not French), the French rendering is not obviously more correct than the Dutch; it is, however, inconsistent with all other primary and secondary sources. The catalog should either standardise on "Frederik Willem Fries" or note both variants with a source.

**Source:** Weiss 2013 dissertation (Chapter III), https://scholarlypublications.universiteitleiden.nl/access/item:2877630/view

---

### Error 2 — Fries's nationality/origin is uncertain and may be wrong
**Classification: Minor**

The catalog lists no biographical information about Fries, but the sole attributed role ("instrumentmaker") implies a known identity. The primary archival source (Notulen Tweede Genootschap, 19.11.1790) recorded Fries as originally from **Strasbourg** (then in the Holy Roman Empire, now Alsace, France). However, Weiss (2013, footnote 57) explicitly states: "The minutes **erroneously** state that Fries was originally from Strasbourg." He is described in the same source as being "of Swiss origin," recruited from London. This contradiction between the archive and the scholarly correction is not reflected anywhere in the catalog, and no authoritative birth record or biographical entry for Fries has been located in any modern database. The catalog should flag this uncertainty.

**Source:** Weiss 2013, Chapter III, fn. 57, https://scholarlypublications.universiteitleiden.nl/access/item:2877630/view

### Error 3 — "Inventor: Antoine Lavoisier" is misleading and technically incorrect for this specific instrument
**Classification: Major**

The catalog assigns Lavoisier the role of **inventor** of this object. This is misleading. Lavoisier invented the *original* gasometer concept (published in his *Traité élémentaire de chimie*, 1789, pp. 346–360; his instrument was built by Pierre Mégnié). The instrument FK 0219 is an explicitly redesigned, simplified, and improved version by van Marum and Fries. The 1952 *Chemisch Weekblad* article on van Marum as chemist (nr. 31, pp. 558–567) states that the gasometer "is constructed in a very ingenious way" and represents van Marum's own design improvements. Weiss (2013, p. 111) confirms van Marum "improved on Lavoisier's gasometer with Fries."

Lavoisier should be credited as the originator of the underlying *concept* (the original gasometer design), not as the inventor of this specific physical object. The current catalog entry may give a reader the impression that FK 0219 *is* a Lavoisier instrument, when it is in fact a Dutch innovation inspired by but distinct from Lavoisier's design. A more accurate role designation would be "inventor of original concept" or "source design," not "inventor."

**Sources:**
- Americanhistory.si.edu Lavoisier Gasometer replica entry: https://americanhistory.si.edu/collections/object/nmah_2603
- Weiss 2013, Chapter III, p. 111
- CHG/KNCV 1952 article: https://chg.kncv.nl/l/library/download/urn:uuid:7f39e9d9-8aa1-4532-9090-05e5f8a34ed2/chw-1952---31--van-marum-als-chemicus--ocr.compressed--1-.pdf

### Error 4 — Object name "verbrandingstoestel" mischaracterises the instrument type
**Classification: Major**

The catalog object name is **"verbrandingstoestel waterstof"** (hydrogen combustion apparatus). This is an imprecise and potentially misleading description. The instrument is primarily a **gasometer** (Dutch: *gazometer*) — a device that stores, measures, and controls the flow of gases for chemical experiments. The Dutch primary literature (1952 *Chemisch Weekblad* article) refers to it explicitly as "de **gazometer** voor de verbranding van waterstof" (the gasometer for the combustion of hydrogen), making clear the instrument type is "gasometer" and its application is hydrogen combustion.

A "verbrandingstoestel" implies a device whose primary purpose is combustion, whereas a gasometer's primary purpose is precise gas measurement and delivery — combustion is the *experiment performed with* the device, not the device's defining character. The scholarly literature on van Marum consistently refers to this as a gasometer. By naming it merely "verbrandingstoestel," the catalog obscures the instrument type, making it harder to compare with related instruments in international museum databases (e.g., the Smithsonian's Lavoisier Gasometer replica, NMAH CH.330605) and misrepresents its technical category in the history of chemistry instrumentation.

**Sources:**
- 1952 Chemisch Weekblad, p. 566: "de reeds genoemde gazometer voor de verbranding van waterstof"
- Weiss 2013, p. 110: "The second device was a gasometer, which enabled a far cheaper demonstration of the decomposition of water"
- NMAH Lavoisier Gasometer: https://americanhistory.si.edu/collections/object/nmah_2603

### Error 5 — Image XMP metadata classifies object as "Fysisch" (Physics) while catalog says "chemische apparatuur" (Chemical apparatus)
**Classification: Minor**

The image file for FK 0219 (JPEG, shot by Martijn Zegel, 2006-11-27) contains XMP metadata (Microsoft Expression Media / iView MediaPro CatalogSets field) that classifies the object under **"Fysisch"** (Physics). The catalog record in the Adlib database classifies it as **"chemische apparatuur"** (chemical apparatus). These are different collection categories. The FK prefix itself stands for *Fysisch Kabinet* (Physical Cabinet), which adds a third layer of ambiguity.

This internal inconsistency suggests either (a) the image was cataloged in a different system with a different classification, or (b) the category was changed at some point in one system without propagating to the other. In practice this instrument straddles physics and chemistry — it is a physical instrument used for chemical demonstrations — but the inconsistency between image metadata and database record should be resolved and documented.

**Source:** XMP metadata embedded in the object image file (expressionmedia:CatalogSets = "Fysisch")

### Omission 6 — No physical dimensions recorded
**Classification: Major**

The catalog record contains **no dimensions** whatsoever — no height, width, depth, or weight. For a museum object of this type, dimensions are essential for:
- Identifying and distinguishing the object from similar instruments
- Planning loans, exhibitions, and storage
- Supporting scholarly comparison with other gasometers (e.g., the Smithsonian replica measures 230 × 71 × 112 cm)
- Verifying the object's identity if records are lost

Physical dimensions are a standard mandatory field in museum cataloging (SPECTRUM, AAT, Dublin Core). Their absence is a significant documentation gap.

---

### Omission 7 — No provenance or acquisition history recorded
**Classification: Major**

The catalog contains **no provenance or acquisition information**. From historical records, the instrument was constructed on commission by van Marum for Teylers Museum in 1790–1791, making Teylers the first owner. Van Marum recruited Fries from London specifically to build this and related instruments. This documented provenance — including the commission, the role of Fries, the laboratory context, and the 1790 introduction of Fries to the Second Society — should be recorded. The complete absence of acquisition data leaves a gap that could complicate future ownership verification or legal due diligence.

**Source:** Weiss 2013, Chapter III, pp. 110–111

---

### Omission 8 — Description is a single sentence with no functional or historical content
**Classification: Major**

The entire catalog description reads: *"Verbrandingstoestel voor waterstof van Martinus van Marum."* This is essentially a restatement of the title plus the designer's name — it adds no information not already present in the structured fields. A proper description should include:

- What the instrument is (gasometer for demonstrating water synthesis/decomposition)
- How it functions (stores and measures hydrogen and oxygen; burns them in a controlled enclosure to demonstrate that their combustion product is water)
- Its scientific significance (demonstrates Lavoisier's theory that water is H₂O, not an element; refutation of phlogiston)
- Its historical context (designed by van Marum to make Lavoisier's expensive experiments accessible; widely copied by academies worldwide)
- Van Marum's motivation (requested by the Académie des Sciences; part of his campaign to introduce Lavoisier's chemistry to the Netherlands)
- Reference to van Marum's 1798 published description of the instrument

The current single-sentence description is functionally useless for research purposes.

**Source:** Weiss 2013; CHG/KNCV 1952 article; van Marum, *Verhandelingen* Vol. 10 (1798)

---

### Omission 9 — No bibliography or reference to van Marum's 1798 published description
**Classification: Major**

Van Marum published a detailed description of this instrument in 1798: *"Beschryving van eenige nieuwe of verbeterde chemische Werktuigen behoorende van Teylers Stichting, en van Proefneemingen met dezelve in't werk gesteld"*, Volume 10 of the *Verhandelingen uitgegeven door Teyler's Tweede Genootschap*. This is the primary published source for the instrument — it is the contemporaneous technical description by the designer himself. It is reprinted in the modern scholarly edition: G. L'E. Turner and T. H. Levere, *Martinus van Marum: Life and Work, Volume IV — Van Marum's Scientific Instruments in Teyler's Museum* (Haarlem: Tjeenk Willink & Zoon, 1967), which catalogued and described all instruments in the collection.

Neither source appears anywhere in the catalog record. This is a major omission: the primary description by the designer and the standard scholarly reference are both absent.

**Sources:**
- van Marum, *Verhandelingen* Vol. 10 (1798): https://play.google.com/store/books/details/Verhandelingen_uitgegeeven_door_Teyler_s_Tweede_Ge?id=CztaAAAAcAAJ
- Turner & Levere 1967: https://www.weberrarebooks.com/product/137855/

### Omission 10 — Van Marum listed as both "designer" and "user" but role distinction not explained
**Classification: Minor**

Van Marum appears twice in the creators field: as **designer** and as **user**. While both attributions are accurate, the catalog does not explain the distinction or its significance. Van Marum designed the instrument in collaboration with Fries (who built it), and he was also the primary experimenter who used it in research and public demonstrations. Without explanatory notes, a catalog user may find the duplication confusing. A brief note clarifying the design vs. use relationship would improve clarity.

---

### Omission 11 — No English-language title
**Classification: Minor**

The catalog provides only a Dutch title ("Verbrandingstoestel voor waterstof") with no English equivalent. Given that Teylers Museum serves an international audience and its website offers an English interface, the absence of an English title ("Hydrogen combustion apparatus" or, more accurately, "Gasometer for the combustion of hydrogen" / "Water synthesis gasometer") limits discoverability and international cross-referencing. The comparable instrument at the Smithsonian (NMAH) is cataloged in English as "Lavoisier Gazometer."

---

### Omission 12 — No condition statement
**Classification: Minor**

No condition information is recorded. For a 230-year-old instrument composed of glass, brass, and mahogany — all of which are susceptible to deterioration — the absence of a condition record is a documentation gap. It is unknown whether the glass is intact, whether the brass fittings are functional, or whether any components have been replaced or restored.
