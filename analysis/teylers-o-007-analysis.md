## Object Description

A drawing on paper (176 x 303 mm) by **Hendrick Avercamp** (Amsterdam, 1585 – Kampen, 1634), dated c. 1605–1634, depicting a winter landscape scene with figures on ice: four figures around a frozen boat (foreground left), kolf players (center), a man with an axe (right bank), a farmhouse (middle left), and a village in the background. Executed in pencil, pen in black, watercolor wash, and white heightening. Monogrammed "HA." Designated one of Teylers' **50 Topstukken**.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. OBJECT NAME FIELD MISUSED — RECURRING STRUCTURAL ERROR (MAJOR)

The "Object Name" field reads **"penseel in waterverf"** — a technique description, not an object type. This information is already present in the separate "Technique" field. The "Object Name" field should read **"tekening"** (drawing), consistent with the "Object Category" field. This structural error appears across multiple Teylers Topstukken entries and misuses a standard cataloging field.

### 2. "PEN IN ZWART" IS ALMOST CERTAINLY WRONG — SHOULD BE BROWN OR GREY (MAJOR)

The catalog records the pen medium as **"pen in zwart"** (pen in black). Across the entire known corpus of Avercamp's finished watercolour drawings — including works at the Rijksmuseum, Harvard, the Getty, and at Sotheby's — the pen medium is consistently recorded as brown ink or grey ink, never black. The 17th-century standard was iron-gall ink, which today appears brown or grey. "Pen in zwart" is anomalous for Avercamp and almost certainly reflects imprecise visual cataloging rather than technical analysis. If the ink is genuinely black, this would be a rare exception requiring explicit explanation.

### 3. DATE LOWER BOUND "CIRCA 1605" IS UNJUSTIFIED (MAJOR)

The catalog gives the date as **"ca 1605 – voor 1634."** A lower bound of c. 1605 would make this one of Avercamp's very earliest known works — an extraordinary claim with no stated basis. Avercamp was still in training in 1605 (approximately age 20), documented as a pupil of Pieter Isaacs in Amsterdam in 1607. His earliest firmly datable paintings are c. 1608–1609. Comparable finished watercolour drawings in other collections are dated c. 1615–1630. The date "c. 1605" is unjustified and should be reconsidered against the Plomp (1997) catalogue entry no. 19 and comparanda in the Welcker raisonné.

### 4. WELCKER CATALOGUE RAISONNÉ ENTIRELY ABSENT (MAJOR)

The bibliography cites only the 1904 Scholten catalogue and a 1997 Plomp catalogue, without specifying entry numbers for either. The foundational scholarly reference for Avercamp — **C.J. Welcker, *Hendrick Avercamp 1585–1634, bijgenaamd "De Stomme van Kampen," en Barent Avercamp 1612–1679* (Zwolle, 1933; revised 1979)** — is entirely absent. The Welcker number is cited as a matter of course in every auction catalogue, major museum record, and scholarly article on Avercamp drawings. Its absence makes the record unusable for scholarly cross-referencing.

### 3. NO PROVENANCE (MAJOR)

The catalog contains no provenance. Three price notations are recorded as inscriptions (f600; f63 - geboden; 40000) but are left entirely uncontextualized. These represent different monetary values from different historical periods and may document consecutive sale prices — potentially important provenance evidence. The old Dutch measurement inscription **"hoog 7 d / br. 12 d"** (height 7 duimen / width 12 duimen) is similarly uninterpreted: these are pre-metric measurements consistent with the dimensions of the work (1 Amsterdam duim ≈ 26 mm: 7 × 26 = 182 mm, 12 × 26 = 312 mm — close to the measured 176 x 303 mm). ICOM and AAM standards require documented provenance for old master drawings of this caliber.

### 4. NO ART-HISTORICAL SIGNIFICANCE STATED (MAJOR)

There is no explanation of why this work is a masterpiece or what it represents in the history of Dutch winter landscape painting. Key context entirely absent:
- Avercamp was the **preeminent Dutch master of the winter landscape** and virtually invented the genre in the early 17th century
- His works document **Little Ice Age conditions** in the Netherlands (the Maunder Minimum period of prolonged cold, c.1645–1715) — a subject of active scholarly and scientific interest
- Avercamp was **mute** ("De Stomme van Kampen") — his relationship to his art and the working community is historically unusual and well-documented
- Whether this work is a **finished independent work** (as the technique and scale suggest) or a preparatory study should be stated

### 5. BIRTHPLACE MISCONCEPTION NOT CLARIFIED (MINOR)

The catalog records **"1585–1634"** without biographical context. Avercamp is popularly associated with Kampen (where he spent most of his adult life), but was **born in Amsterdam**. He moved to Kampen as a child. The catalog should note both Amsterdam (birth) and Kampen (active) to avoid reinforcing the common misconception that he was born in Kampen.

### 6. BIBLIOGRAPHY ENTRIES NOT USABLE AS SCHOLARLY CITATIONS (MINOR)

Both bibliography entries lack author names, publication years, publisher details, and — critically — **specific entry/catalogue numbers** for this work. A bibliography entry reading "Scholten 1904" without the catalogue entry number is not a usable scholarly citation.

### 7. SUBJECT TAG "VISSEN" NOT SUPPORTED BY DESCRIPTION (MINOR)

The subject field includes **"vissen"** (fishing), but the catalog description makes no mention of fishing in the composition. If a figure with fishing equipment is present, it should be described; if not, the tag is unsupported.

### 8. NO EXHIBITION HISTORY (MINOR)

No exhibition history is recorded.

### 9. OLD MEASUREMENT INSCRIPTION NOT INTERPRETED (MINOR)

The inscription **"hoog 7 d / br. 12 d"** (height 7 duimen / width 12 duimen) is recorded but not explained. These are old Dutch duimen measurements corresponding to the physical dimensions of the work. Identifying and interpreting this inscription would assist in understanding the work's early collecting history.

### 10. PRICE NOTATIONS NOT CONTEXTUALIZED (MINOR)

Three price notations (**f600; f63 - geboden; 40000**) are recorded as inscriptions without context. The vast difference in values (63 vs. 600 vs. 40,000) suggests different currencies, periods, or bidding stages. These could document the work's sales history across potentially three centuries and are potentially significant provenance evidence that deserves interpretation.
