## Object Description

A **working scale model of a parabolic solar concentrator coupled to a steam piston mechanism**, manufactured in Paris in 1879 by instrument maker A. Salleron, based on the solar engine technology developed by Augustin Mouchot (1825–1912). The device consists of a silvered parabolic dish (~48 cm diameter) on a mahogany base. A blackened copper boiler is positioned at the focal point; tilting and rotating the dish tracks the sun and concentrates radiation on the boiler. Steam from the heated water drives a piston coupled to a set of wheels, demonstrating the complete conversion of solar radiation into mechanical motion. The museum likely acquired it in 1879 in direct response to the gold-medal demonstration by Mouchot and his assistant Abel Pifre at the **1878 Exposition Universelle in Paris**. This is one of the earliest solar-to-mechanical energy conversion devices in any museum collection worldwide. A comparable model exists in the Musée des Arts et Métiers, Paris (acquired 1880).

## Key Findings

### 1. PIFRE MISLABELED AS "INVENTOR" (uitvinder)

The catalog lists Abel Pifre as *uitvinder* (inventor) alongside Mouchot. This is a factual misattribution. Every credible historical source (Wikipedia, Musée des Arts et Métiers, Cabinet Magazine, academic sources) describes Pifre as Mouchot's **assistant** who later purchased his patents and continued the technology independently. Pifre was not a co-inventor of the solar collector: the parabolic solar collector was invented and patented by Mouchot alone in the 1860s, predating Pifre's involvement entirely. Calling Pifre an "inventor" of this device misrepresents the historical record. The correct role designation is *ingenieur* or *assistent*, not *uitvinder*. Teylers' own public website describes him correctly as Mouchot's assistant.

### 2. DESCRIPTION FIELD IS NOT A DESCRIPTION

The Description field contains only the object title repeated verbatim: *"Model van een zonnecollector met stoomzuiger."* This provides zero information about the object's physical form, components, or operation. Teylers' own public-facing website carries a substantive description: *"A black water caldron is placed in the focus of a silvered dish which can be turned perpendicular to the sun's rays. The collected thermal radiation heats the water to its boiling point. Then the escaping steam can move a piston with coupled wheels."* This content is entirely absent from the Adlib record.

### 3. NO DIMENSIONS RECORDED

No physical dimensions are given for a physical scientific instrument. Secondary sources give the dish diameter as approximately 48 cm. Without dimensions, the object cannot be properly identified or compared with related instruments.

### 4. NO PROVENANCE OR ACQUISITION HISTORY

No acquisition date or source is recorded. It is documented through secondary sources (including Teylers' own public website) that the museum purchased this instrument in 1879, likely in response to the 1878 Exposition Universelle demonstrations. This provenance information is entirely absent from the Adlib record.

### 5. Mouchot's role label is imprecise

Mouchot is listed as *uitvinder* (inventor) — technically defensible but imprecise. Teylers' own website correctly describes the model as made "after Mouchot" (*naar Mouchot*), not "by Mouchot." By 1879 Mouchot had returned to teaching and withdrawn from active development; he did not design or commission this specific scale model.

### 6. "A. Salleron" — manufacturer partially unidentified

"A." is unexplained. Research identifies the firm as that of **Jules Salleron** (Jules B. Salleron, 1829–1897), precision instrument maker at 24, rue Pavée, Paris. The "A." does not correspond to "Jules." The catalog does not clarify whether this is a person's name, a firm name, or a different family member.

### 7. Object name strips the steam engine component

The Object Name field records only *zonnecollector* (solar collector), omitting the steam piston that is integral to the instrument and present in the title. The correct classification is **solar motor** or **solar steam engine model**, not merely a collector.

### 8. 1878 Exposition Universelle context absent

The instrument's reason for existence is the sensation generated by Mouchot and Pifre's gold-medal performance at the 1878 Paris Exposition Universelle. This historical context — central to understanding why the object was made and purchased in 1879 — is wholly absent from the formal record.
