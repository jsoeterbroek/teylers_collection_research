## Object Description

This is a **solar microscope** (*zonnemicroscoop*) — essentially the world's first projector — made by **J. Mortier** in Amsterdam in 1776. A solar microscope uses sunlight reflected through a condensing lens to project hugely magnified images of microscopic specimens onto a wall or screen in a darkened room. A flea could be projected to six feet wide. These instruments were simultaneously scientific tools and public entertainment devices, making microscopy a communal experience for the first time. The 1776 date places this instrument at the intersection of Pieter Teyler van der Hulst's lifetime collecting (he died 1778) and Martinus van Marum's early curatorship, making its provenance historically significant.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. HOW A SOLAR MICROSCOPE WORKS — NOT EXPLAINED

The catalog provides no description of the instrument's function. A solar microscope operates as follows:

1. Mounted in a window shutter or wall opening of a **darkened room**
2. An external adjustable **mirror** reflects sunlight through the instrument (continuously adjusted via an "endless screw" to track the sun)
3. A **condensing lens** concentrates the light at a focal point where a specimen is placed between glass plates
4. A **positive lens** projects a magnified, inverted image onto a distant wall or screen
5. Images could be enlarged **several hundred times** — audiences saw fleas, lice, plant cells, and blood vessels projected to enormous size

### 2. THE SOLAR MICROSCOPE AS THE FIRST PROJECTOR

The catalog does not mention the cultural significance of this instrument. Solar microscopes were **the first projection technology**:

- **Public entertainment**: Instrument makers charged admission for "Solar Microscope Exhibitions" in darkened rooms, projecting specimens to enormous size (1760s onward)
- **Democratization of science**: Before the solar microscope, only one person could look through a microscope at a time. The solar microscope made microscopy a **communal, social experience**
- **Religious framing**: Microscopy was presented as revealing "the Bible of NATURE" — divine complexity visible at the smallest scale
- **Precursor to cinema**: The Museum of Cinema in Girona catalogs solar microscopes as part of pre-cinema history, alongside magic lanterns
- **Scientific education**: Used in schools and universities for teaching anatomy and natural history to entire classes simultaneously

### 3. INVENTION HISTORY

The solar microscope has a layered invention history:

| Date | Inventor | Contribution |
|------|---------|-------------|
| c. 1738 | **Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit** | Integrated mirror and projection device — first practical solar microscope |
| c. 1738–43 | **Johann Nathanael Lieberkuhn** | Demonstrated at the Royal Society in London; widely credited as inventor |
| c. 1740 | **John Cuff** | Crucial improvement: stationary optical tube with movable mirror controlled from inside the room |
| 1774 | **Benjamin Martin** | "Opake Solar Microscope" for viewing opaque specimens |

The instrument remained popular from the 1740s through the mid-19th century, when it was replaced by artificial light sources (limelight, oxyhydrogen).

### 4. J. MORTIER: OBSCURE MAKER

The DWC/KNAW database of Dutch scientific instrument makers lists **"Mortier, J. de"** but provides no biographical details — no dates, no city, no list of instruments. Extensive searching found no additional information about this maker. No other instruments by Mortier were found in other museum databases (Science Museum London, Smithsonian, Whipple Museum).

This makes the Teylers instrument potentially a **unique surviving example** of Mortier's work — a significant fact unmentioned in the catalog. If other Mortier instruments exist, they remain undocumented online.

### 5. POSSIBLE PROVENANCE: PIETER TEYLER'S OWN COLLECTION

The 1776 date raises an intriguing provenance question. **Pieter Teyler van der Hulst** (1702–1778) owned physical instruments in his private collection at his Damstraat house before his death. His 1756 will stipulated these collections should go to the Foundation. After his death on 8 April 1778, the Foundation inherited his instruments.

A solar microscope dated 1776 — two years before Pieter Teyler's death — could have been:
1. **Acquired by Pieter Teyler himself** (the dates fit perfectly)
2. **Acquired by the Tweede Genootschap** (1778–1784)
3. **Acquired by Martinus van Marum** after 1784 from private collection sales

If this instrument belonged to Pieter Teyler personally, it would be one of the few instruments directly traceable to the museum's founder. The catalog makes no mention of provenance.
