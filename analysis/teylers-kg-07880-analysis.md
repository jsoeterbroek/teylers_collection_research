## Object Description

This is a topographical print depicting the Nieuwe Brouwerskolk (New Brewers' Pool) at Overveen, near Haarlem, engraved by Antoine Alexandre Joseph Cardon (1739–1822) after a drawing by Augustijn Claterbos (1750–1828). It is part of a series of views of Haarlem and environs published by "de Compagnie" in Haarlem, c. 1790–1795. This impression is a proof print (proefdruk) on blue paper, making it a particularly rare and special impression.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. TECHNIQUE SIMPLIFIED: Should be "ets en gravure," not just "ets"

Teylers classifies the technique as **"ets" (etching)** only. The Noord-Hollands Archief more accurately describes this print as **"ets en gravure"** (etching and engraving combined). Evidence:

- The inscription **"A. Cardon Sculps."** uses "sculpsit" — the standard term for engraving
- The fine tonal work in the sky, foliage, and figures shows both etched lines (loose, fluid) and engraved lines (precise, controlled)
- Cardon was trained as an engraver and is consistently described as such in biographical sources
- 18th-century topographical prints routinely combined both techniques on a single plate

### 2. PRODUCTION DATE CAN BE SIGNIFICANTLY NARROWED

Teylers gives **c. 1770–1822** — a 52-year range spanning both artists' lifetimes. This can be narrowed to **c. 1790–1795** based on:

- Claterbos joined the Haarlem Guild of St. Luke in **1777** (earliest active date)
- The Beekmans Prenten dealer dates Cardon/Claterbos Haarlem views to **c. 1790**
- A comparable view (Kraantje Lek at Overveen) from the same series is dated **1792**
- The Noord-Hollands Archief dates their impression to **c. 1793**
- The publisher "de Compagnie" appears active c. 1790
- Claterbos's active period ended c. 1806

A date of **c. 1793** is the best specific estimate.

### 3. ANTOINE CARDON: BRUSSELS-BASED, NOT HAARLEM

Cardon (1739–1822) was a **Brussels-born and Brussels-based** painter, portraitist, and engraver:
- Studied painting in Vienna, then worked in Rome (3 years) and Naples
- In Naples, "laid aside the brush for the graver" — transitioning to engraving
- Became professor at the Royal Academy of Fine Arts in Brussels
- Named member of the Royal Institute of the Netherlands by King William I (1822)
- Active Freemason; engraved Masonic diplomas and emblems
- Father of **Anthony Cardon** (1772–1813), also an engraver

He was not based in Haarlem — this was a cross-border commission typical of 18th-century Dutch-Belgian artistic collaboration. This biographical context is absent from the catalog.

### 4. AUGUSTIJN CLATERBOS: HAARLEM LOCAL ARTIST

Claterbos (born 22 November 1750, Haarlem — died 14 May 1828, Haarlem) spent his entire career in Haarlem:
- Member of the Haarlem Guild of St. Luke from 1777
- Also worked as a **wijnroeier** (wine tax collector) — a dual occupation
- Painted portraits (including pastels), landscapes, and topographical views
- Buried in the Sint Bavokerk, Haarlem
- A monograph by Bert Sliggers of Teylers Museum was published in 1990: *Augustijn Claterbos, 1750–1828* (64 pp.)

His identity as a Haarlem artist who also served a civic function adds local historical depth absent from the catalog.

### 5. THE NIEUWE BROUWERSKOLK: HISTORICALLY SIGNIFICANT SITE

The Brouwerskolk's history connects to Haarlem's once-thriving beer brewing industry:

- Haarlem was a major brewing center; as the Spaarne river became too polluted, brewers needed clean dune water
- In **1646**, the brewers' guild purchased land at the end of the Rampenvaart and dug the **Nieuwe Brouwerskolk** further west to access fresh groundwater from the dune aquifer
- A sluice system channeled clean water to Haarlem breweries via water ships
- Conflict with linen bleacheries (blekerijen), which also used dune water but polluted it, was a recurring issue
- By the late 18th century, the site had become a popular scenic destination
- Multiple artists depicted it: Claterbos (c. 1793), Frans Milatz (1804), Wybrand Hendriks (1821), Leendert Overbeek (1792)
- The site is now part of **Nationaal Park Zuid-Kennemerland**

This local historical context would greatly enrich the catalog record.

### 6. "PROEFDRUK" AND BLUE PAPER: RARE COMBINATION

The print's proof status and blue paper together indicate a particularly **special and rare impression**:

- **Proefdruk** (proof): An impression pulled before the regular edition, typically in very limited numbers
- **Blue paper**: Made with indigo dye; scarcer and more expensive than white paper. The tinted ground creates a middle tone, enhancing the print's tonal range
- Blue paper was sometimes used specifically for proof impressions to distinguish them from the regular white-paper edition
- The combination suggests this was a deliberately privileged impression — possibly for the artist, the publisher, or a patron

### 7. BILINGUAL INSCRIPTIONS: MARKET CONTEXT

The Dutch/French bilingual captions ("GEZICHT aan de NIEUWE BROUWERS-KOLK te OVERVEEN" / "VUE de l'endroit, dit le NIEUWE BROUWERS-KOLK, à OVERVEEN") reflect:
- The cultural bilingualism of late 18th-century Dutch elite society
- An intention to reach international (particularly French-speaking) collectors
- Standard practice for higher-end topographical print series in this period

### 8. SERIES CONTEXT

This print belongs to a **series of topographical views of Haarlem and environs** by Cardon after Claterbos, published by "de Compagnie" in Haarlem. Known subjects include:
- Views from the Noorder Spaarne
- Views from the Zuider Spaarne
- Kraantje Lek at Overveen (1792)
- The Nieuwe Brouwerskolk (this print)

The series context is not mentioned in Teylers' catalog.

### 9. CROSS-REFERENCE: NOORD-HOLLANDS ARCHIEF

The Noord-Hollands Archief in Haarlem holds another impression of this print, cataloged as "Nieuwe Brouwerskolk 1," described as "topogr. prent: ets en gravure op blauwig papier" (topographical print: etching and engraving on bluish paper), 255×360 mm. Available via Europeana.
