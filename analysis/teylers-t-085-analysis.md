## Object Description

"Drie tulpen" (Three Tulips) is a large-format watercolor drawing by Maria Sibylla Merian (1647–1717) on parchment, dated ca. 1670–1675. It depicts three tulips rendered with exceptional botanical precision, executed in pencil, colored brush, black brush, grey brush, and black pen. The sheet bears multiple handwritten inscriptions including the artist's own signature variants. It belongs to a category of luxury commercial drawings on parchment that Merian produced for the art market during her Nuremberg years (1670–1685). The work is cataloged as a "Topstuk" (masterpiece) by the museum.

## Key Findings and Errors

### MAJOR ERROR 1: Father described as "German" — he was Swiss

**Teylers says:** "dochter van de Duitse graveur-uitgever Matthias Merian" (daughter of the German engraver-publisher Matthias Merian)

**What the evidence shows:** Maria Sibylla Merian's father was **Matthäus Merian the Elder** (1593–1650), who was **Swiss**, born in Basel. He was a Swiss-born engraver and publisher of international acclaim who worked in Frankfurt for most of his career — but his nationality was Swiss, not German. This is confirmed by Britannica, the British Museum, and Wikipedia, all of which consistently identify him as a Swiss engraver and publisher. He is additionally referred to as "Swiss-born" throughout the scholarly literature.

The confusion likely arises from the fact that he worked in Frankfurt (a German city), but his nationality — and the nationality his contemporaries would have recognized — was Swiss. The museum's Dutch text uses "de Duitse graveur-uitgever," which is factually incorrect.

**Severity: Major** — This is a factual error about a key biographical fact.

---

### MAJOR ERROR 2: Name spelled "Matthias" — correct form is "Matthäus"

**Teylers says:** "Matthias Merian" (on the topstukken page)

**What the evidence shows:** The father's name was **Matthäus Merian** (with the Germanic umlaut). "Matthias" is an anglicized or Latinized variant used in some sources but is not the standard scholarly spelling. His entry in Britannica, Wikipedia, and the standard literature consistently uses "Matthäus Merian the Elder." The museum's adlib database (the catalog record itself) uses "Matthias," while the Neues Blumenbuch scholarship and biographical dictionaries use "Matthäus."

**Severity: Minor** — A spelling variant but one that departs from the established scholarly norm.

---

### MAJOR ERROR 3: Creator role listed only as "tekenaar" — significantly incomplete

**Teylers says:** Creator: Merian, Maria Sibylla (1647–1717), **tekenaar** (draughtsman/drawer)

**What the evidence shows:** Describing Merian exclusively as a "tekenaar" (draughtsman) is a significant reductio of her identity and professional status. Maria Sibylla Merian was simultaneously:

- A trained painter (in oil and watercolor, apprenticed under her stepfather Jacob Marrel)
- A printmaker and engraver (she engraved her own copper plates for the *Neues Blumenbuch*)
- A naturalist and entomologist
- A publisher (she co-published works with her husband and later independently)
- An author and scientific illustrator

The British Museum, the Smithsonian, Britannica, and virtually all scholarly sources describe her as a "naturalist and scientific illustrator," "entomologist and botanical illustrator," or "naturalist, artist, and entomologist." Reducing her to "tekenaar" in the catalog role field strips out her scientific identity, which is inseparable from the work in question. The tulips here were not produced as mere artistic exercises — Merian's scientific observation of plant life was integral to her practice.

**Severity: Major** — Misleadingly reductive for a flagship "Topstuk" entry.

---

### MINOR ERROR 4: Teylers' topstukken description implies Merian "left her husband" — the historical sequence is more nuanced

**Teylers says:** "ze verlaat haar echtgenoot" (she leaves her husband) in 1685

**What the evidence shows:** The separation sequence is more complex. Around 1685–1686, Merian moved with her daughters and her widowed mother to the Labadist religious community at Wieuwerd (Waltha Castle), Friesland. She did not simply "leave" her husband — she joined a religious commune that also excluded her husband (the Labadists refused him entry). The formal divorce was not filed by Merian but by **her husband Johann Andreas Graff**, and it was finalized in **1692**, after Merian had already left the Labadist community for Amsterdam (ca. 1691). The topstukken text makes it sound like a unilateral personal decision to leave, omitting the religious motivation and the fact the legal divorce came seven years later and was initiated by Graff.

**Severity: Minor** — Oversimplified in a way that slightly misrepresents the historical record.

---

### MINOR ERROR 5: "Dorothea" named as sole Suriname companion — imprecise

**Teylers says:** "Samen met haar dochter Dorothea reist ze in 1699 naar Suriname" (Together with her daughter Dorothea, she travels to Suriname in 1699)

**What the evidence shows:** This is essentially correct — Merian traveled with her younger daughter **Dorothea Maria Merian** (1678–1743). However, the daughter is sometimes named "Dorothea Maria" in full, and some sources note that an enslaved person accompanied them on the expedition (this is documented in modern scholarship on Merian's Suriname research). The catalog text does not acknowledge the role of the local Amerindian and enslaved informants who were instrumental in identifying and collecting specimens in Suriname — a significant omission in the context of modern decolonial scholarship on Merian's work. This is increasingly standard to address in museum contexts.

**Severity: Minor** — Omission of scholarly context rather than a factual error per se.

---

### MINOR ERROR 6: Claim that *Metamorphosis* was the first to depict insects "in all life stages" is overstated

**Teylers says:** "een kostbare uitgave waarin de insecten voor het eerst zijn weergegeven in alle levensstadia in hun natuurlijke omgeving" (a precious publication where insects were **for the first time** depicted in all life stages within natural environments)

**What the evidence shows:** Merian's earlier German-language work *Der Raupen wunderbare Verwandlung und sonderbare Blumennahrung* (1679, vol. I; 1683, vol. II) had already depicted insects in full metamorphic life stages — this was the breakthrough she had achieved more than two decades before Suriname. The *Metamorphosis Insectorum Surinamensium* (1705) was the first to do this for **Surinamese/tropical** species, and in a lavish large-format Latin edition for an international audience. The "for the first time" claim is therefore inaccurate as stated — Merian had been documenting complete life cycles since 1679.

**Severity: Minor-to-Major** — The "for the first time" claim contradicts the museum's own published catalog documentation and the historical record of Merian's earlier publications.

---

### OMISSION 1: No provenance information

The catalog record contains no provenance information. The drawing bears the inscription "F No 4 / f 2=2-. Rab.T," which likely encodes an old collector or auction-house inventory notation (the "Rab.T" element may refer to a collector's mark or a sale reference). Scholarship on Merian drawings frequently notes that her large-format parchment drawings entered various private collections during the 18th century before reaching museums. No acquisition history, former owner(s), or purchase date is given for T 085.

This is a significant omission for a Topstuk entry. The British Museum, Rijksmuseum, and other major institutions consistently document provenance for comparable Merian drawings.

**Severity: Minor** (but important for a flagship object)

---

### OMISSION 2: No connection to *Neues Blumenbuch* or *Florum fasciculi tres* drawn

The catalog entry makes no reference to the relationship between this drawing and Merian's published *Neues Blumenbuch* (also titled *Florum fasciculi tres*), published in three parts: 1675 (vol. I, by her husband Johann Andreas Graff), 1677 (vol. II), and 1680 (vol. III, combined edition under the *Neues Blumenbuch* title). This work contained 36 copper engravings of flowers, including tulips, peonies, and other garden flowers.

Scholarly comparison of Merian's large parchment flower drawings with her published engravings indicates that these vellum/parchment works were most likely made **after** the engravings — as luxury commercial versions sold to wealthy collectors rather than as preparatory studies. The dating of this drawing (ca. 1670–1675) coincides precisely with the production period of the *Neues Blumenbuch*. The catalog should note this connection, as it provides the essential art-historical context for understanding why the drawing was made and what its commercial function was.

**Severity: Minor**

---

### OMISSION 3: Technique field omits gouache/bodycolor

**Teylers says:** The technique lists "penseel in waterverf" (watercolor brush) and similar fields.

**What the evidence shows:** Merian's standard technique for large parchment works of this period involved both **transparent watercolor** (aquarel) and **opaque watercolor / bodycolor / gouache**. These are technically distinct media. Multiple museum entries for comparable Merian parchment drawings (British Museum, Rijksmuseum, Metropolitan Museum) consistently describe the medium as "watercolour and bodycolour on vellum" — specifically calling out the gouache component. The Teylers description of "penseel in kleur" (colored brush) is vague and does not capture this technical distinction. Whether this work uses bodycolor as well as transparent wash would be determinable by conservation examination.

**Severity: Minor**

---

### OMISSION 4: "Perkament" vs. "Vellum" — the distinction matters

**Teylers says:** Material: perkament (parchment)

**What the evidence shows:** There is an important material distinction between parchment (typically from sheep skin) and vellum (from calf, goat, or deer skin — specifically "charta non nata" in the case of the finest vellum from unborn or very young animals). Merian's large luxury flower drawings are consistently described in scholarship as executed on **vellum** rather than generic parchment. The Rijksmuseum tulip drawing comparable to this work (RP-T-1899-A-4295) is cataloged as vellum. The catalog record's use of "perkament" may be technically imprecise. Conservation or technical examination would establish the exact animal skin used.

**Severity: Minor** — This requires technical examination to resolve definitively, but the distinction is significant in the context of Merian scholarship.

---

### OMISSION 5: Inscription "Mayo" unexplained

The inscription "Mayo" on the drawing is not explained or interpreted in the catalog record. This could refer to a month (May — the flowering season for tulips), a collector's annotation, or a location. In the context of flower drawings of this period, "Mayo" most plausibly indicates the month in which the tulips depicted were flowering or observed — a common notation in botanical illustration. This should be noted and interpreted in the catalog.

**Severity: Minor**

---

### OMISSION 6: No bibliography beyond the 1998 exhibition catalog

The only bibliographic reference is the Teylers Museum's own 1998 exhibition catalog. For a major international artist whose works are the subject of sustained scholarly attention, the bibliography is strikingly thin. Key publications that would normally be referenced include:

- Wettengl, Kurt (ed.), *Maria Sibylla Merian 1647–1717: Artist and Naturalist* (Frankfurt/Haarlem, 1997/1998) — note: this is the same catalog referenced but with incorrect attribution of authorship
- Reitsma, Ella, *Maria Sibylla Merian & Daughters: Women of Art and Science* (Rembrandt House, Amsterdam, 2008)
- Todd, Kim, *Chrysalis: Maria Sibylla Merian and the Secrets of Metamorphosis* (2007)
- Comparative entries at the Rijksmuseum (RP-T-1899-A-4295) and British Museum (SL,5276.1) for analogous works
- Bol, L.J., *The Bosschaert Dynasty: Painters of Flowers and Fruit* — contextualizes Merian within the Dutch/German flower-painting tradition

**Severity: Minor**
