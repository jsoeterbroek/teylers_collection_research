## Object Description

This is a set of 18th-century compound pulley blocks (*katrollen*) made by the Amsterdam instrument makers **J. Kampman and Jean Pelletier**, used to demonstrate mechanical advantage through block-and-tackle pulley systems. The apparatus retains its **original cord** — a rare survival of organic material on an 18th-century scientific instrument. It is one of two pulley demonstrations by Kampman & Pelletier in the Teylers collection (the other being FK 0018, a pulley stand), and forms part of a larger suite of Newtonian mechanics apparatus based on designs by **Willem Jacob 's Gravesande** (1688–1742).

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. PELLETIER IDENTITY: POTENTIAL CATALOG ERROR

The catalog lists the maker as "Jean Pelletier (1736–?)" with an unknown death year. Research reveals a strong candidate match:

**Jean C. Pelletier** (1736–1780) was a French-born engraver and printmaker active in Amsterdam, documented by the National Gallery of Art, the Metropolitan Museum, and Wikidata (Q43624413). His dates (born 1736), initials (J.C. / Jean), and Amsterdam location all match. Both professions — engraving and instrument making — involve precision metalwork with the burin.

Meanwhile, FK 0018 lists the same maker as "**J.C. Pelletier**" (not "Jean Pelletier"). The DWC/KNAW database lists "Pelletier, J.C." as a scientific instrument maker.

**If these are the same person:**
- The death year is known: **1780**, not "?"
- The Kampman–Pelletier partnership could only have been active **1775–1780** (5 years, not 24)
- Both FK 0018 and FK 0020 would date to **c. 1775–1780**, significantly narrower than the catalog's 1775–1799
- The catalog should note his dual career as engraver and instrument maker

**The naming inconsistency** between FK 0018 ("J.C. Pelletier") and FK 0020 ("Jean Pelletier (1736–?)") is itself an error — the same maker should be listed consistently.

### 2. DIFFERENCE BETWEEN FK 0018 AND FK 0020

The catalog gives both instruments the identical description ("Toestel ter demonstratie van de werking van de katrol") without explaining how they differ. The Dutch terminology reveals the distinction:

| | FK 0018 | FK 0020 |
|---|---|---|
| Title | Statief met twee **poelies** | **Katrollen** |
| Form | Fixed stand with two pulley wheels | Set of pulley blocks |
| Materials | Mahogany, brass | Mahogany, brass, **cord** |
| Function | Single fixed/movable pulley demonstrations | Compound block-and-tackle demonstrations |

- **"Poelie"** = individual pulley wheel/sheave
- **"Katrol"** = complete pulley assembly or block

FK 0018 demonstrates basic pulley principles (force redirection, simple mechanical advantage). FK 0020 demonstrates compound systems (block and tackle, increasing mechanical advantage with multiple pulleys). This is the standard pedagogical progression in 's Gravesande's mechanics curriculum.

### 4. ORIGINAL CORD: PRESERVATION-SIGNIFICANT

FK 0020 lists "cord" as a material — FK 0018 does not. If this cord is original (c. 1775–1780/1799), it is approximately **230–250 years old**. This is notable because:

- Natural fiber ropes (hemp was standard in 18th-century Netherlands) deteriorate far faster than brass and mahogany
- Most surviving 18th-century pulley apparatus in museums have **replacement cord**
- Original cord provides material-culture evidence: fiber type, diameter, twist direction, and construction technique
- Hemp rope was a major Dutch industry — the Netherlands was Europe's leading rope producer in the 18th century

The catalog does not note whether the cord is original or a replacement.

### 5. PHYSICAL PRINCIPLES NOT DESCRIBED

The catalog says only "demonstratie van de werking van de katrol." A compound pulley set demonstrates:

- **Increasing mechanical advantage**: each additional pulley in a block-and-tackle system multiplies the force advantage
- **The "golden rule of mechanics"**: force gained equals distance lost — pulling more rope through the system lifts the weight less distance
- **Equilibrium and work**: the conservation principle that work input equals work output (minus friction)
- **Practical applications**: cranes, hoists, ship rigging — directly relevant to 18th-century Dutch maritime technology

### 6. 'S GRAVESANDE'S PULLEY CLASSIFICATIONS

's Gravesande systematically distinguished pulley types in *Physices Elementa Mathematica* (1720):
- **Trochlea fixa** (fixed pulley): redirects force without mechanical advantage
- **Trochlea mobilis** (movable pulley): provides 2× mechanical advantage
- **Polyspastos** (compound/block and tackle): combines fixed and movable pulleys for increasing advantage

FK 0020 ("Katrollen") corresponds to the polyspastos demonstrations. The 127 copper plates in the 1742 third edition served as blueprints for instrument makers including the Kampman workshop.

### 7. DATING AND PROVENANCE

If Pelletier died in 1780, the instrument dates to **c. 1775–1780**. **Martinus van Marum** became Teylers director in 1784 and acquired instruments from "private collection sales in Holland in the late 1780s." If so, FK 0020 was likely purchased secondhand — possibly from the Kampman workshop's remaining stock or from a collector who had previously acquired it.

The definitive provenance reference is **Turner & Levere, *Martinus van Marum: Life and Work, Volume IV: Van Marum's Scientific Instruments in Teyler's Museum*** (Leiden: Noordhoff International, 1973).
