## Object Description

M 01518 is a large cluster of quartz crystals (rock crystal, i.e. colourless transparent quartz) from the Dauphiné region of the French Alps, south of Grenoble. The specimen weighs 6,900 grams and was acquired around 1802. The photograph shows a dense, multi-directional cluster of elongated prismatic crystals with pyramidal terminations, predominantly colourless to pale cream-yellow in tone, with some brownish iron-staining at the crystal bases. The crystals display the characteristic hexagonal prism faces and rhombohedral pyramid faces typical of alpha-quartz. No matrix rock is visible; the cluster appears to be a free-standing druse.

The Dauphiné region (now part of Isère, Drôme and Hautes-Alpes departments) has been one of the world's premier localities for rock crystal since at least the 17th century. The Oisans massif, particularly the La Gardette mine near Bourg-d'Oisans, produced some of the finest quartz specimens in European collections. Alpine crystal hunters ("cristalliers") worked these deposits commercially, supplying Parisian, Genevan and Milanese gem cutters as well as collectors. The term "cristal de roche" — literally "rock crystal" — was the standard French name for colourless quartz in 18th-century natural history literature and trade.

The specimen dates to the period when Teylers director Martinus van Marum was actively building the mineral cabinet. The production date of "circa 1802" in the catalog most likely refers to the date of acquisition by Teylers rather than any date of formation or collection in the field. Quartz is of course a geological mineral millions of years in the making, making the phrase "production date" categorically inappropriate for a natural specimen.

## Key Findings

### 1. SCIENTIFIC NAME IS OBSOLETE AND IMPRECISE

The catalog lists "Cristal de roche" as the scientific (common) name. This is an 18th-century French trade name, not a scientific mineral name. The IMA-approved (International Mineralogical Association) mineral name is **Quartz**, with the chemical formula **SiO₂**. The variety name for colourless transparent quartz is "rock crystal," which corresponds to the Dutch "bergkristal" used in the title — so the title is actually more informative than the "scientific name" field.

No chemical formula, crystal system, or IMA status is given anywhere in the catalog record. For a mineral catalog, these are fundamental omissions. The correct data would be: formula SiO₂; crystal system trigonal (hexagonal in high-temperature β-quartz); space group P3₁21 or P3₂21; Mohs hardness 7; specific gravity 2.65.

### 2. "PRODUCTION DATE" FIELD IS CATEGORICALLY WRONG FOR A MINERAL

The catalog lists "Production date: circa 1802" and "Production period: begin 19e eeuw (verzameld)." The parenthetical "(verzameld)" (collected) indicates someone was aware this is anomalous. However, quartz crystals do not have a "production date." The appropriate fields would be:

- **Acquisition date:** circa 1802
- **Geological age:** Cenozoic (Alpine orogeny), broadly Miocene–Pliocene for Alpine fissure quartz
- **Date collected:** unknown

Using "production date" for a natural specimen conflates the museum acquisition date with a date of manufacture, which is appropriate only for man-made objects. This is a systemic cataloging error affecting the entire mineral collection.

### 3. LOCALITY IS INSUFFICIENTLY PRECISE

The catalog lists "Dauphine, S of Grenoble : Frankrijk" (Dauphiné, south of Grenoble, France). While geographically correct at a provincial level, this is insufficient for mineralogical reference. The Dauphiné encompasses a large area. Known historic quartz localities include:

- **La Gardette mine**, Villard-Notre-Dame, Bourg-d'Oisans, Isère — considered the finest Alpine quartz locality
- **Les Deux-Alpes, Oisans, Isère** — another classic locality
- **Various Alpine fissure deposits** in the Belledonne and Écrins massifs

Without specifying the mine or commune, the provenance cannot be verified or cross-referenced in mineralogical databases. The standard format for localities in international databases (e.g., Mindat) would be: [mine name], [commune], [département], Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes, France.

### 5. NO PROVENANCE OR ACQUISITION INFORMATION

There is no record of how this specimen came to Teylers — whether purchased directly, received as a gift, or part of a named collection. Van Marum was building the mineral cabinet actively around 1802; knowing the source collection or dealer would be significant for provenance research. Several major mineral collections entered Teylers around this period, including portions of the Gmelin collection. This link is absent.

### 6. NO BIBLIOGRAPHY OR PUBLISHED REFERENCES

The catalog contains no references to publications in which this specimen may have been described, illustrated, or used as a reference specimen. Given the age and quality of the collection, there may well be references in 19th-century mineralogical literature.

### 7. TWINNING NOT NOTED

Quartz from the Dauphiné region is especially famous for displaying **Dauphiné-law twinning** (a rotational twin about the c-axis), which is named specifically after specimens from this region. The image shows what appears to be a complex cluster, but no observation about twinning is recorded. Dauphiné-law twins are indistinguishable in external habit but detectable by etching or optical methods. This is a scientifically significant characteristic of Dauphiné quartz specimens that is entirely absent from the catalog.
