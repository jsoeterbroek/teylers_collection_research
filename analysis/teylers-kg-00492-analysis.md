## Object Description

A copper engraving by **Albrecht Dürer** (Nuremberg, 1471 – Nuremberg, 1528), dated 1504, depicting Adam and Eve in the Garden of Eden. Bartsch 1, Meder 1 — the most prestigious numbering in both major Dürer catalogues. One of the foundational works of Northern Renaissance art, the engraving is the first known depiction of Adam and Eve that prioritized **ideal classical beauty** over the narrative of sin. Dürer modelled Adam on the Apollo Belvedere and Eve on the Medici Venus, deploying contrapposto to demonstrate perfect human proportions. More preparatory drawings survive for this print than for any other Dürer engraving. The four animals in the scene — cat, rabbit, ox, and elk — represent the four **humoral temperaments** in pre-lapsarian harmony.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. IMAGE DIMENSIONS — LIKELY LISTED IN WRONG ORDER (MAJOR ERROR)

The catalog gives image dimensions as "192 x 247 mm." This implies a **landscape-format (wider than tall) print** — which is **demonstrably wrong** for this composition. *Adam and Eva* is a tall, vertical (portrait-format) print. Measurements from major collections are consistent:

- Rijksmuseum: **251 x 192 mm** (height x width)
- Metropolitan Museum: **251 x 200 mm**
- Art Institute of Chicago: **250 x 193 mm**
- Morgan Library: **250 x 192 mm** (platemark)

The Teylers catalog entry of "192 x 247 mm" almost certainly lists **width first, then height** — the reverse of the standard convention. Corrected, the dimensions would be **247 x 192 mm**, fully consistent with all other institutions. This is a **systematic notation error**, not an anomalous impression.

### 2. "AD" BEFORE MONOGRAM — REDUNDANT IN TRANSCRIPTION

The inscription transcription reads "ALBERT/ DURER/ NORICUS/ FACIEBAT/ AD [monogram] 1504." The "AD" is Dürer's famous **monogram** (the letter A with D inscribed within it) — it is not a separate text element "AD." Standard transcription reads: *ALBERTVS DVRER NORICVS FACIEBAT [monogram] 1504*. Listing "AD" as a separate element before the monogram is slightly misleading.

### 3. "FACIEBAT" SIGNIFICANCE NOT GLOSSED

The inscription includes "FACIEBAT" (imperfect tense: "was making") rather than "FECIT" (past tense: "made"). This is a direct allusion to **Pliny the Elder's** account that the greatest Greek painters (Apelles, Polyclitus) signed works with the imperfect tense as a gesture of humility — implying that even finished masterpieces are never truly complete. Michelangelo later used the same convention on his Pietà. Dürer's deliberate choice of this classical form signals his ambition to be regarded as a humanist artist in the Italian mold. This is a key art-historical point that goes completely unmentioned.

### 4. FOUR TEMPERAMENT ANIMALS NOT IDENTIFIED

The catalog lists "cat, rabbit, ox, deer" as subjects but provides no explanation. These four animals represent the **four humoral temperaments** of medieval medicine:

- **Cat** = choleric
- **Rabbit** = sanguine
- **Ox** = phlegmatic
- **Elk/Deer** = melancholic (Dürer's own self-identified temperament)

Before the Fall, the four humors were in perfect balance within the human body — symbolised by the animals' peaceful coexistence (notably: the cat does not pounce on the mouse at its feet). The Fall destroys this balance, introducing disease and temperamental imbalance. The catalog's bare listing of animals misses the entire iconographic programme.

### 5. "BIRD" SHOULD BE "PARROT"

The subject list includes "bird." The bird depicted is specifically a **parrot**, and its presence is iconographically significant: variously interpreted as a herald of paradise, a symbol of wisdom, a New World association (parrots were exotic animals associated with the recently discovered Americas), or a herald of the Virgin Mary (the "new Eve"). The generic term "bird" loses this specificity.

### 6. GOAT MISSING FROM SUBJECT LIST

The composition includes a **goat** (upper right), representing the biblical scapegoat. It is absent from the Teylers subject list.

### 7. "? FvH" PROVENANCE MARK — UNIDENTIFIED

The catalog records "? FvH" as a provenance mark, with the question mark indicating that even Teylers' own catalogers are uncertain of the identification. The definitive reference — Frits Lugt, *Les marques de collections de dessins & d'estampes* (1921, expanded editions) — was not consulted. Without checking Lugt, the collector associated with this mark cannot be identified. This unresolved mark represents a gap in the ownership history.

### 8. BARTSCH/MEDER REFERENCES NOT CITED

The catalog cites no standard print reference catalogues. For a Dürer engraving, the following should be cited:

- **Bartsch 1** (Le Peintre Graveur, vol. VII, p. 30, no. 1)
- **Meder 1** (Joseph Meder, Dürer-Katalog, Vienna, 1932)
- **Hollstein** German VII.5.1
- **Schoch/Mende/Scherbaum** no. 39

### 9. STATE/IMPRESSION QUALITY NOT INDICATED

The catalog does not indicate whether this is an early or late impression. *Adam and Eva* was printed in multiple states (Meder catalogued at least three). Early impressions show exceptional tonal range, fine line work, and details such as the mouse's whiskers clearly. Later impressions from the worn plate show degraded fine lines and flattened contrasts. This distinction significantly affects both scholarship and market value (early impressions can fetch GBP 150,000–250,000+).
