## Object Description

The complete first edition of the **Encyclopédie, ou Dictionnaire raisonné des sciences, des arts et des métiers**, edited by **Denis Diderot** (Langres, 1713 – Paris, 1784) and initially co-edited by **Jean le Rond d'Alembert** (Paris, 1717 – Paris, 1783), published in Paris and Amsterdam, 1751–1780. The complete first edition comprises **35 folio volumes**: 17 text volumes + 11 plates volumes + 4 supplement text volumes + 1 supplement plates volume + 2 analytical index volumes (*Table analytique*). Contains 71,818 articles and 3,129 plates, with more than 130 contributors. The work was condemned by the Parlement de Paris (1759) and placed on the Catholic Church's *Index Librorum Prohibitorum* by Pope Clement XIII (1759), forcing the completion of the text volumes in secret. Acquired by Teylers **in 1780** — four years before the museum's formal opening in 1784, making it one of the earliest documented acquisitions in the collection. Designated one of Teylers' **50 Topstukken** and central to the 2026 Teylers exhibition **"Gevaarlijke Boeken."**

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. D'ALEMBERT LISTED AS CO-EDITOR FOR FULL 1751–1780 RUN — WRONG (MAJOR)

The catalog lists both Diderot and d'Alembert as editors against the full publication run of 1751–1780. D'Alembert **resigned as co-editor at the beginning of 1758**, following the controversy over his article "Genève" in volume VII. He had no editorial role for volumes 8–17 (published 1765) or the Supplement volumes (1776–1780). Diderot was the sole editor for the remaining 27 years of the project. The catalog record as structured implies joint editorship throughout — a factual misrepresentation of one of the most historically significant editorial partnerships in European intellectual history.

### 2. FALSE "NEUCHÂTEL" IMPRINT NOT RECORDED — CENSORSHIP HISTORY ABSENT (MAJOR)

The catalog records publication places as **"Parijs, Amsterdam."** This omits a historically defining fact: volumes 8–17 of the text (published 1765) bear the false imprint **"A Neufchâtel, chez Samuel Fauche et Compagnie"** — they were actually printed in Paris but used the false Neuchâtel address to evade the **1759 condemnation by the Parlement de Paris** (which banned the work and revoked the publishing privilege). Erasure of the Neuchâtel imprint strips out the censorship context that is fundamental to the work's identity. Teylers itself made the censorship history the central theme of its own 2026 "Gevaarlijke Boeken" exhibition — yet the catalog record contains no trace of it.

### 3. D'ALEMBERT'S NAME INCORRECT IN TWO RESPECTS (MINOR)

The catalog records the co-editor as **"Jean Baptiste Le Rond d' Alembert"** — wrong in two ways:
- **Spurious space**: "d' Alembert" should be "d'Alembert" (no space)
- **Wrong forename**: The correct scholarly form (Britannica, Wikipedia, ARTFL) is **"Jean le Rond d'Alembert"** — not "Jean Baptiste Le Rond." "Baptiste" is an erroneous variant from some early biographical traditions and is not the standard scholarly name.

### 4. SUPPLEMENT PUBLISHER "BRUNET" OMITTED (MINOR)

For the Supplement volumes (1776–1777), the Paris publishers were Panckoucke, Stoupe, **and Brunet** (libraire, rue des Écrivains). The catalog records only Panckoucke and Stoupe. Brunet's omission is confirmed by French-language Wikipedia's *Supplément à l'Encyclopédie* article and BnF bibliographic records.

### 5. NO PROVENANCE — ACQUISITION IN 1780 NOT RECORDED (MAJOR)

The catalog contains no provenance. Teylers' own 2026 exhibition text ("Gevaarlijke Boeken") states: *"De Encyclopédie van Diderot en d'Alembert werd overigens al gekocht in 1780, vier jaar voor de opening van het museum."* (The Encyclopédie was purchased in 1780, four years before the museum's opening.) This is a remarkable institutional fact — the encyclopedia was acquired as a banned book, before the museum existed, making it one of the earliest documented purchases in the Teylers collection. Its complete absence from the catalog is extraordinary.

### 6. NO SET DESCRIPTION — VOLUME COUNT ABSENT (MAJOR)

The catalog provides no physical description of the set. The complete first edition comprises **35 folio volumes**: 17 text volumes + 11 plates volumes + 4 supplement text volumes + 1 supplement plates volume + 2 analytical table volumes. Whether Teylers holds a complete set or partial set is not stated. This is the most basic physical description fact for a multi-volume work and is entirely absent.

### 7. NO CENSORSHIP OR PUBLICATION HISTORY (MAJOR)

No mention of:
- Condemnation by the **Parlement de Paris (1759)** and revocation of the publishing privilege
- Placement on the **Index Librorum Prohibitorum** by Pope Clement XIII (1759)
- Completion of volumes 8–17 in **secret**, with the false Neuchâtel imprint
- **Le Breton's secret censoring** of the text before printing volumes 8–17, unknown to Diderot until after publication — one of the most famous episodes in publishing history
- The fact that Teylers acquired the work **as a banned book**, nine years after the condemnation
