## Object Description

This is a rare **gold** example of a "Judenmedaille" (Prague Jewish medal) depicting Empress Eleanor of Portugal (1434–1467), wife of Emperor Frederick III. It belongs to a well-documented series of pseudo-medieval cast medals produced in Prague c. 1600–1650 to glorify the Habsburg dynasty's ancestry. The medal features Eleanor enthroned in full imperial regalia on the obverse, with a rose design on the reverse accompanied by Latin verses praising her virtue. Gold examples of this series are described as "particularly rare" in the numismatic literature; no other gold example of the Eleanor subject has been identified at auction.

## Visual Analysis of the Medal

**Obverse:** A crowned empress seated frontally on an ornate Gothic throne, wearing full imperial regalia. She holds the **Reichsapfel** (imperial orb) in her left hand and a **scepter** in her right hand. Her gown is richly detailed with drapery folds and decorative borders. The crown is elaborate with fleur-de-lis elements. The throne features Gothic architectural details. Around the rim, **Gothic capital letters** spell the identification inscription. The entire composition is deliberately archaizing — imitating medieval seal design rather than contemporary early 17th-century style.

The gold surface is in excellent condition, with warm lustre and sharply preserved detail. The chasing (hand-finishing after casting) is exquisitely fine, particularly in the drapery, crown, and throne details.

**The deliberately pseudo-medieval style** — Gothic lettering, frontal enthroned pose, seal-like composition — is characteristic of the "Judenmedaillen" series and was intended to give the medal an appearance of antiquity, as if it were a contemporary 15th-century artifact.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. THIS IS A "JUDENMEDAILLE" — A WELL-DOCUMENTED SERIES

The medal belongs to the so-called **"Judenmedaillen"** (Jewish medals / Prague Jewish medals) — a famous and well-documented group of pseudo-medieval cast medals produced in Prague c. 1600–1650. The series was designed to **glorify the ancestors of the House of Habsburg**, depicting rulers and their consorts from Charlemagne onward.

The name "Judenmedaillen" has been in use since 1699 and was popularized by Johann David Köhler in his *Historische Münzbelustigung* (1729–1747). Despite the name suggesting Jewish manufacture, modern scholarship indicates the maker was more likely a Christian goldsmith in Prague. The exact maker remains unknown.

**Characteristics of the series:**
- Deliberately archaizing style modeled on medieval seals
- Gothic capital letter inscriptions
- Thin castings, all heavily chased (hand-finished)
- Consistently c. 51–52 mm diameter
- Primarily produced in silver; gold examples are particularly rare
- Produced during the era of Emperor Rudolf II's Prague court (1576–1612) or shortly after

**Subjects in the series include:** Charlemagne, Charles IV of Luxembourg, Albert II and Elizabeth of Luxembourg, Frederick III, Eleanor of Portugal, Maximilian I and Mary of Burgundy, St. Elizabeth of Hungary, and others.

This essential identification as part of the Judenmedaillen series is **entirely absent** from Teylers' catalog.

### 2. ELEONORA'S BIRTH YEAR: 1434, NOT 1436

Teylers lists Eleonora as "(1436-1467)." Most scholarly sources give her birth year as **18 September 1434** in Torres Vedras, Portugal. The 1436 date appears in some older references but 1434 is the currently accepted date.

### 3. THE INSCRIPTIONS TRANSLATED

**Obverse:** *"Leonora, filia Eduardi Regis Portugalliae, Friderici III Imperatoris uxor"*
Translation: **"Leonora, daughter of Eduard, King of Portugal, wife of Emperor Frederick III"**

**Reverse:** *"Ut rosa flores splendore corusco praefulget, sic Leonora virtutum amato choro praestat"*
Translation: **"As the rose shines forth among flowers with glittering splendor, so Leonora excels in the beloved chorus of virtues"**

The rose metaphor is directly reflected in the reverse design — a rose — connecting Eleanor's beauty and virtue to the queen of flowers. The Latin is courtly, humanist verse typical of Habsburg panegyric.

### 4. TITLE MISLEADING: NOT SPECIFICALLY A MARRIAGE MEDAL

The Teylers title says "Huwelijk van Eleonora... met keizer Frederik III, in 1452" — implying this commemorates the specific event of their 1452 marriage. This is **misleading**. The medal is part of a **dynastic portrait series** produced c. 1600–1650 depicting Habsburg ancestors. It does not commemorate the marriage itself; rather, it depicts Eleanor as one figure in a comprehensive lineage. The inscription identifies her by her dynastic position (daughter of a king, wife of an emperor) but makes no mention of the wedding. The date 1452 appears only in the Teylers title, not on the medal itself.

### 5. GOLD RARITY AND VALUE

Gold examples of the Judenmedaillen are described as "particularly rare." Comparable auction results:
- **Gold St. Elizabeth Judenmedaille** (CNG 100): 19.62g, 52mm — sold for **$3,250**
- **Gold Charlemagne Judenmedaille** (NAC 162, 2025): 12.85g, 51mm — sold for **5,500 CHF** (~$6,300)
- **Silver Eleanor of Portugal** (MDC Monaco 2022): 13.02g, 51.5mm — sold for **€1,488**

No other **gold** example of the Eleanor subject was found at auction. The Teylers medal at 16.2642g contains approximately 4.6 ducats' weight of gold. Its numismatic value would be substantially higher than the gold melt value.

### 6. PROVENANCE FULLY TRACED

**Haijo Tuinhout** (1805–1880): A Dutch numismatic collector whose collection was auctioned after his death. A published auction catalog exists: *Catalogus van eene kostbare en fraaie verzameling penningen en munten, eene uitgebreide numismatische bibliotheek...* (Catalog of a costly and fine collection of medals and coins, an extensive numismatic library...).

**Gerardus Theodorus Bom** (1814–1884): Amsterdam-based publisher, bookseller, and **auctioneer**. He conducted the Tuinhout sale on 9–10 February 1880 and simultaneously auctioned a collection of numismatic and heraldic books.

The provenance chain: Tuinhout collected → died 1880 → auctioned by Bom, 9 February 1880 → purchased by Teylers Museum.

### 7. PRODUCTION IN PRAGUE: CONTEXT

Prague was the natural production site because:
- **Emperor Rudolf II** (r. 1576–1612) moved the imperial court to Prague, making it a center of arts and medal-making
- The Prague mint operated continuously from 1526–1856
- The Judenmedaillen series served **dynastic propaganda** — asserting the antiquity and continuity of Habsburg rule by depicting their lineage back to Charlemagne
- Skilled goldsmiths, medalists (including Alessandro Abondio), and metalworkers congregated in Prague

### 8. ELEONORA'S HISTORICAL SIGNIFICANCE

The catalog provides minimal context. Eleanor of Portugal's significance includes:
- Her marriage to Frederick III on **16 March 1452 in Rome** was the **last papal coronation** of a Holy Roman Emperor
- Her dowry was **60,000 gold florins**
- Through her son **Maximilian I**, she is the ancestral link between the Portuguese House of Aviz and the Habsburg dynasty
- Through Maximilian, she is an ancestor of **Charles V** and all subsequent Habsburg rulers
- Her tomb at **Neukloster Abbey** in Wiener Neustadt (attributed to the workshop of Nicolaus Gerhaert van Leyden) is a major work of late Gothic sculpture

### 9. TECHNIQUE CONFIRMED: CAST AND CHASED

The catalog's question mark on "gegoten?" (cast?) can be resolved: all Judenmedaillen are **cast and then heavily chased** (hand-finished with engraving tools). The chasing is responsible for the fine detail visible in the image. The "ongerand" (unmilled edge) is consistent with cast medal production.
