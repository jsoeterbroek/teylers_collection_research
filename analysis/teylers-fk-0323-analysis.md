## Object Description

A **seven-foot Newtonian reflecting telescope** built by **William Herschel** (1738–1822) at his workshop in Slough, England, and acquired by Teylers Museum director **Martinus van Marum** in 1790 after visiting Herschel's observatory in person and observing nebulae through his instruments. Van Marum purchased the instrument for the small observatory on the roof of the Teylers Oval Room.

The telescope features an **octagonal mahogany tube** of approximately 7-foot (2.1 m) focal length, a **speculum metal primary mirror** (~155 mm / 6⅛ inch aperture), a diagonal secondary mirror directing light to a perpendicular eyepiece (confirming Newtonian design), mounted on a mahogany altazimuth stand with brass fittings. Herschel made hundreds of such instruments commercially — by 1795 he claimed to have made over 200 seven-foot mirrors alone. This telescope is a smaller sibling of the instrument Herschel used to **discover Uranus on 13 March 1781**.

A second listed maker, **Frédéric Guillaume Fries**, was a Swiss-born instrument maker previously working in London whom van Marum recruited to Haarlem in 1790, introducing him to Teylers' Second Society in November of that year. His specific role in this instrument (maintenance, assembly, or adaptation) is not established.

## Key Findings

### 1. ISAAC NEWTON LISTED AS CO-CREATOR OF AN 1790 INSTRUMENT — MISLEADING

The catalog lists Isaac Newton (1642–1727) as "inventor" in the same creator field as the actual makers, implying direct creative involvement in a 1790 instrument. Newton invented the Newtonian telescope *type* in 1668 — 122 years before this instrument was made. He had no involvement in FK 0323 whatsoever. The correct framing — used by Teylers' own Dutch website — is "naar Isaac Newton" (after Isaac Newton). Listing Newton as a co-creator alongside Herschel and Fries is factually misleading: his dates (1642–1727) further underscore the impossibility of his involvement.

### 2. NO DESCRIPTION OF THE INSTRUMENT

No free-text description. The catalog provides no explanation of what the telescope is, how it works, its optical design (Newtonian reflecting), the primary and secondary mirror system, the tube material or shape, or the mount type. For a designated *topstuk* (highlight) of the museum, this is a serious omission.

### 3. NO DIMENSIONS RECORDED

No tube length, mirror aperture, overall height, or weight. Comparable Herschel 7-foot Newtonians at the National Maritime Museum (AST0960) record 2134 × 197 mm overall with 160 mm speculum aperture. The absence of any dimensional data is a significant gap for a physical instrument record.

### 4. ROLE OF FRÉDÉRIC GUILLAUME FRIES NOT EXPLAINED

Fries is listed as "instrumentmaker" with no distinction from Herschel's role. The historical record shows Fries was van Marum's newly recruited Haarlem instrument maker in 1790, responsible for general maintenance and fabrication at Teylers. His specific contribution to FK 0323 — if any beyond transit, installation, or maintenance — is unestablished. The catalog gives no indication whether he made parts, assembled, or simply maintained the telescope, creating a false equivalence with its actual builder (Herschel in Slough).

### 5. NO PROVENANCE / ACQUISITION HISTORY

No provenance recorded. The circumstances are historically significant and well documented: van Marum personally visited Herschel at Slough, observed nebulae through his telescopes, and purchased this instrument directly from Herschel in 1790. This chain of custody and acquisition narrative is entirely absent.

### 6. Bibliography citation is incorrect and incomplete

Documentation field reads: "Van Marum's Scientific **Instrument** in Teylers Museum, Descriptive Catalogue by G. L. Turner." Issues:
- Correct title: ***Van Marum's Scientific Instruments*** (plural) ***in Teyler's Museum*** (possessive)
- Co-author **T. H. Levere** is omitted entirely
- No publication details: Vol. IV of *Martinus van Marum: Life and Work*, Noordhoff, 1973

### 7. Newton's birth year 1642 is Julian calendar — modern standard is 1643 (Gregorian)

Newton was born 25 December 1642 (Old Style / Julian) = 4 January 1643 (New Style / Gregorian). Modern scholarly standard uses 1643. The catalog should clarify which system it uses.

### 8. Van Marum's role "researcher" understates his primary role as commissioner/acquirer

Van Marum was the driving force behind the acquisition — he personally visited Herschel, observed with the telescopes, and purchased the instrument. "Researcher" implies active scientific use of this specific instrument but omits his role as commissioner/director.

### 9. "United Kingdom" is anachronistic for 1790

In 1790 the political entity was the **Kingdom of Great Britain**. The United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland came into existence on 1 January 1801. The correct contemporaneous designation is "Kingdom of Great Britain."
