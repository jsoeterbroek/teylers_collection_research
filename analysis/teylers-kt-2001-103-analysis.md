## Object Description

This is a pencil and grey wash drawing by Augustijn Claterbos (1750–1828), depicting a harbour scene with two tall stone towers (one partially ruined), boats on a river or harbour basin, and a walled town with a church in the background. The drawing was donated by the Van Weijdom Claterbos family (descendants of the artist) to Teylers Museum in 2003. It is part of a named group of Claterbos works in the collection.

## Visual Analysis of the Drawing

The drawing shows a lively harbour scene in pencil with grey wash on paper, framed by a deliberate brown border line:

- **Two prominent stone towers** dominate the center of the composition. The left tower is lighter in tone, with visible windows; the right tower is darker and more massive, partially ruined or unfinished, with crumbling masonry at its base. Both towers stand directly at the water's edge. A projecting beam or lever is visible at the top of the right tower, suggesting a **harbour crane** (havenkraan/Krahnen).
- **Water** fills the foreground, with several boats: a rowing boat with two figures in the left foreground, a small skiff with a single oarsman in the right foreground, and sailing vessels visible at the right edge.
- **A barrel** floats in the water near the base of the towers.
- **The walled town** extends behind the towers to the left, with buildings, a church tower with a pointed roof, and city walls visible. Figures are scattered along the quayside.
- **Hills** rise in the background behind the town, fading into atmospheric distance.
- **Birds** dot the sky, which is rendered in varied grey washes suggesting clouds.

The technique is accomplished: pencil establishes the drawing's structure, grey wash creates atmospheric depth and tonal modeling, and the brown border line frames the finished composition. The overall effect is atmospheric and picturesque, in the tradition of Rhine or Mediterranean harbour views.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. SCENE LIKELY AN IMAGINARY/COMPOSITE LANDSCAPE

The Teylers title "Landschap met stad en water" is generic. The depicted scene — stone towers at a river harbour, walled town with church, hills behind — is strongly reminiscent of **Rhineland river towns** or northern Italian port cities. The two towers with a projecting crane beam recall historical harbour crane towers found at towns like **Andernach**, **Bingen**, or **Trier** on the Rhine.

However, there is **no evidence that Claterbos ever traveled outside Haarlem**. He spent his entire career in one city. This drawing is almost certainly either:
- An **imaginary landscape** (fantasielandschap) composed from print sources
- A **copy after another artist's work** — possibly after prints by Herman Saftleven, Jan van Goyen, or other Rhineland landscape specialists
- A **drawing academy exercise** based on models provided by his teacher Cornelis van Noorde

The catalog provides no discussion of whether the scene is topographical or imaginary — a significant omission for understanding the work.

### 2. THE VAN WEIJDOM CLATERBOS FAMILY: DESCENDANTS OF THE ARTIST

The credit line "Schenking van de familie Van Weijdom Claterbos, 2003" identifies the donors as **direct descendants** of Augustijn Claterbos. The double surname "Van Weijdom Claterbos" was adopted in the 19th century. Genealogical records in the Haarlem archives document:
- Johannes Jacobus van Weijdom Claterbos (father), married Cornelia Gerarda Boon
- Albertus Cornelis van Weijdom Claterbos (born 23 September 1834, Haarlem)
- Johannes Jacobus van Weijdom Claterbos (born 27 February 1842, Haarlem)

A later family member, **F.H. van Weijdom Claterbos**, was the author of *Viennese Clockmakers and What They Left Us* (1979), showing continued scholarly/collector interests.

The family donated drawings that had been kept within the family for approximately **175 years** — a remarkable example of direct artistic provenance.

### 3. "KT" PREFIX EXPLAINED

The "KT" prefix most likely stands for **"Kunst Tekeningen"** (Art Drawings), distinguishing drawings from prints (KG = Kunst Grafiek). The "2001" in the inventory number likely represents the year of accession registration, while "103" indicates this is the 103rd work in that accession batch — suggesting the Claterbos family donation comprised a **substantial collection of over 100 drawings**.

### 4. PRODUCTION DATE CAN BE NARROWED

The range "c. 1770 – before 1828" spans nearly Claterbos's entire adult life. It can be narrowed:
- Claterbos joined the Haarlem Guild of St. Luke in **1777** — marking his professional start
- His documented active period is **c. 1777–1806**
- The accomplished grey wash technique is consistent with his mature working period

**Proposed narrowed date: c. 1777–1806.** The "c. 1770" lower bound may reflect possible student works before Guild membership, but professional output is more likely from 1777 onward.

### 5. CLATERBOS'S TEACHER: CORNELIS VAN NOORDE — TEYLERS CONNECTION

Claterbos was a pupil of **Cornelis van Noorde** (1731–1795), a landscape painter who co-founded the **Haarlemse Teekenacademie** (Haarlem Drawing Academy) in 1772. The academy was initially housed in **"De Hulst"**, the home of **Pieter Teyler van der Hulst** on the Damstraat — the very same Pieter Teyler whose estate founded Teylers Museum. This creates a direct institutional link between Claterbos's artistic training and the museum that now holds his work. This context is absent from the catalog.

### 6. DESCRIPTION INCOMPLETE

The catalog description mentions "Menschen in bootjes op het water, in het midden een grote steen" and a tower with a lever mechanism — but omits key visual elements:
- The second tower (there are clearly **two** towers, not one)
- The walled city with church
- The atmospheric sky treatment
- The floating barrel
- The sailing vessels on the right

### 7. CLATERBOS MONOGRAPH

A monograph by **Bert Sliggers** (Teylers Museum curator) was published for a Teylers exhibition: *Augustijn Claterbos, 1750–1828: opleiding en werk van een Haarlems kunstenaar* (64 pp., ISBN 9789066302280, 1990). This is the key scholarly source and should be referenced in the catalog.
