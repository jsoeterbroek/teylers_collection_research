## Object Description

This is a reproductive engraving by Christian Friedrich Boëtius (1706–1782) after a painting by Karel Dujardin (c. 1626–1678), depicting an Italianate pastoral landscape with a cow lying down and a sheep standing beside it, near a large tree. Made in Dresden in 1764, the print reproduces a painting from the collection of Christian Ludwig von Hagedorn, director of the Dresden Academy. It is listed among Boëtius's best-known works.

## Visual Analysis of the Print

The image shows an Italianate pastoral landscape. In the foreground, a large cow lies resting on the ground, facing left, with clearly visible horns. Behind and to the right of the cow stands a woolly sheep, also facing left. To the right, a large deciduous tree with gnarled trunk and leafy canopy frames the scene. In the background, rolling hills and mountains fade into a cloudy sky. A distant figure can be discerned on the left. The scene is typical of Karel Dujardin's pastoral compositions set in the Italian countryside.

The print demonstrates fine tonal work, with careful modeling of the animals' forms and delicate rendering of foliage and sky — characteristic of reproductive engraving rather than etching.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. TECHNIQUE INCORRECTLY CLASSIFIED: "ets" should be "gravure"

Teylers catalogs this as **"ets" (etching)**. This is almost certainly incorrect:

- The inscription uses **"fc."** (fecit), and Teylers' own extracted inscription says **"Sc."** (sculpsit) — the standard Latin abbreviation for engraving with a burin, not etching.
- Boëtius was consistently described in all historical sources as a **Kupferstecher** (copper engraver), never as an etcher.
- His other known prints in the Metropolitan Museum of Art are classified as **"engraving."**
- Reproductive prints of this period translating paintings into print were overwhelmingly done in **engraving** (burin work), which provided the fine tonal gradations needed to reproduce painted effects. Etching was used for more spontaneous, original artistic work.
- The fine, regular line work visible in the print — particularly the systematic crosshatching in the sky and the precise tonal modeling of the animals — is characteristic of engraving, not etching.

Ironically, Teylers lists Boëtius's role as **"graveur" (engraver)** while simultaneously classifying the technique as "ets" (etching) — a self-contradictory catalog entry.

### 2. Karel Dujardin birth year incorrect: 1622 should be c. 1626

Teylers lists Dujardin as "(1622-1678)." Current art-historical scholarship, based on a baptism record dated 27 September 1626 in the Lutheran Church in Amsterdam (established in Jennifer Kilian's catalogue raisonné), places his birth at **c. 1626**. The National Gallery London, the Rijksmuseum, the Getty Museum, and other major institutions all use c. 1626. The date 1622 derives from outdated 19th-century sources.

### 3. "Ex Collectione C.L. ab Hagedorn" — provenance identified

The inscription **"Ex Collectione C.L. ab Hagedorn"** explicitly states the original painting came from the collection of **Christian Ludwig von Hagedorn** (1712–1780), who was:

- **General Director of the Dresden Academy of Fine Arts** (appointed 1764 — the same year as this print)
- **Director of the royal picture collection in Dresden** (1763–1780)
- A significant art collector, specializing in Dutch and German 17th-century paintings, particularly landscapes
- An amateur engraver himself
- Author of *Lettre à un Amateur de la Peinture* (1755) and *Betrachtungen über die Mahlerey* (1762)

The "C.L." stands for **Christian Ludwig**, and "ab" is the Latin nobiliary particle equivalent to "von." This critical provenance information is entirely absent from Teylers' catalog record.

### 4. Original painting dimensions recorded on print

The inscription **"Alt. 11 3/4 unc. Lat. 9 1/4 unc."** records the dimensions of the original painting: height (*altitudo*) 11¾ inches, width (*latitudo*) 9¼ inches. Using the historical Dresden Zoll (approximately 23.6 mm), this gives approximately **277 × 218 mm** (roughly 28 × 22 cm). This is valuable information for identifying the source painting, which has not yet been definitively located in a current museum collection.

### 5. Publisher/seller information

The bottom inscription reads: "zu finden in der Long. Schwarzische Grosse Kunstl. bey dem Hof. Kupf. Stech. Boetius vorm Wald[…] Thore." This translates as: "to be found at the Long. Schwarzische Grosse Art shop at the Court Copper Engraver Boëtius's before the Wald[…] Gate." This reveals that:

- Boëtius held the title **Hof-Kupferstecher** (Court Engraver) — further confirming his role as an engraver, not etcher
- He sold prints commercially from a shop near one of Dresden's city gates
- The print was a commercial publication, not a private commission

### 6. Dimensions anomaly: paper trimmed into plate mark

The paper height (239 mm) is **smaller** than the plate height (304 mm), which is physically impossible for a complete impression — the paper must be at least as large as the plate for printing. This confirms the **paper has been trimmed** after printing, cutting into the plate mark at top and/or bottom. This was common when prints were bound into albums or mounted. The width relationship is normal (paper 251 mm > plate 226 mm), showing margins survive on the sides. The trimming may have affected some inscription text.

### 7. Description field empty

Teylers provides no description text for this object. A description such as "Italianate pastoral landscape with a reclining cow and standing sheep beside a large tree, with mountains in the background. Reproductive engraving after a painting by Karel Dujardin from the collection of C.L. von Hagedorn" would significantly improve the record.

### 8. Boëtius biographical context

Christian Friedrich Boëtius (born 1 April 1706, Leipzig — died 13 December 1782, Dresden) was a pupil of Paul Christian Zink and C.A. Wartmann. He was made **professor at the Dresden Academy in 1764** — the same year this print was made, and the same year Hagedorn became Academy director. This suggests the print may have been created as part of a collaborative project to document Hagedorn's collection, or to mark both appointments. Boëtius also contributed plates to the *Recueil d'estampes d'après les plus célèbres tableaux de la Galerie Royale de Dresde* (1753–1757).

### 9. "KG" collection prefix

The "KG" prefix in Teylers' numbering system most likely stands for **"Kunstgrafiek"** (art prints/graphic art) or possibly **"Kopergravure"** (copper engraving), distinguishing prints from drawings (which use different prefixes such as "KT").

## Object Context

This print belongs to the tradition of 18th-century reproductive engraving, where skilled engravers translated famous paintings into prints for wider circulation. The 1764 date places it at a significant moment: both Boëtius (as professor) and Hagedorn (as director) were newly appointed at the Dresden Academy. The print simultaneously documented Hagedorn's collection, demonstrated Boëtius's engraving skill, and made Dujardin's pastoral composition available to a broader public.
