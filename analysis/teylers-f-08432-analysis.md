## Object Description

This is one of the most famous fossils in the history of science — a nearly complete skeleton of a giant salamander (*Andrias scheuchzeri*), approximately 1 meter long, discovered in 1725 near **Öhningen** on Lake Constance, Germany. In 1726, Swiss naturalist **Johann Jakob Scheuchzer** published it as *Homo diluvii testis* ("Man, witness of the Flood") — the skeleton of a human who drowned in the biblical Deluge. The fossil remained misidentified for 85 years until **Georges Cuvier** visited Teylers Museum in **1811**, carefully prepared the fossil with a needle, uncovered the forelimbs, and definitively proved it was a giant salamander. This is one of the founding demonstrations of comparative anatomy and a classic case study in how scientific understanding evolves. Purchased by Martinus van Marum in **1802** for 14 Louis d'or, the specimen remains the **lectotype** of *Andrias scheuchzeri* and is displayed in its original showcase.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. DATE "23-5 MILLION YEARS AGO" IS TOO BROAD

The catalog gives "23-5 million years ago," which is simply the entire Miocene epoch (23.03–5.33 Ma). Current geological research is far more precise:

- The Öhningen deposits are classified as **Middle Miocene, Serravallian stage**
- Radiometric and biostratigraphic dating places them at approximately **13–12 Ma**
- A 2025 publication explicitly identifies the site as "The Miocene (Serravallian) Öhningen Konservat-Lagerstätte"
- The deposits formed in a **maar** (volcanic crater lake) in the Hegau volcanic area

**Correct age: approximately 13 million years ago (Middle Miocene, Serravallian).** The catalog range of 23–5 Ma is 4× broader than warranted by current geological knowledge.

### 2. SCHEUCHZER'S IDENTIFICATION HISTORY NOT EXPLAINED

The catalog title preserves the historic name "Homo diluvii testis" but provides no context. **Johann Jakob Scheuchzer** (1672–1733) was a Swiss physician and naturalist who acquired the fossil in 1725 from the Öhningen quarries. In 1726, he published *Homo diluvii testis et theoskopos* — a 24-page pamphlet arguing the skeleton belonged to a human drowned in the biblical Flood. He dated the publication "4032 after the Great Flood" rather than using the conventional calendar.

Scheuchzer's full title translates as "Man, Witness of the Flood and God-seer." He republished the description with a copperplate engraving in his monumental *Physica sacra / Kupfer-Bibel* (1731–1735). The original 1726 pamphlet is digitized on e-rara.ch.

### 3. CUVIER'S REIDENTIFICATION: THE FOUNDING MOMENT OF COMPARATIVE ANATOMY

The catalog does not describe the single most important event in this object's history. In **1811**, with the Netherlands annexed to the French Empire, **Georges Cuvier** visited Teylers while inspecting educational establishments. At the museum:

1. He was granted permission to prepare the fossil further
2. Using a sharp needle, he carefully removed surrounding matrix
3. He **uncovered the shoulder girdle and both forelimbs** — previously hidden in the rock
4. He recognized **small teeth along the crown of the skull** — incompatible with human anatomy
5. He declared: "nothing but a salamander, or rather a proteus of gigantic dimensions and of an unknown species"

This was published in his *Recherches sur les ossemens fossiles de quadrupèdes* (1812). Prior doubters included Johannes Gessner (1758, suggested catfish) and Petrus Camper (1787, suggested lizard), but Cuvier was the first to make a definitive identification through comparative anatomy.

### 4. PROVENANCE NOT DESCRIBED

The catalog provides no acquisition history. The chain:

1. **1725**: Fossil acquired from Öhningen quarries by Scheuchzer via intermediaries Hans Kaspar Blass and Hans Ulrich Holzhalb from Stein am Rhein
2. **1726**: Published as *Homo diluvii testis*
3. **1733**: Scheuchzer dies; fossil passes to his descendants in Zürich
4. **1802**: Purchased by **Martinus van Marum** from Scheuchzer's grandson in Zürich for **14 Louis d'or**, along with a fossilized swordfish
5. **1802–present**: Continuously at Teylers Museum

### 5. TYPE SPECIMEN STATUS NOT MENTIONED

The catalog does not note that this is the **lectotype** of *Andrias scheuchzeri* — the definitive reference specimen for the species. The specimen preserves a partial skeleton complete to the 16th vertebra. A 3D scan is available on Sketchfab (by Olof Moleman/@lordtrilobite).

The taxonomic history:
- 1831: Friedrich Holl renamed it *Salamandra scheuchzeri*
- 1837: Johann Jakob von Tschudi erected the genus *Andrias* (meaning "image of man" — a nod to the original misidentification), creating *Andrias scheuchzeri*

### 6. LIVING RELATIVES NOT MENTIONED

*Andrias scheuchzeri* belongs to the family **Cryptobranchidae** — giant salamanders that include living species:

- **Andrias japonicus** (Japanese giant salamander, up to 1.44 m)
- **Andrias davidianus** and relatives (Chinese giant salamanders — at least 4 species)
- **Cryptobranchus alleganiensis** (North American hellbender)

The morphological characters between fossil and living *Andrias* are "highly conserved" — the animals look remarkably similar despite 13 million years of separation. This makes the Teylers specimen directly relevant to conservation biology of living giant salamanders (now critically endangered).

### 7. SIGNIFICANCE IN THE HISTORY OF SCIENCE

The catalog does not contextualize this fossil's role in intellectual history:

- **From biblical literalism to deep time**: The fossil went from being "proof of biblical shallow time" (Scheuchzer) to "a visible embodiment of geological deep time" (Cuvier). This transition is one of the defining moments of modern science.
- **The name itself is a monument to error**: Both *Andrias* ("image of man") and *scheuchzeri* commemorate the original misidentification
- **Cited in every history of paleontology**: Martin Rudwick's *Scenes from Deep Time* and Stephen Jay Gould's works discuss it extensively
- **Parallels the Ostromia story**: Teylers' other crown jewel (F 06928) was also misidentified for over a century, making misidentification-and-correction a recurring Teylers theme

The catalog lists 14 associated documents but does not describe them. These likely include publications by Scheuchzer (1726), Cuvier (1812), Tschudi (1837), and volumes from the *Martinus van Marum: Life and Work* series. This cross-referencing is valuable but the documents should be identified.
