## Object Description

F 16390 is one of the infamous **Lügensteine** (German: "lying stones"; Dutch: "liegstenen") — deliberately carved limestone forgeries designed to deceive the German palaeontologist Johann Bartholomeus Adam Beringer (1667–1740), Dean of the Faculty of Medicine at the University of Würzburg. The stones were carved and planted in a hillside near Würzburg around 1725 by two colleagues who resented Beringer's arrogance: **J. Ignaz Roderick** (Professor of Geography, Algebra and Analysis at the University of Würzburg) and **Johann Georg von Eckhart** (Privy Counsellor and University Librarian). The stones depicted various organisms including insects, birds, plants, snails, worms, astronomical objects (suns, moons, comets), and Hebrew letters.

The catalog image shows a single carved pale-grey limestone slab (approximately 10–15 cm across) bearing a relief carving of what appears to be a crab or spider-like organism in the centre, with a small museum label attached in the lower right corner. The stone is irregular in outline, typical of the Lügensteine format, and the carving is in a three-dimensional raised relief style distinct from genuine fossilised impressions.

Beringer's hired diggers (three local boys from Eivelstadt) delivered him approximately 2,000 such stones between May 1725 and November 1725. He published an illustrated monograph on his "findings," titled *Lithographiae Wirceburgensis* (Würzburg: Philipp Wilhelm Fuggart, 1726), with detailed engravings of the stones. Shortly after publication he discovered the hoax, took legal action against Roderick and von Eckhart, and won his case on 13 April 1726. Both perpetrators were removed from their university positions; Roderick was banished from Würzburg. Despite this, Beringer's professional reputation never fully recovered. He died in 1740.

The Teylers Museum holds at least six Lügensteine. The stones represent one of the most celebrated cases of scientific fraud in history and are significant objects for the history of palaeontology and scientific methodology.

## Key Findings

### 1. MAKER NAMES ARE INCONSISTENTLY SPELLED AND POSSIBLY WRONG

The catalog lists the two makers as **"J.G. von Eckhart"** and **"J.I. Roderique."** These names require scrutiny:

**Von Eckhart:** Sources vary between "Johann Georg von Eckhart" and "Georg von Eckhart." The catalog's abbreviation "J.G. von Eckhart" is consistent with "Johann Georg von Eckhart" and is broadly acceptable.

**Roderick:** The catalog uses **"J.I. Roderique"** — a French-influenced spelling of the name. Sources give varying forms:
- "J. Ignatz Roderick" (Museum of Hoaxes, other English-language sources)
- "J. Ignace Roderique" (Bonhams auction catalogue; some German sources)
- "J. Ignaz Roderick" (scholarly German usage)

The form "Roderique" appears in period documents and French-language sources. However, the combination "J.I." with "Roderique" conflates two different spelling traditions without committing to a single authoritative form. The Bonhams catalogue of a copy of *Lithographiae Wirceburgensis* gives his name as "J. Ignace Roderique" which is probably the most period-accurate form. The catalog should commit to a single verified spelling with a source citation.

**Critically: the physical carving of the stones was likely performed by hired carvers or the digger-boys (Niklaus Hehn and others), not by Eckhart and Roderick themselves.** The two perpetrators conceived and directed the hoax, but were not the manual craftsmen who carved the stones. Listing them as "maker" in the sense of physical fabricator is technically arguable but may mislead.

### 2. OBJECT CATEGORY IS INCORRECT: "FossilSpecimen" IS WRONG

The catalog classifies F 16390 under the object category **"FossilSpecimen."** This is the most significant classification error in the record. The Lügensteine are explicitly **not** fossil specimens — they are **carved limestone forgeries made to resemble fossils**. The catalog's own "Object name" field correctly says "Fossil, imitated," which directly contradicts the "FossilSpecimen" category. These objects belong in a category of **fabricated/fraudulent objects** or at minimum "imitated fossil" — grouping them with genuine fossil specimens in the same taxonomic category as the Mosasaurus and cave bear is scientifically wrong and will produce incorrect results in any collection analysis or filtering operation.

### 3. "SCIENTIFIC NAME" FIELD IS MISUSED

The catalog gives a "Scientific name" of **"Lügensteine (Liegstenen)"** — using a German/Dutch colloquial term for the hoax stones as if it were a Linnaean binomial. This is a fundamental misuse of the scientific name field. Lügensteine are not organisms; they have no scientific name. The field should either be left blank, marked "N/A," or repurposed with an appropriate term like "carved limestone forgery." Using the hoax name in a scientific name field could cause confusion in any database that aggregates biological nomenclature.

### 4. DESCRIPTION IS ENTIRELY ABSENT

The catalog provides no narrative description whatsoever. The following information is absent:
- What is depicted on this specific stone (the catalog image shows what appears to be a crab/arthropod carving)
- The dimensions of the stone
- The material (oolitic limestone or Muschelkalk from the Würzburg area)
- The context of the Beringer hoax
- The relationship of this stone to the other five (or more) Lügensteine in the Teylers collection
- Whether the stone corresponds to any engraving in *Lithographiae Wirceburgensis* (1726), which would be of significant historical value

### 5. PRODUCTION DATE IS IMPRECISE AND POSSIBLY WRONG

The catalog gives "ca. 1725" as the production date and "18th century" as the production period. The known timeline is more precise: Roderick and von Eckhart began planting stones for Beringer's diggers in **spring/May 1725**, and deliveries continued until **November 1725**. The book was published in early **1726** and the hoax was exposed in **1726**. The production date of "ca. 1725" is broadly correct but could be more precise as "1725" or "1725–1726."

### 6. PROVENANCE AND ACQUISITION HISTORY ABSENT

No provenance chain is given. The documentation reference "Martinus van Marum. Life and work" suggests that the acquisition is documented in that multi-volume work (published 1969–1976 by the Hollandsche Maatschappij der Wetenschappen), but this reference is not unpacked in the catalog. Known facts that should be stated:
- Approximately 500 Lügensteine survive in collections worldwide out of the approximately 2,000 carved
- Teylers holds at least six specimens
- They came to Teylers likely via Martinus van Marum's collecting activities (the documentation reference implies this)
- Some Lügensteine are also held at Oxford University Museum of Natural History

### 7. THREE CATALOG IMAGES ARE UNDESCRIBED

The catalog contains three separate images (F 16390.jpg, F 16390-2.jpg, F 16390_01.jpg) but provides no indication of how many stones are depicted, whether these are three images of the same stone or images of different stones in the collection, and whether each stone has a separate catalog entry. The relationship between the images and the object count is ambiguous.

### 8. BAVARIAN GEOGRAPHY NOTE

The catalog places the object in "Europe \| Germany \| Bavaria \| Würzburg." Würzburg is indeed in Bavaria (Free State of Bavaria), which is geographically correct.
