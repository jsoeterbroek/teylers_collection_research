## Object Description

TMNK 00741 is a large silver commemorative medal struck in Amsterdam in 1667 to celebrate the Peace of Breda, which ended the Second Anglo-Dutch War (1665–1667). The treaty was signed on 31 July 1667 in the city of Breda, Netherlands. The medal was struck by order of the States of Holland (Staten van Holland) and is one of the most artistically ambitious Dutch medals of the seventeenth century.

The medal is the work of Christoffel Adolphi (also known as Adolfszoon, c. 1631–1684), the leading Dutch medal engraver of the period, working in Amsterdam. The inscriptions were composed by the Amsterdam poet Jan Zoet (1609–1674), a prolific writer known for his satirical and politically engaged verse. The obverse celebrates Dutch triumph in the war — most vividly symbolized by the Raid on the Medway (June 1667), during which Admiral Michiel de Ruyter's fleet entered the Thames estuary and destroyed the English fleet at anchor. The reverse celebrates the peace itself and the resumption of trade.

The medal is notable for a politically explosive element: the figure being trampled by Hollandia on the obverse was identified by contemporaries as **Discord** (Tweedracht), but the motto "PROCUL . HINC . MALA . BESTIA . REGNIS" ("Be gone, pernicious beast, from our realms") was widely understood as an insult directed personally at King Charles II of England. The British government formally protested; the Dutch States apologized and ordered the dies destroyed. That TMNK 00741 survives is evidence that some strikes were made before the dies were suppressed — giving this medal historical significance beyond its commemorative function. The medal is referenced in the standard corpus of Dutch historical medals compiled by Gerard van Loon.

## Key Findings

### 1. TRAMPLED FIGURE MISIDENTIFIED AS "MEDUSA"

The Teylers catalog describes the figure beneath Hollandia's feet as **"Medusa"** (specifically noting "Charles II"). This is incorrect on both counts.

The figure is **Discord (Tweedracht/Discordia)** — a conventional allegorical personification representing conflict and strife, here depicted as a prone hag. Standard numismatic literature consistently identifies this figure as Discord, not Medusa. Discord is iconographically distinct from Medusa: Medusa is a mythological Gorgon whose severed head appears on shields (the aegis), not as a full prostrate body. Visual inspection of the image confirms a prone female figure (not a severed head), consistent with Discord.

The identification "Charles II" in the Teylers record is a partial truth. The motto "PROCUL . HINC . MALA . BESTIA . REGNIS" was understood by contemporaries as a reference to Charles II — the "mala bestia" (evil beast) of the kingdoms — but the figure on the medal is allegorical, not a portrait. The Dutch States denied that Charles II was depicted and framed the figure as abstract Discord. Conflating "Medusa" with "Charles II" as if they were equivalent is a categorical error that confuses allegorical iconography with a historical portrait identification. The correct characterization is: the figure is Discord/Discordia; the motto was read as an allusion to Charles II.

This distinction matters because it affects scholarly interpretation of the medal's iconographic program. The Rijksmuseum describes the obverse as: "Nederlandse leeuw vertrapt wapentuig" and other sources as: "Holland tramples Discord; at her feet repose a lion and a lamb."

### 2. DIPLOMATICALLY SENSITIVE NATURE OF THE MEDAL NOT NOTED

The catalog makes no mention of the medal's extraordinary political and diplomatic history. The "MALA BESTIA" motto caused an international incident: Charles II's government formally complained to the Dutch States-General; the States of Holland issued an official apology, disclaimed any intent to insult the king, and **ordered the dies to be destroyed**. This order was one of the stated English grievances leading to the declaration of the Third Anglo-Dutch War in 1672.

The survival of this medal (and others like it) therefore has significant historical meaning. The catalog record provides none of this context, leaving the object without the most important fact about its historical significance.

### 3. OBVERSE DATE INSCRIPTION REQUIRES CLARIFICATION

The obverse inscription "IUN : 22 . 1667" refers to the date of the Raid on the Medway — specifically 22 June 1667 (Old Style), when English ships were burned at Chatham. The reverse gives "BREDAE . AO . 1667" referring to the treaty date of 31 July 1667. The catalog lists the production date as "1667–1668," which is reasonable, but does not explain what the two dates in the inscriptions signify. A reader seeing "IUN 22 1667" without context will not know this refers to the Medway raid, not the treaty.

### 4. REVERSE DATE IS PARTIALLY MISREAD IN CATALOG

The catalog transcribes the reverse legend as "REDIIT . CONCORDIA . MATER . BREDAE . AO . 1667." Visual inspection of the reverse image confirms the exergue reads: "REDIIT · CONCORDIA · MATER · / IUL · 31 · BRE · DAE · Aº · 1667" — that is, "JUL 31" (31 July) is present in the inscription, referring to the date of the treaty's signing. The catalog transcription omits the "IUL . 31" date element, which is significant: it specifies the exact treaty date. This is a transcription omission.

### 5. CATALOG REFERENCES ENTIRELY ABSENT

No standard catalog references are listed. The medal is documented in authoritative Dutch medallurgical literature:

- **Van Loon, Vol. II** (Gerard van Loon, *Beschryving der Nederlandsche Historipenningen*, 1723–1731) — the standard reference for seventeenth-century Dutch medals, which contains this piece
- **V&A** collection reference: object O350058 (Christopher Adolfszoon, 1667)
- **Rijksmuseum**: NG-VG-1-1015 (diameter 7.2 cm, weight 123.83 g — comparable to Teylers piece)
- **Royal Museums Greenwich**: MEC0117 (44mm variant) and related objects

The absence of any Van Loon reference is a standard cataloging gap for Teylers numismatic entries, but it is a meaningful omission for scholarly users.

### 6. PRODUCTION DATE "1667-1668" UNCERTAIN

The Rijksmuseum and V&A both date this medal simply to **1667**. The Teylers "1667–1668" date range is not explained and may reflect uncertainty or a misattribution to a later striking. The edge inscription refers to the reconciliation of 1667 and there is no documentary reason to extend the production into 1668.

### 7. WEIGHT COMPARISON WITH RIJKSMUSEUM EXEMPLAR

The Teylers exemplar weighs 122.236 g at 71.2 mm diameter. The Rijksmuseum exemplar (NG-VG-1-1015) weighs 123.83 g at 72 mm diameter. The slight differences are consistent with two separate strikes from the same dies (die wear, striking pressure, and silver alloy variations all affect final weight). No condition assessment is given in the Teylers record.

### 8. ROLE OF JAN ZOET

The catalog credits Jan Zoet (1609–1674) as "designer." His role was specifically as the **author of the inscriptions** — a poet who composed the Latin and Dutch texts for the medal. He did not design the dies. The medailleur (die cutter) was Christoffel Adolphi. The distinction between inscription author and die designer is important in medallurgical cataloging and should be clearer in the record.
