## Object Description

M 11800 is a collection of wooden crystal models made in pear wood, commissioned by or under the direction of French abbé René Just Haüy (28 February 1743 – 1 June 1822) to serve as three-dimensional teaching aids accompanying his landmark four-volume publication *Traité de Minéralogie* (1801). Haüy, professor of mineralogy at the Muséum national d'Histoire naturelle in Paris and widely regarded as the founder of modern crystallography, devised the mathematical theory that crystal forms arise from the stacking of fundamental "integrant molecules" — a theory he called the law of rational intercepts (later formalised as the law of rational indices). His models demonstrated these crystal forms in tangible, measurable three-dimensional objects, with each model precisely crafted with accurate dihedral angles.

The models were hand-carved in pear wood by a woodworking family and made available for purchase alongside the *Traité*. Teylers Museum director Martinus van Marum corresponded with Haüy and purchased 597 models between 1802 and 1804, paying for them through correspondence. Van Marum used the models to reorganise the museum's mineral collection according to Haüy's crystallographic system, displaying some in the central showcase of the Oval Room and storing the remainder in five large drawers. The models range from approximately 2.5 to 10 cm in size. Each model bears a label in iron-gall ink, reportedly written by Haüy himself, giving the crystal form name.

According to the authoritative study by Wim Saeijs (*Mineralogical Record*, vol. 39 no. 5, 2008), **565 of the original 597 models survive at Teylers**, making this the most complete collection of Haüy crystal models in the world. The Teylers catalog and topstukken page, however, state the number as **550** — an undercounting of 15 specimens.

No image is currently available in the online catalog for M 11800 (the image request returns a "not found" placeholder).

## Key Findings

### 1. WRONG COUNT: CATALOG SAYS 550, AUTHORITATIVE STUDY SAYS 565

The Teylers catalog, the museum's topstukken page, and promotional materials consistently state that the collection contains **550 models**. However, the definitive scholarly study — Wim Saeijs, "An appraisal of Haüy's wooden crystal models," *Mineralogical Record*, vol. 39, no. 5 (2008), pp. 385–396 — established that **565 of the original 597 models survive at Teylers**. This discrepancy of 15 specimens is significant:

- 597 originally purchased (1802–1804)
- 565 currently present (per Saeijs 2008)
- 550 stated in Teylers catalog and topstukken (15 undercounted)

This is not a trivial rounding difference. It means either 15 models have been lost since Saeijs's 2008 inventory, or — more likely — the catalog has never been updated to reflect the findings of the published study.

### 2. CATALOG URL IS BROKEN

The catalog record at priref 7040544 (https://teylers.adlibhosting.com/ais6/details/museum/7040544) consistently redirects to the Axiell search homepage rather than displaying the object detail page. This means the public-facing catalog record for one of Teylers' flagship "topstukken" is effectively inaccessible via its direct URL. Other records (M 01518 priref 7029903, M 01524 priref 7029909, etc.) load correctly.

### 3. NO IMAGE IN THE CATALOG

The GetContent image URL for M 11800 returns an "IMAGE NOT FOUND" placeholder. A collection of this world-class significance — the most complete Haüy crystal model set anywhere — has no photograph in the online catalog. This is a major documentation failure for an object that is both on public display and internationally recognised.

### 4. DATE AND CREATOR ATTRIBUTION ARE IMPRECISE

The production period is listed as "18de eeuw" (18th century), but the models were commissioned and produced specifically to accompany the *Traité de Minéralogie* published in 1801, and Teylers purchased them between **1802 and 1804** — which is the 19th century. The attribution of "18th century" is technically incorrect for the Teylers acquisition; it may reflect Haüy's theoretical work in the 1780s–1790s but misrepresents when these specific objects were made and acquired.

Additionally, René Just Haüy is listed as "onderzoeker" (researcher) in the creator field, but his precise role was as intellectual designer and commissioner of the models — they were physically carved by craftsmen (a woodworking family). The actual maker is unidentified in the catalog.

### 5. NO DIMENSIONS FOR THE COLLECTION OR INDIVIDUAL MODELS

The catalog gives no dimensions for the collection as a whole or for individual models. The known range is approximately 2.5 to 10 cm, but this is absent from the record. Given that M 11800 encompasses hundreds of individual objects, a collection-level record should specify the dimensional range at minimum.

### 6. HAÜY'S DATES AND SIGNIFICANCE ABSENT

The catalog lists Haüy as creator but gives no dates. René Just Haüy (1743–1822) was an ordained priest, Honorary Canon of Notre-Dame, member of the Paris Academy of Sciences (elected 1783), and Professor of Mineralogy at the Muséum national d'Histoire naturelle. He is recognised as the father of modern crystallography. His law of rational intercepts, his theory of integrant molecules, and his mineral classification system were transformative for 19th-century science. None of this context appears in the catalog record.

### 7. NO ACQUISITION PROVENANCE DETAILS

The catalog provides no information about how the models were acquired. The known record is:

- Van Marum entered into correspondence with Haüy following publication of the *Traité de Minéralogie* (1801)
- He purchased 597 models in multiple transactions between 1802 and 1804
- The acquisition is documented in Van Marum's correspondence, held in the Teylers archives

The acquisition story — one of the most direct personal connections between two founders of their respective fields — is absent from the public-facing catalog.

### 8. RELATIONSHIP TO ROMÉ DE L'ISLE MODELS NOT NOTED

Teylers also holds crystal models by Jean-Baptiste Louis Romé de L'Isle (1736–1790), who preceded Haüy in developing systematic crystal form descriptions. These are displayed alongside the Haüy models in the Oval Room. The relationship between the two sets — representing consecutive generations of crystallographic theory — is not indicated in the catalog record.

### 9. IRON-GALL INK LABELS BY HAÜY UNMENTIONED

Each model bears a label in iron-gall ink, reportedly written in Haüy's own hand, giving the crystal form name. This is an extraordinary feature — the labels are effectively manuscript autographs from a founding figure of crystallography. Their existence is entirely unmentioned in the catalog record, and their conservation status (iron-gall ink is known to deteriorate paper) is presumably unmonitored.
