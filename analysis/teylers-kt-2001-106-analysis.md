## Object Description

This is a grey wash drawing by Augustijn Claterbos (1750–1828), depicting two elegantly dressed women conversing animatedly in a fashionable late 18th-century Dutch interior. The drawing is an accomplished genre/conversation piece executed entirely in brush with grey wash, without pencil underdrawing. It was donated by the Van Weijdom Claterbos family (descendants of the artist) to Teylers Museum in 2003.

## Visual Analysis of the Drawing

The drawing depicts two women standing face-to-face in an elegant domestic interior, conversing with expressive hand gestures:

**The women:**
- **The woman on the left** wears a light-colored dress (likely silk) with a fitted bodice, wide skirt with bustle, and a sash at the waist. She holds a fan or letter in her lowered right hand. Her hair is arranged in a **high powdered coiffure** with a decorative lace cap — characteristic of the **1780s fashion**.
- **The woman on the right** wears a darker dress with a wide skirt with ruffled hem and a dark **shawl or mantelet** draped over her shoulders. Her hairstyle is similar — high and powdered with a cap. She stands near a paneled door, as if having just entered or about to leave.
- Both women gesture expressively — right hands raised — suggesting animated conversation or a dramatic exchange.

**The interior:**
- **Striped wallpaper** in vertical alternating light and dark bands covers the walls — fashionable in the **1780s–1790s** during the transition from Rococo to neoclassical taste
- A large **wall mirror** with an ornamental carved frame topped by an urn finial — a luxury furnishing
- A **Rococo/Louis XV upholstered chair** (bergère or fauteuil) with curved cabriole legs
- **Wainscoting** (wooden paneling) along the lower wall
- A **paneled door** on the right with classical molding
- A **patterned floor covering** (carpet or rug) visible at the bottom

**Technique:** The drawing is executed entirely in grey wash with a brush — no pencil lines visible. The tonal range is remarkable: from near-white highlights on the lighter dress to deep shadows in the room's corners. The striped wallpaper is rendered with controlled parallel brushstrokes. The drapery folds of the dresses are modeled with sophisticated tonal gradation.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. THE FASHION DATES THE DRAWING TO c. 1780–1790

The women's clothing and hairstyles provide precise dating evidence that the catalog ignores:

- **High powdered hairstyles with lace caps**: Characteristic of the **1780s** (the "pouf" style reached its peak before the French Revolution simplified fashion)
- **The wide-skirted dresses with bustles**: The silhouette with a pronounced bustle/back draping (robe à l'anglaise or robe à la polonaise) is typical of **c. 1775–1790**
- **The dark shawl/mantelet** on the right-hand woman: A practical overwrap fashionable in the 1780s
- **Striped wallpaper**: Peak fashion **1780s–1790s**, neoclassical transition period

**Proposed date: c. 1780–1790**, based on costume and interior decoration evidence. This is far more precise than the catalog's "c. 1770 – before 1828."

### 2. PRODUCTION DATE RANGE FAR TOO BROAD

The catalog gives **c. 1770 – before 1828** — a 58-year span covering Claterbos's entire adult life. Multiple lines of evidence narrow this dramatically:
- **Fashion evidence** (above): c. 1780–1790
- **Claterbos's active period**: c. 1777–1806 (Guild of St. Luke membership from 1777)
- **Interior decoration style**: Striped wallpaper + Rococo furniture = transitional period, 1780s

**Best estimate: c. 1785–1790.**

### 3. GENRE SCENE IS UNUSUAL FOR CLATERBOS

Claterbos is primarily known as a landscape artist and portraitist. The RKD describes him as a "Haarlem drawer-painter of landscapes, figures and portraits." This **genre/interior scene** represents a less-documented aspect of his practice. The drawing demonstrates that his abilities extended beyond topographical views — he could render fashionable interiors, costume detail, and human interaction with considerable skill. This is worth noting in the catalog.

### 4. BRUSH-ONLY TECHNIQUE (GRISAILLE) NOTABLE

Unlike the previous Claterbos drawing (KT 2001 103) which used pencil plus grey wash, this work uses **grey wash only** — no underlying pencil scaffolding. This requires greater confidence and directness: each brushstroke is final. The result is a monochrome brush painting (grisaille) with painterly qualities:
- Volumes defined by tonal gradation rather than contour lines
- Soft atmospheric effects in the room
- Fluid rendering of fabric folds and textures

The technique places this work closer to a small painting than a typical drawing.

### 5. POSSIBLE PURPOSE: ALBUM AMICORUM OR CONVERSATION PIECE MODEL

At 189×150 mm (roughly 7.5×6 inches), the drawing's small format suggests several possible purposes:
- An **album amicorum** (friendship album) contribution — the dimensions fit typical album page sizes (10×15 cm to 21×18 cm). Such albums experienced a resurgence in the mid-to-late 18th century.
- A **collector's album piece** — small finished drawings were collected in albums by Dutch amateurs
- A **model for a print** — small grisaille drawings frequently served as preparatory works for reproductive engravings
- The animated, theatrical quality of the two women's gestures may also suggest an **illustration for a literary or theatrical text**

### 6. DOMESTIC LIFE CONTEXT

The scene documents fashionable **late 18th-century Dutch domestic life** among the upper-middle class:
- The mirror was still a luxury item in this period
- The Rococo chair suggests a household with taste and means
- Striped wallpaper had replaced older leather wall hangings (goudleer) as the fashionable wall covering
- The animated conversation with expressive hand gestures may depict a genre scene inspired by theatrical comedy, or simply record the social life of Claterbos's Haarlem circle
