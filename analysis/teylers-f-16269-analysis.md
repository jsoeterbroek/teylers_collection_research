## Object Description

F 16269 is an assembled skeletal mount of the cave bear (*Ursus spelaeus* Rosenmüller, 1794), displayed in the First Fossil Hall of Teylers Museum. As stated on the Teylers topstukken page, this is a **composite skeleton** — bones from multiple individual cave bears found together in Moravian caves were assembled to create a single articulated mount, because the provenance of individual bones to specific individuals could not be determined scientifically. The specimen comes from the Moravia region (now the Czech Republic), near Brno, and dates to approximately 20,000–24,000 years ago (Late Pleistocene, around the Last Glacial Maximum).

The catalog image shows the fully mounted skeleton on a display stand in the museum's First Fossil Hall, with what appears to be a separate large cave bear skull displayed on a pedestal to its left. The skeleton is large and robustly built, with a characteristic domed skull, prominent sagittal crest, and the large flat grinding teeth associated with the predominantly herbivorous diet now attributed to *Ursus spelaeus*. The animal would have stood approximately 1.7 metres at the shoulder and measured up to 3.5 metres when reared on its hind legs, weighing up to approximately 1,000 kg (males); significantly larger than any living bear species.

Cave bears inhabited the mountainous regions of Europe and parts of Asia during the Middle and Late Pleistocene, and became extinct approximately 24,000 years ago — before or at the onset of the Last Glacial Maximum, and well before the arrival of anatomically modern humans in most of central Europe, suggesting that climate change rather than human hunting was the primary extinction driver.

## Key Findings

### 1. WRONG SCIENTIFIC NAME AUTHORITY: "BLUMENBACH" IS INCORRECT

The catalog attributes *Ursus spelaeus* to **Blumenbach**, but the correct authority is **Rosenmüller, 1794**. The species was described by Johann Christian Rosenmüller in his 1794 doctoral thesis *Quaedam de ossibus fossilibus animalis cuiusdam, historiam eius et cognitionem accuratiorem illustrantia* from Leipzig, based on a skull from the Zoolithenhöhle cave in Franconia. Rosenmüller is universally recognised as the naming authority.

Blumenbach (Johann Friedrich Blumenbach, 1752–1840) was a contemporary naturalist who described several Pleistocene megafauna taxa around 1799 (*Mammuthus primigenius*, *Coelodonta antiquitatis*, *Megaloceros giganteus*), which may explain the confusion. But he did not describe *Ursus spelaeus*. Due to unclear statements in some of Blumenbach's works, later authors such as Cuvier (1806) and Oken (1839) incorrectly affixed Blumenbach's name to the cave bear — a historical error that has persisted in popular literature and, evidently, in this catalog.

The GBIF Backbone Taxonomy and all modern authoritative sources (including the dedicated Cave Bear PMC article, Britannica, and the *Ursus spelaeus* Wikipedia article) confirm: ***Ursus spelaeus* Rosenmüller, 1794**.

### 2. AGE IS UNDERSTATED: "ca. 20,000 years ago" IS TOO RECENT

The catalog states the production period as approximately 20,000 years ago. Current scientific consensus places cave bear extinction at approximately **24,000–27,000 years ago**, i.e., before or at the onset of the Last Glacial Maximum. The most recent comprehensive analysis (Stiller et al. 2016, PMC) concludes extinction at approximately 24,000 cal. years BP. Earlier studies by Pacher & Stuart (2009) suggested 27,000–28,000 BP.

The figure of 20,000 years ago postdates the generally accepted extinction of the species and falls within the Last Glacial Maximum itself (23,000–19,000 BP), a period from which no reliable cave bear remains have been radiocarbon dated. Stating "20,000 years ago" is therefore inconsistent with the scientific literature.

A defensible range would be: **approximately 24,000–28,000 years ago (Late Pleistocene, MIS 3/2 transition)**.

### 3. COMPOSITE NATURE OF SKELETON NOT DISCLOSED

The Teylers topstukken page explicitly states that this is a **composite skeleton** assembled from bones of multiple individuals found together in Moravian caves. This critical information is entirely absent from the catalog record. Composite skeletal mounts are common for cave bear specimens because caves served as communal hibernation and death sites, meaning the bones of many individuals are mixed. A visitor or researcher consulting the catalog has no way of knowing they are looking at an assemblage rather than a single individual.

This omission is scientifically significant: the composite nature affects the specimen's use in morphometric studies and renders individual biological data (age at death, sex, pathologies) inapplicable to the mounted skeleton as a whole.
