## Object Description

This is a two-part quarto volume containing Job Baster's *Natuurkundige Uitspanningen, behelzende eenige waarneemingen over sommige zee-planten en zee-insecten, benevens derzelver zaadhuisjes en eyernesten* ("Natural Recreations, containing some observations on certain sea-plants and sea-insects, together with their seed-vessels and egg-nests"). Published by J. Bosch in Haarlem between 1759 and 1765, the work comprises Part I (169 pp, 16 plates) and Part II (167 pp, 13 plates), bound together as one volume. The 29 plates are hand-colored copperplate engravings depicting marine organisms — hydropolyps, corallines, mollusks, their eggs, and larvae. This is a foundational work in Dutch marine biology and one of the earliest systematic illustrated studies of invertebrate marine life from the North Sea and Oosterschelde.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. JOB BASTER: A MAJOR 18TH-CENTURY NATURALIST

The catalog identifies the author only as "J. Baster." Job Baster (2 April 1711 – 6 March 1775) was a Dutch physician and naturalist of considerable international standing, born and based in Zierikzee, Zeeland. He studied medicine at Leiden under Herman Boerhaave, defending his doctoral thesis *De Osteogenia* in 1731 at the age of just 20 — a thesis later selected by Albrecht von Haller for inclusion in his prestigious collection of medical dissertations. After his studies, Baster traveled to Paris and London, where he befriended the botanist Philip Miller (keeper of the Chelsea Physic Garden) and Hans Sloane (founder of the British Museum). He then returned to Zierikzee, where he practiced medicine for the rest of his life while devoting his evenings to natural history.

Baster held membership in several of the most prestigious scientific societies of his era:
- **Fellow of the Royal Society of London** (elected 1738, on the recommendation of Boerhaave and Willem 's Gravesande)
- **Member of the Academia Caesarea Leopoldina** (the Imperial Academy of natural scientists, as indicated by "Acad. Caes." on the title page of the Latin edition)
- **Member of the Hollandsche Maatschappij der Wetenschappen** (the Dutch Society of Sciences in Haarlem, founded 1752)
- **Member of the Zeeuwsch Genootschap der Wetenschappen** (the Zeeland Scientific Society in Middelburg)

In 1764, at age 53, Baster suddenly lost sight in his left eye. Surgery failed to restore it, and his declining health forced him to cease his marine investigations. He died on 6 March 1775 and was buried in the family grave at the Onze Lieve Vrouwe Monsterkerk in Zierikzee.

No authenticated portrait of Baster exists. A statue in his honor was unveiled in Zierikzee in 1976, though it is an artist's imagining rather than a likeness.

### 2. WHAT THIS WORK DOCUMENTS: PIONEERING MARINE BIOLOGY

*Natuurkundige Uitspanningen* ("Natural Recreations" or "Natural Amusements") is Baster's magnum opus. Beginning around 1759, he systematically investigated marine organisms in the Oosterschelde estuary and along the North Sea coast. The work documents:

- **Hydropolyps** ("zeeboompjes" — sea-trees): Baster was the first author to describe and illustrate the medusa stage (free-swimming jellyfish form) of hydropolyps. This was a groundbreaking observation in understanding the complex life cycles of cnidarians.
- **Mollusks and their reproduction**: He documented the eggs of mussels, oysters, periwinkles, and other species. He described the egg capsules of whelks, buccinums, and nudibranchs. He was the first to describe veliger larvae of mollusks, which he termed "handgranaatjes" (little hand grenades) due to their shape.
- **Corallines and the zoophyte debate**: Baster argued that corallines were of vegetable (plant) rather than animal nature. He noted that polyps on corallines were unevenly distributed and that different species of polyps could inhabit the same coralline structure. This positioned him against John Ellis, the English naturalist whom Linnaeus called "the lynx-eyed discoverer of zoophytes."

Each part of the work was issued in separate fascicles ("books") with their own divisional title pages. Part I has divisional titles dated 1759, 1760, and 1761; Part II has titles dated 1762 and 1765.

### 3. THE ZOOPHYTE CONTROVERSY WITH JOHN ELLIS

A major scientific debate forms the intellectual backdrop of this work. In the mid-18th century, naturalists fiercely disputed whether organisms found on the sea floor were plants, animals, or — as Carl Linnaeus proposed — "zoophytes" (plant-animals). Baster took the position that corallines were vegetable in nature, presenting evidence including: the sparse and uneven distribution of polyps on corallines; the presence of multiple polyp species on single coralline structures; and the apparent ability of corallines to sprout and grow in seawater.

John Ellis published a detailed rebuttal in the *Philosophical Transactions* of the Royal Society (Vol. 50, read 9 June 1757), titled "Remarks on Dr. Job Baster's Observationes de Corallinis." Ellis argued that corallines possessed a "gelatinous inside" communicating through stems, demonstrating animal organization. He noted that burning corallines produced a "pungent volatile alkaline smell" identical to horn, hair, or oysters — not vegetation. Ellis was ultimately vindicated; Baster's position on corallines was incorrect and appears to have damaged his international reputation. However, Baster's meticulous observational work on other organisms — particularly his pioneering descriptions of hydropolyp medusae and mollusk larvae — retained lasting scientific value.

### 4. THE LATIN PARALLEL EDITION: *OPUSCULA SUBSECIVA*

The catalog does not mention that a simultaneous Latin edition was published under the title *Jobi Basteri med. doct., Acad. Caes., Societat. Reg. Lond., et Holland. Socii Opuscula subseciva, observationes miscellaneas de animalculis et plantis, quibusdam marinis, eorumque ovariis et seminibus continentia* ("Leisure-hour minor works, containing miscellaneous observations on certain marine small animals and plants, and their ovaries and seeds"). This edition was published by the same printer, Joannem Bosch in Haarlem, over the same period (1759–1765). The Latin edition was intended for the international scientific audience, while the Dutch *Natuurkundige Uitspanningen* served the domestic readership. Both editions contain the same 29 plates. The Latin title page identifies Baster as a doctor of medicine and fellow of the Imperial Academy, the Royal Society of London, and the Holland Society.

The third book (fascicle) of the *Opuscula subseciva* was dedicated to **Laurens Theodorus Gronovius** (1730–1777), a Dutch naturalist famous for his ichthyological work *Museum ichthyologicum* (1754), as a token of friendship and gratitude for numerous benefits.

### 5. THE 29 PLATES: ILLUSTRATORS AND ENGRAVERS

The 29 hand-colored folding copperplate engravings are among the most significant features of this work. The catalog does not identify the artists. Based on bibliographic records:

- **T. Rhodius** is credited as the illustrator ("ill.") in catalog records of the Latin edition. He drew the original designs from Baster's observations and specimens.
- **Cornelis van Noorde** (1731–1795), a Haarlem-based artist and engraver, is associated with the plates in some bibliographic sources. Van Noorde was a remarkably versatile artist — etcher, mezzotint engraver, draughtsman, and painter — known especially for his vignettes made for the Haarlem printer Johannes Enschede. He was a pupil of Frans Decker and Tako Hajo Jelgersma, and became a member of the Haarlem Guild of St. Luke in 1761.
- **J. Rhodius** also appears in some catalog records as a contributor to the illustrations.

Crucially, Cornelis van Noorde co-founded the **Haarlemse Teekenacademie** (Haarlem Drawing Academy) in 1772, established in **De Hulst** — the home of Pieter Teyler van der Hulst, the founder of Teylers Museum. Van Noorde's works are held in the Teylers Museum collection and the North Holland Archives. This creates a direct link between the illustrator of the Teylers catalog object and the founder of the museum itself.

The plates depict hydropolyps, corallines, mollusk egg capsules, veliger larvae, nudibranchs, and various marine invertebrates. In copies with original hand-coloring (as indicated for the Dutch edition), the plates are scientifically significant as they represent some of the earliest accurate color representations of these organisms.

### 6. PUBLISHER J. BOSCH IN HAARLEM

The catalog identifies the publisher as "J. Bosch, Haarlem" without further context. Joannes (Jan) Bosch was a Haarlem-based publisher active from at least 1758 through the mid-1760s. He specialized in scientific publications, including medical and natural history works. In addition to Baster's Dutch and Latin editions, Bosch published works on colonial medicine, such as *Aanmerkingen over de in Curaçao van tyd tot tyd in zwang gaande ziekten* (Remarks on diseases prevalent in Curaçao). His location in Haarlem — a center of Dutch printing and scientific culture, home to the Hollandsche Maatschappij der Wetenschappen — positioned him well for publishing learned works.

### 7. CONNECTION TO TEYLERS MUSEUM AND THE HOLLANDSCHE MAATSCHAPPIJ

Multiple threads connect this work to Teylers Museum:

- **Baster and the Hollandsche Maatschappij der Wetenschappen**: Baster was a member of the Hollandsche Maatschappij, the scientific society founded in Haarlem in 1752. He was an active member who could recommend others for election — in 1767, the marine naturalist Martinus Slabber was admitted to the society on Baster's recommendation. The Hollandsche Maatschappij, while a separate institution from Teylers, has been closely intertwined with it since the society moved to Hodshon House at Spaarne 17 in 1831, directly across from the museum.

- **Pieter Teyler van der Hulst** (1702–1778), the wealthy Haarlem Mennonite whose bequest created Teylers Museum, was a contemporary of Baster. Although Mennonites were excluded from formal membership in the Hollandsche Maatschappij, Pieter Teyler was deeply influenced by its scientific culture. His will (1756) established the Teyler Foundation for the promotion of religion, art, and science.

- **Cornelis van Noorde**, associated with the plates, co-founded the Haarlem Drawing Academy in Pieter Teyler's own house and has works in the Teylers collection.

- **The book's publication in Haarlem** by J. Bosch places it squarely within the Haarlem scientific and publishing milieu that gave rise to both the Hollandsche Maatschappij and Teylers Museum.

### 8. BASTER'S SCIENTIFIC CORRESPONDENCE NETWORK

Baster maintained an extensive correspondence with leading scientists of his era, though the catalog does not mention this:

- **Carl Linnaeus** (1707–1778): Baster corresponded with Linnaeus regarding marine organisms, particularly seaweed research and the zoophyte question. At least one letter is preserved: Baster to Linnaeus, dated 20 March 1767, sent from Zierikzee to Uppsala (reference L3887). This letter is held by the Linnean Society of London and has been digitized through the Linnaean Correspondence Project via the Alvin database at Uppsala University Library.

- **John Ellis** (c. 1710–1776): Their published exchanges in the *Philosophical Transactions* document the zoophyte debate. Ellis's "Remarks on Dr. Job Baster's Observationes de Corallinis" (1757) is a key document in the history of marine biology.

- **Laurens Theodorus Gronovius** (1730–1777): The Leiden naturalist and ichthyologist, to whom Baster dedicated part of the *Opuscula subseciva*, was clearly a close friend and collaborator.

- **Herman Boerhaave** (1668–1738): Baster's teacher at Leiden, who recommended him for Royal Society fellowship.

- **Philip Miller** (1691–1771): The Chelsea Physic Garden keeper, whom Baster befriended in London. Miller named the plant genus *Basteria* (a Florida flowering shrub) after him. Baster translated Miller's gardening work into Dutch as *Maandelijksche tuinoefeningen*, which went through multiple editions.

### 9. BASTER'S BROADER SCIENTIFIC LEGACY

Beyond this specific work, Baster's legacy includes:

- The malacological journal **Basteria**, published since 1936 by the Nederlandse Malacologische Vereniging (Netherlands Malacological Society), is named in his honor.
- The brackish-water snail *Heleobia stagnorum* is commonly known as "Basters drijfslak" (Baster's floating snail).
- He is credited with **introducing goldfish to the Netherlands**.
- He cultivated rare plants in his garden, including specimens from Java.
- He assembled a decorative buffet adorned with tropical shells and coral fragments, which is preserved at the Stadhuismuseum in Zierikzee.
- He published on atmospheric phenomena, weather, bird migration, salt extraction from seaweed, and foreign agricultural potential.
