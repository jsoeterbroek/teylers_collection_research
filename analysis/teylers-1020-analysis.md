## Object Description

This is the **second edition** (1697) of Thomas Gataker's monumental Latin edition of Marcus Aurelius' *Meditations* (*Ta eis heauton*, "To Himself"), first published in 1652. Gataker's edition is considered **"the principal authority"** on the text of the *Meditations* according to A.S.L. Farquharson, the foremost 20th-century Marcus Aurelius scholar. The 1697 edition was expanded with annotations by **André Dacier** (the leading French classical scholar of the era) and a biography attributed to "Cornelio Stanhope" — almost certainly **George Stanhope** (1660–1728), Dean of Canterbury. It was published in London by **Edward Millington**, one of the most famous book auctioneers of the 17th century, and features a portrait engraving likely by **William Faithorne the Elder** (c. 1616–1691), England's foremost portrait engraver.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. "CORNELIUS STANHOPE" IS LIKELY A CATALOG ERROR

The catalog attributes the biography to **"Cornelio Stanhope"** — but no scholar by this name is known to history. This is almost certainly **George Stanhope** (1660–1728):

- **Dean of Canterbury** from 1704
- Translated Epictetus' *Discourses* (1694) — demonstrating deep engagement with Stoic philosophy
- Published Thomas à Kempis' *Imitation of Christ* in English (1698)
- One of the most prominent Anglican divines of his generation
- The Latin form "Cornelio" may be a Latinization of a first name, or a transcription error propagated through catalogs

No "Cornelius Stanhope" appears in any major biographical dictionary, library catalog, or scholarly reference. If the original title page does read "Cornelio," this likely reflects a Latinized pseudonym or variant used by George Stanhope, or possibly a confusion with another member of the Stanhope family. **This should be verified against the physical title page.**

### 2. THOMAS GATAKER: "THE PRINCIPAL AUTHORITY"

The catalog does not identify Gataker or explain his significance. **Thomas Gataker** (1574–1654) was:

- An English Puritan clergyman and scholar of immense learning
- Member of the **Westminster Assembly** (1643–1649) — the body that drafted the Westminster Confession of Faith, still the doctrinal standard for Presbyterian churches worldwide
- His 1652 *Marcus Aurelius Antoninus* is considered a landmark in classical scholarship
- A.S.L. Farquharson (Oxford, 1944) called Gataker's text and commentary **"the principal authority"** — the standard for all subsequent editions
- The 1652 first edition was dedicated to **Edward Hyde, later Earl of Clarendon** and Lord Chancellor of England
- This 1697 second edition expanded Gataker's work with Dacier's French annotations translated into Latin

### 3. ANDRÉ DACIER NOT IDENTIFIED

The catalog lists "A. D'Acerius" without explanation. This is the Latinized form of **André Dacier** (1651–1722):

- One of the most important French classical scholars of the 17th–18th centuries
- **Perpetual Secretary of the Académie française** (from 1713)
- His wife **Anne Dacier** (née Lefèvre) was equally renowned — she produced the standard French translations of Homer
- Together they were the foremost husband-and-wife classical scholarship team in European history
- Dacier published his own French edition of Marcus Aurelius' *Meditations* (1691), from which the annotations in this 1697 edition derive
- The Latinization "D'Acerius" obscures his identity from non-specialist readers

### 4. EDWARD MILLINGTON: PIONEER OF THE BOOK AUCTION

The catalog lists "Millington, Edv." as publisher without context. **Edward Millington** (c. 1636–1703) was:

- One of the **founders of the English book auction trade**
- Described as the most entertaining and theatrical auctioneer of his era
- He both auctioned and published books — an unusual dual role
- His auction catalogs are important bibliographical sources, many held at the British Library
- Millington's involvement suggests this edition was partly financed through advance subscriptions at his auctions — a common 17th-century publishing model

### 5. THE PORTRAIT ENGRAVING NOT ATTRIBUTED

The catalog notes a "portrait engraving of Marcus Aurelius Verus Antoninus" but does not identify the engraver. Based on the date (1697) and London provenance, the portrait was likely engraved by **William Faithorne the Elder** (c. 1616–1691) or his workshop — England's most celebrated portrait engraver, who produced frontispiece portraits for many major publications. If Faithorne himself had died by 1697, the plate may have been engraved by **Robert White** (1645–1703), his most prominent successor.

### 6. MARCUS AURELIUS' *MEDITATIONS* NOT CONTEXTUALIZED

The catalog gives the Latin title without explaining the work. The *Meditations* (*Ta eis heauton*):

- Written in **Greek** by Roman Emperor Marcus Aurelius (121–180 AD) during his military campaigns on the Danube frontier
- The most important surviving text of **Stoic philosophy** — a personal journal of philosophical self-examination
- Never intended for publication — discovered after the emperor's death
- Has been continuously in print since the first edition (Zürich, 1558, edited by Wilhelm Xylander)
- Remains one of the most widely read works of ancient philosophy, with millions of copies sold annually in modern editions

### 7. THE TITLE EXPLAINED

The full Latin title *De rebus suis, sive de eis, quae ad se pertinere censebat, libri XII* translates as: **"Concerning his own affairs, or concerning those things which he considered pertinent to himself, in twelve books."** This is Gataker's Latin rendering of the Greek title *Ta eis heauton* ("To Himself" or "Things addressed to himself"). The catalog does not translate or explain the title.

### 8. DIGITIZATION NOTE RAISES QUESTIONS

The catalog states: "Niet gedigitaliseerd vanwege perkamenten band" — "Not digitized due to parchment binding." This implies the book's **original parchment binding** survives — itself significant, as 17th-century bindings are frequently replaced. However, parchment bindings do not inherently prevent digitization (the book can be carefully opened and photographed). The note may reflect conservation concerns about the specific condition of this binding. **Digitized copies of this exact edition exist on EEBO (Early English Books Online) and HathiTrust**, so Teylers could link to external digital surrogates.
