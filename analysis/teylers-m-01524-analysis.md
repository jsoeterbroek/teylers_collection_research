## Object Description

M 01524 is a large, visually spectacular specimen of stibnite (antimony trisulfide, Sb₂S₃) from the Ichinokawa Mine, Saijō, Ehime Prefecture, Japan — one of the most celebrated mineral localities in the world. The catalog image shows a towering cluster of bladed prismatic crystals displaying the characteristic lead-grey to black metallic lustre and bright mirror-like cleavage faces of stibnite. The crystals are elongated parallel to the c-axis with characteristic striated faces and grow in a complex fan-like or sheaf arrangement, reaching impressive height. The base consists of a mass of smaller interlocking crystals. There is no obvious matrix rock — the specimen appears to consist almost entirely of stibnite crystals, which is typical of Ichinokawa material.

The Ichinokawa Mine is located on Shikoku Island, near the city of Saijō (formerly Saijō-chō) in Ehime Prefecture, Japan. It operated as an antimony mine from at least the 7th century (tradition holds it supplied antimony for the Great Buddha of Nara, cast in 748 CE) and reached peak production between 1875 and 1900, when it produced 16,600 tons of antimony. The mine closed around 1957–1962. During the late 19th century, Ichinokawa produced the largest and finest stibnite crystals ever documented: single crystals up to approximately 60 cm long and 15 cm in diameter. Specimens were exhibited at the Kyoto Exhibition (1871) and the Paris World's Fair (1878), where they won silver medals. For over a century, Ichinokawa was considered the premier world locality for stibnite.

## Key Findings

### 1. SCIENTIFIC NAME IS OBSOLETE FRENCH AND NOT THE IMA-APPROVED NAME

The catalog uses **"Antimoine sulfure"** — an archaic French name meaning "antimony sulfide." This is not the IMA-approved mineral name. The correct IMA-approved name is **Stibnite** (symbol: Sbn), with the chemical formula **Sb₂S₃**. The Dutch title "Antimoniet kristal" is closer to the accepted vernacular but is itself an older form; the internationally recognised Dutch name is also "stibniet."

Other historical synonyms include: antimonite, antimony glance, grey antimony — none of which should appear in a modern scientific catalog. Using "Antimoine sulfure" dates the catalog entry to 18th or 19th-century French mineralogical literature (Haüy used this term) and has not been appropriate since the IMA standardised mineral nomenclature in the 20th century.

### 2. PREFECTURE NAME IS MISSPELLED

The catalog lists the province as **"Echime Prefecture"**. The correct spelling is **Ehime Prefecture** (愛媛県). This is a straightforward transcription error. The Mindat standard locality is: Ichinokawa Mine, Saijō City, Ehime Prefecture, Shikoku Island, Japan.

### 3. MISSING CHEMICAL FORMULA AND MINERALOGICAL DATA

No chemical formula, crystal system, or physical property data appears in the record. For a mineral catalog, this is a fundamental omission. The complete standard data is:

- Formula: Sb₂S₃
- Crystal system: Orthorhombic
- Space group: Pbnm (No. 62)
- Unit cell: a = 11.234 Å, b = 11.314 Å, c = 3.837 Å; Z = 4
- Mohs hardness: 2
- Specific gravity: 4.56–4.64
- Lustre: Metallic, bright on cleavage faces
- Cleavage: Perfect on {010}, imperfect on {100} and {110}
- Streak: Lead-grey
- Crystal habit: Prismatic, elongated parallel to c; striated on prism faces; often in parallel groups or fan-like clusters

### 4. "PRODUCTION DATE: ONBEKEND" IS INAPPROPRIATE FOR A MINERAL

As with other minerals in the Teylers collection, the "production date" field is structurally inappropriate for a natural specimen. Stibnite does not have a production date. The field should distinguish between:

- **Geological age of formation:** Approximately Late Cretaceous–Palaeogene (the Ichinokawa deposit is associated with hydrothermal activity related to subduction-arc volcanism)
- **Date collected / date of peak production:** Most fine Ichinokawa specimens entered collections in the late 19th century (1875–1900 peak production period)
- **Acquisition date by Teylers:** Listed as "19th century" — more precision is desirable

### 5. DIMENSIONS ENTIRELY ABSENT

Given the visual evidence that this is an exceptional, large specimen (comparable to the finest Ichinokawa material in other major collections), dimensions are critical for cataloging. ### 6. ICHINOKAWA MINE SIGNIFICANCE NOT EXPLAINED

The catalog gives the locality accurately but provides no context for why Ichinokawa is significant. Ichinokawa was for over a century considered the premier world locality for stibnite, and its finest specimens are held in the Natural History Museum London, the Smithsonian, the Muséum national d'Histoire naturelle in Paris, and other major institutions. A specimen from this locality is a world-class mineralogical object. None of this context appears in the catalog.

### 7. NO PROVENANCE OR ACQUISITION INFORMATION

The record contains no information on how this specimen entered the Teylers collection. Given that Ichinokawa's finest specimens entered European collections primarily through trade in the 1870s–1900s, knowing the acquisition route would be historically significant. Was it purchased at the Paris 1878 World's Fair? Via a dealer? As part of another collection? The record is silent.

### 8. NO ASSOCIATED MINERALS LISTED

In their natural state, Ichinokawa stibnite specimens sometimes occur with minor associated minerals including cervantite (Sb₂O₄, an oxidation product), valentinite (Sb₂O₃), and rarely kermesite (Sb₂S₂O). The catalog makes no mention of associated minerals.
