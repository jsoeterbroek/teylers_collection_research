## Object Description

This is an etching by Karel Dujardin (c. 1626–1678), serving as the **title page (frontispiece) for the series "Dieren" (Animals)** — a set of 8 etchings of pastoral animal subjects, all dated 1652. It is cataloged as **Hollstein 1** (Hollstein VI, p. 29, no. 1), **Bartsch 1**, and **Dutuit IV.1** — the very first entry in Dujardin's cataloged etched oeuvre. Dujardin both made and published this series himself.

## Visual Analysis of the Print

The image depicts an Italianate stone wall — weathered and crumbling — serving as the central plane for the artist's inscription. The wall occupies most of the composition. On the left, a rocky outcrop with vegetation; on the right, a large tree trunk with leafy branches framing the upper right. At the bottom center, a stepped wall fountain: a small water spout with a cross-shaped tap pours into a rectangular stone basin, from which a second spout feeds a lower basin. The ground around the fountain is rough and sandy.

The inscription is etched into the wall surface:

> **K · DV · JARDIN fe.**
> **et Excud. 1652**
> **A · D ·**

At the lower right corner, the plate number **"1"** is visible, confirming this as the first plate of a series.

The etching technique is clearly visible: free, sketchy lines with characteristic acid-bitten quality, particularly in the foliage and sky. The horizontal hatching in the sky and the loose crosshatching on the wall are typical of 17th-century Dutch etching, distinctly different from the controlled burin work of engraving.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. SERIES IDENTIFICATION MISSING: Title page for "Dieren" series

This print is not just a standalone "title page with fountain" — it is the **frontispiece for Dujardin's etching series "Dieren" (Animals)**, consisting of 8 plates (Hollstein 1–8), all dated 1652. The series includes pastoral subjects: two mules, a cow and calf, donkeys, sheep and goats — all set in Italianate landscapes. This essential context for understanding the print is entirely absent from Teylers' catalog. The Metropolitan Museum of Art titles it: *"Frontispiece with stepped fountain; a stone wall with water spout pouring water into a rectilinear basin, from which a second spout pours water into a smaller rectilinear basin, flanked by tree branches."*

### 2. CREATOR ROLE INCORRECT: "graveur" should be "etser"

Teylers lists Dujardin's role as **"graveur" (engraver)**. This is incorrect. Dujardin was a **painter and etcher**, never an engraver. Every major museum — the Met, the National Gallery of Art (Washington), the Art Institute of Chicago, the V&A — consistently catalogs his prints as **etchings**. His prints show the free, sketchy line quality characteristic of etching (acid-bitten lines through a ground), not the precise, controlled lines of burin engraving. The technique field correctly says "ets" (etching), making the "graveur" role self-contradictory with the technique classification.

### 3. Karel Dujardin birth year: 1622 vs. c. 1626

Teylers lists "(1622-1678)." This remains debated among institutions:
- **1622**: Used by Met, Britannica, and some older sources
- **c. 1626**: Used by National Gallery London, Getty Museum, Art UK, and based on a baptism record of 27 September 1626

The NGA uses the compromise **"c. 1622"**. Modern scholarship increasingly favors c. 1626 (Jennifer Kilian's catalogue raisonné). If born c. 1626, Dujardin would have been about 26 when he made this series in 1652.

### 4. Hollstein and Bartsch references missing

This print is documented as:
- **Hollstein VI, p. 29, no. 1** (state 4 in Met's impression)
- **Bartsch 1**
- **Dutuit IV.1**

None of these standard catalog references appear in Teylers' record.

### 5. The "A.D." inscription explained

The "A.D." in the inscription stands for **Anno Domini** ("in the year of our Lord"), a standard formalization on 17th-century title pages. The full inscription reads: "K. Du Jardin made and published [this], in the year of our Lord 1652."

### 6. Multiple states exist — state not identified

The Met's impression is cataloged as **state 4** of this print (Hollstein VI.29.1.4). The plate number "1" at lower right was added in a later state. Teylers does not identify which state their impression represents. The presence of the plate number "1" (visible in the image) indicates this is not the earliest state.

### 8. Related object reference unexplained

The catalog references "Titelpagina, catalog record 7614" but provides no context for what this related object is. It may be another state of the same print, or an alternative title page.

### 9. The fountain motif in context

The stepped stone fountain with Italianate architectural character sets the tone for the pastoral, Italian-influenced animal subjects that follow in the series. Dujardin studied and worked in Italy, and his etchings reflect the warm light and rustic architecture of the Italian countryside. The fountain also provides a practical flat stone surface on which to inscribe the publication details — a conventional design solution for printmaking frontispieces.
