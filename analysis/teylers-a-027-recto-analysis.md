## Object Description

A red chalk drawing with white heightening on paper by **Michelangelo Buonarroti** (1475–1564), c. 1511, depicting a study for one of the **ignudi** (nude male figures) on the Sistine Chapel ceiling, most probably near the Persian Sibyl / Separation of the Earth from the Waters. Described as "probably the best-preserved example" of Michelangelo's Sistine preparatory studies. The verso (A 027 verso) contains swift sketches of figures surrounding God the Father in the **Creation of Adam** — connecting this single sheet to two of the most significant Sistine compositions. Part of approximately 22–25 Michelangelo drawings at Teylers, forming one of the **top five collections of Michelangelo drawings in the world**, acquired in 1790 via Willem Anne Lestevenon from the Odescalchi heirs in Rome for 10,000 guilders. Designated one of Teylers' **50 Topstukken**.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. SPECIFIC IGNUDO NOT IDENTIFIED

The title states "an Ignudo" (een ignudo) without further specification. Scholarship has connected this study to the ignudi **above the Persian Sibyl**, in the zone of the **Separation of the Earth from the Waters**. The two ignudi flanking this panel show dynamic twisting poses consistent with this drawing. The catalog's generic title loses this specificity.

### 2. VERSO NOT GIVEN EQUAL PROMINENCE

The recto/verso distinction in the catalog gives no indication that A 027 **verso** contains equally significant material: swift sketches of **figures surrounding God the Father in the Creation of Adam**. A single sheet thus connects Michelangelo's working process for two of the most celebrated Sistine compositions simultaneously. The verso material deserves explicit mention in the catalog record.

### 3. COLLECTION SIZE AND GLOBAL SIGNIFICANCE NOT STATED

Teylers holds approximately **22–25 Michelangelo drawings** — one of the **top five collections in the world**:

| Institution | Holdings (approx.) |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Casa Buonarroti, Florence | ~200 sheets |
| British Museum, London | ~90 drawings |
| Ashmolean Museum, Oxford | Third largest globally |
| Teylers Museum, Haarlem | ~22–25 drawings |

The catalog does not contextualise the global significance of Teylers' Michelangelo holdings, nor that these drawings are considered among the **best-preserved Michelangelo drawings in the world**.

### 4. "DI BUONA ROTI" INSCRIPTION NOT EXPLAINED

The inscription "di buona Roti" is a partial rendering of "di Buonarroti." Paul Joannides has identified a corpus of drawings bearing this annotation and named the unknown writer the **"Bona Roti collector"** — a 16th- or 17th-century figure who systematically attributed drawings in the Christina/Odescalchi albums to Michelangelo. Recognising this inscription as belonging to a documented attribution system is important provenance evidence. The catalog transcribes it but does not contextualise it.

### 5. "PHI" INSCRIPTION NOT CROSS-REFERENCED WITH LUGT

The catalog records the "phi" inscription without identifying the collector. This mark should be cross-referenced against Frits Lugt, *Les marques de collections de dessins & d'estampes* (1921, expanded editions) to determine the collector's identity and thereby strengthen the provenance chain.

### 6. PROVENANCE CHAIN INCOMPLETE — QUEEN CHRISTINA ABSENT

The catalog states "Purchased from Odescalchi heirs, Rome, 1790" but omits the crucial earlier provenance through **Queen Christina of Sweden** (1626–1689) and **Cardinal Decio Azzolino** (d. 1689). The full chain is:

1. Michelangelo's studio (c. 1511)
2. Passed through several hands to Queen Christina of Sweden (Rome)
3. Cardinal Decio Azzolino (bequeathed 1689)
4. Marchese Pompeo Azzolino (inherited)
5. Principe Livio Odescalchi, Duca di Bracciano (purchased c. 1692–1696)
6. Odescalchi heirs, Rome
7. Purchased by Willem Anne Lestevenon on behalf of Teylers, 1790

This is one of the most distinguished provenance chains in the art world — omitting Queen Christina from the public-facing catalog is a significant missed opportunity.

### 7. WHO ACQUIRED THEM FOR TEYLERS — NOT NAMED

The catalog says "purchased from Odescalchi heirs" but does not name **Willem Anne Lestevenon van Berkenrode** (1750–1830), the Dutch politician and member of Teylers Second Society who negotiated the purchase in Rome. The sum of **10,000 guilders** for approximately 1,700 drawings was considered astronomically large at the time. This is the most important acquisition in Teylers' history.

### 8. MEDIUM — "GRAPHITE" REQUIRES CLARIFICATION

The catalog records "red chalk, graphite, white heightening." The presence of **graphite** (as distinct from lead stylus/metalpoint) in a c. 1511 Michelangelo drawing is notable — graphite pencils as we know them were not in common use until the late 16th century. If graphite is confirmed by technical analysis, it is significant. If the "graphite" refers to a lead or black chalk underdrawing or stylus underdrawing, the terminology should be corrected. This requires clarification in the catalog.
