## Object Description

A six-volume folio **composite atlas** (*atlas factice*) published in Amsterdam by the firm of Reinier Ottens (1698–1750) and Josua Ottens (1704–1765), c.1725–1750. The Ottens brothers were among the most prominent map publishers of 18th-century Amsterdam, heirs to the firm founded by their father Joachim Ottens (1663–1719). The *Atlas Major* is not a conventionally authored work with a fixed text: it belongs to the tradition of the made-to-order luxury atlas, assembled on commission from maps by many different cartographers (F. de Wit, G. de L'Isle, H. Jaillot, N. Visscher, J.B. Homann, G. van Keulen, P. Schenk, and many others), all engraved and hand-colored. No two copies are identical. Known configurations range from 2 to 15 volumes. The Teylers copy is six volumes.

## Key Findings

### 1. JOSUA OTTENS OMITTED AS CO-AUTHOR — CONTRADICTED BY OWN RECORD

The catalog lists only Reinier Ottens (1698–1750) as author, yet the **publisher field in the same record** correctly names both brothers: "Regnerum & Josuam Ottens." The title page of the atlas itself reads "Apud Regnerum & Josuam Ottens." Every institutional catalog that records this atlas (UW-Madison OCLC 9910188685302121, Harvard, Bonhams, Sanderus, Geographicus, Aradergalleries) attributes it to Reinier and Josua Ottens jointly. **Josua Ottens (1704–1765) is systematically erased from the author field in a record that simultaneously names him in the publisher field.** This is an internal contradiction.

### 2. COMPOSITE/FACTICE NATURE NOT RECORDED

The catalog classifies the object as "boek" (book), implying a conventionally authored and published work. This is fundamentally misleading. The *Atlas Major* is an **atlas factice** — a custom-assembled collection of maps from many different cartographers, bound to order for wealthy buyers. Individual copies vary widely in map count, selection, sequence, and volume configuration. No two copies are identical. This defining characteristic is entirely absent from the record, meaning a catalog user cannot understand:
- Why this copy may differ from other copies of the "same" work
- Why no authoritative edition statement exists
- Why the maps originate from dozens of cartographers spanning decades

### 3. FULL LATIN TITLE NOT RECORDED

The catalog title is simply "Atlas major." The full title is: *Atlas major cum generales omnium totius orbis regnorum rerumpubl atque insularum tum particulares praecipuarum in iis provinciarum ducatuum comitatuum ceterarumque minorum regionum ac divisionum tabulas geographicas continens ex optimis ac novissimis quibusque variorum auctorum tabulis collectus* (UW-Madison catalog, OCLC 9910188685302121). The short title creates confusion with Blaeu's entirely different *Atlas Maior* (1662–65), the most famous atlas to bear a similar name.

### 4. Language field absent

No language recorded. Known copies are multilingual (Latin, French, Dutch, English). The catalog note itself uses French, suggesting the Teylers copy is similarly multilingual.

### 5. No provenance

No acquisition history recorded. Given Teylers was founded in 1778 and the atlas dates to 1725–1750, whether this was part of the founding collection or acquired later is historically significant.

### 6. Physical description incomplete

No map count, no geographic coverage statement, no list of contributing cartographers. Comparable six-volume Ottens atlas copies contain 400–500+ hand-colored maps.

### 7. Uninformative note

The note "Les cartes sont gr. et col. sous le même titre" (the maps are engraved and colored under the same title) restates what the illustrations field already says and contributes no information about the composite nature or cartographic provenance.
