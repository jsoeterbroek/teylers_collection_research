## Object Description

This is an extraordinarily rare **gold striking** of a coin normally produced in silver — a quarter guilder (5 stuivers) of the Province of Holland, dated 1692. Gold strikings (*gouden afslagen*) are off-metal presentation pieces produced in tiny numbers (sometimes just one or two) for dignitaries or mint officials, using the standard silver-coin dies. They are among the rarest and most valuable items in Dutch numismatics; comparable gold strikings have sold at auction for **€180,000–€245,000**. The dies were engraved by **Daniël Drappentier** (1643–1714), Engraver General of Holland, West Friesland, Zeeland, and Utrecht, and the coin was produced at the Dordrecht mint under mint master **Mattheus Sonnemans**. It was bequeathed to Teylers in 1896 by **Adriaan Justus Enschedé** — owner of the famous Joh. Enschedé printing firm, printers of Dutch banknotes since 1814.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. EXTREME RARITY NOT INDICATED

The catalog identifies this as a "[gouden afslag]" but gives no indication of its extraordinary rarity or value. Gold strikings of Dutch silver denominations are:

- Produced in **quantities of 1–3 pieces** as presentation or trial pieces
- Among the **rarest items in Dutch numismatics**
- Worth **tens of thousands of euros** at auction — comparable pieces have sold for €180,000–€245,000

This coin may be **unique**. The catalog should note its exceptional numismatic significance.

### 2. THE "ARGENT" CONTRADICTION: A HALLMARK OF AUTHENTICITY

The obverse reads "MO NO **ARGENT** COMIT HOLL" — "New **Silver** Coin of Holland" — yet the coin is gold. The catalog does not explain this. The contradiction is **intentional and expected**: the same dies made for the silver quarter guilder were used without modification to strike this gold piece. The "ARGENT" inscription on a gold coin is actually a **hallmark of authenticity** — it proves the coin was struck from standard silver-denomination dies, confirming it as a genuine off-metal presentation piece.

### 3. INSCRIPTIONS NOT TRANSLATED

**Obverse:** *MO NO ARGENT COMIT HOLL 1692 / 5 S*
= "New Silver Coin of the County of Holland, 1692 / 5 Stuivers"

**Reverse:** *HAC NITIMVR HANC TVEMVR*
= **"On this we rely, this we defend"** — referring to the Bible depicted on the altar. The Dutch Republic draws strength FROM Scripture and commits to DEFENDING it. This was a profoundly political statement: the Republic was founded partly in revolt against Catholic Spain over religious freedom.

### 4. THE DUTCH MAIDEN: SYMBOL NOT EXPLAINED

The reverse depicts the **Hollandse Maagd** (Dutch Maiden) — a national personification of the Netherlands used since the 16th-century Dutch Revolt:

- **Liberty cap on spear** (right hand): The *vrijheidshoed*, referencing the Roman *pileus* given to freed slaves — symbolizing freedom from Spanish tyranny
- **Left arm on Bible on altar**: The Republic's foundation on Protestant faith and religious liberty
- Together: **political freedom and religious freedom** — the twin pillars of the Dutch Republic

On 25 May 1694 (two years after this coin), the States of Holland formally standardized this design for all provincial coinage. The 1692 quarter guilder already uses it, predating the standardization.

### 5. DANIËL DRAPPENTIER: ENGRAVER GENERAL

The catalog lists "Daniël Drappentier (1643–1714)" without context. He was:

- Appointed **Engraver General** (*Generaal Graveur*) of Holland and West Friesland in **1671**, and subsequently of Zeeland and Utrecht
- Responsible for **all coin and medal dies** at those provincial mints
- Engraver to the court of **Stadtholder-King William III**
- Created commemorative medals for the Glorious Revolution (1688) and Anglo-Dutch alliance
- Designer of the **Scheepjesschelling** (ship's shilling)
- Father of **Jan Drappentier**, who succeeded him at the Dordrecht mint

### 6. MATTHEUS SONNEMANS: 37-YEAR TENURE

The catalog lists the mint master without context. Sonnemans served as mint master of Holland at Dordrecht from **1678 to 1715** — a 37-year tenure spanning William III's entire reign. He succeeded his brother-in-law Simon Rodermond, illustrating how mint positions were kept within family networks. His mint mark was a flower symbol (❀).

### 7. ADRIAAN JUSTUS ENSCHEDÉ: THE BANKNOTE DYNASTY

The provenance "Legaat Adriaan Justus Enschedé, 1896" conceals a remarkable story:

**Adriaan Justus Enschedé** (1829–1896) was:
- Owner of **Joh. Enschedé**, which has printed **Dutch banknotes since 1814** and **postage stamps since 1866**
- Haarlem's **City Archivist** from 1857
- Member of **Teylers Tweede Genootschap** from 1876
- In **1888**, on his advice, Teylers appointed its first numismatic curator (**Theodorus Marinus Roest**), who published the museum's first coin catalog (1889)
- Upon his death, bequeathed his entire coin collection to Teylers — forming a core part of the numismatic holdings

The **Enschedé dynasty** (founded 1703) is one of the most important printing families in European history. Adriaan Justus's role in professionalizing Teylers' numismatic collection is historically significant and unmentioned.

### 8. THE "5 S" VALUE NOT EXPLAINED

The "5 S" flanking the coat of arms = **5 Stuivers = 1/4 Guilder (Gulden)**. In the Dutch Republic: 1 Guilder = 20 Stuivers, so 5 Stuivers = one quarter. The silver version weighs 3.98 g (Rijksmuseum KOG-MP-1-1562A); this gold version at 6.8943 g is heavier due to gold's higher density.
