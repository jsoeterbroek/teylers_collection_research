## Object Description

This is an 18th-century inclined plane demonstration apparatus (*hellend vlak*), built after the design of Willem Jacob 's Gravesande (1688–1742), the pioneering Leiden professor who introduced experimental demonstration-based physics teaching to continental Europe. The instrument was made by the Amsterdam instrument-making partnership of J. Kampman and Adam Steitz, active in the third quarter of the 18th century. It belongs to the tradition of Newtonian physics demonstration apparatus that 's Gravesande codified in his landmark textbook *Physices Elementa Mathematica* (1720).

## Visual Analysis of the Object

The photograph shows a finely crafted physics demonstration apparatus consisting of:

- **A mahogany base platform** resting on four shaped mahogany feet, forming a stable triangular foundation
- **An inclined mahogany beam** rising from the base at an adjustable angle, supported by a **brass quadrant arc** with brass hinge brackets — this arc allows the angle of incline to be set and measured
- **A brass cylindrical roller** (or weighted carriage) sitting on the inclined surface, connected by a fine cord or wire
- **A vertical brass arm** extending upward from the top end of the base, terminating in a **brass pulley wheel** at the apex
- **A cord** running from the roller on the incline, over the pulley at the top, to a **hanging brass cylindrical weight** suspended vertically
- **Brass fittings** throughout: hinge plates, screws, the graduated arc, pulley mount, and decorative elements

The construction quality is high, with dark mahogany showing the rich patina of age and polished brass fittings. The overall form is elegant and designed for lecture-hall demonstration — large enough to be visible to an audience while being stable on a demonstration table.

**How it works:** The apparatus demonstrates the mechanical advantage of the inclined plane. A weight on the slope is connected via a cord and pulley to a freely hanging weight. By varying the angle of the incline (using the graduated brass arc), the demonstrator shows that the force needed to hold or move a body on an inclined plane depends on the sine of the angle of inclination. When the hanging weight exactly balances the component of gravitational force pulling the roller down the slope, the system is in equilibrium. This directly demonstrates Newton's resolution of forces and the relationship: F = mg sin(θ).

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. WILLEM JACOB 'S GRAVESANDE: PIVOTAL FIGURE IN PHYSICS EDUCATION

The catalog notes "naar Willem Jacob 's Gravesande" (after Willem Jacob 's Gravesande) but provides no information about who he was. 's Gravesande (1688–1742) was one of the most influential figures in 18th-century physics:

- **Born** 26 September 1688 in 's-Hertogenbosch, died 28 February 1742 in Leiden
- **Appointed** professor of mathematics and astronomy at Leiden University in **1717**, where he taught until his death
- **Pioneered** the method of teaching physics through experimental demonstration — he was the first professor in continental Europe to systematically use physical apparatus in lectures to demonstrate Newtonian principles
- **Published** *Physices Elementa Mathematica, Experimentis Confirmata* ("Mathematical Elements of Natural Philosophy, Confirmed by Experiments") in **1720** in Latin, with an English translation by J.T. Desaguliers appearing in 1720–1721. The work went through multiple editions (3rd ed. 1742, posthumous 5th ed. 1747)
- **Attracted students from across Europe** to Leiden, including Voltaire, Albrecht von Haller, and connections to Émilie du Châtelet
- **Famous experiments:** His brass ball-drop experiments into clay (1722) provided early experimental evidence for what we now call kinetic energy (vis viva, proportional to mv²), contributing to the vis viva controversy between Leibnizian and Newtonian dynamics
- **Designed hundreds of demonstration instruments** in close collaboration with the Leiden instrument maker **Jan van Musschenbroek** (1687–1748), which were meticulously illustrated in his textbook with 62+ folding copper-engraved plates

The Teylers catalog should identify 's Gravesande as "Professor of Mathematics and Astronomy at Leiden University (1717–1742), pioneer of experimental physics teaching."

### 2. THE TEXTBOOK: *PHYSICES ELEMENTA MATHEMATICA* (1720)

's Gravesande's textbook was the first continental European textbook of physics on Newtonian principles. Key facts:

- **Full Latin title:** *Physices Elementa Mathematica, Experimentis Confirmata. Sive Introductio ad Philosophiam Newtonianam*
- **English title:** *Mathematical Elements of Natural Philosophy, Confirm'd by Experiments: or, An Introduction to Sir Isaac Newton's Philosophy*
- **First edition:** Leiden, 1720 (2 volumes, Latin)
- **English translation:** by John Theophilus Desaguliers (1683–1744), published London 1720–1721
- **Later editions:** 3rd edition (Leiden, 1742); 5th edition (London, 1747, translated by Desaguliers's son)
- **Plates:** The work contained **62 folding copper-engraved plates** illustrating demonstration apparatus — these plates served as blueprints for instrument makers across Europe to reproduce 's Gravesande's designs
- **Structure:** Volume 1 covered mechanics and fluid mechanics; Volume 2 covered astronomy and other topics
- **The inclined plane** is treated in 's Gravesande's discussion of simple machines and the resolution of forces in the mechanics section of Volume 1. The apparatus shown in the plates served as the model for copies made by instrument makers throughout the 18th century — including the Teylers instrument

The phrase "naar 's Gravesande" in the catalog title means this instrument was built as a **copy of the design illustrated in the textbook**, not by 's Gravesande himself (who died in 1742, before the instrument's production period begins).

### 3. THE MAKERS: J. KAMPMAN AND ADAM STEITZ — AMSTERDAM INSTRUMENT-MAKING PARTNERSHIP

Both J. Kampman and Adam Steitz are documented in the **DWC/KNAW database of Scientific Instrument Makers in the Netherlands** (the database continuing Maria Rooseboom's foundational 1950 work *Bijdrage tot de Geschiedenis der Instrumentmakerskunst in de Noordelijke Nederlanden*). However, detailed biographical entries are limited. What can be established:

**Adam Steitz:**
- Active in **Amsterdam**, mid-18th century
- Built a **Gregorian reflector telescope** around **1750** for the wealthy Amsterdam merchant **Jacobus van der Wall**. This telescope (mirror diameter 23 cm) was recognized as very good for its time. After Van der Wall's death in 1782, it was bequeathed to the **Leiden Observatory**, where it remains today (alternating display between the J.H. Oort Building and Museum Boerhaave)
- Steitz was therefore a versatile instrument maker producing both optical instruments (telescopes) and physics demonstration apparatus

**J. Kampman:**
- Active in **Amsterdam**, mid-18th century
- Listed in the DWC/KNAW scientific instrument makers index
- Worked in partnership with Adam Steitz

**Their joint production for Teylers Museum is substantial.** Multiple instruments in the Teylers Fysisch Kabinet bear the attribution "J. Kampman, Adam Steitz Amsterdam" with the same date range of 1750–1774:

This pattern reveals that **Kampman and Steitz produced an entire suite of 's Gravesande demonstration apparatus** — a coordinated acquisition of physics teaching instruments, all built in Amsterdam to 's Gravesande's published designs. The Teylers catalog treats each instrument in isolation without noting this significant pattern.

### 4. DATING 1750–1774: CAN IT BE NARROWED?

The date range 1750–1774 appears to be based on the **active period of the makers** rather than a specific acquisition date. Several observations:

- Adam Steitz's telescope for Van der Wall is dated "around 1750," confirming activity from at least that date
- The Teylers Museum opened in **1784** with Martinus van Marum as director; the Fysisch Kabinet was established from that date
- Van Marum is known to have purchased "expensive instruments and even whole scientific collections from personal estates" using funds from both Teylers and the Koninklijke Hollandsche Maatschappij der Wetenschappen
- The instruments could have been purchased as a **batch from an existing physics cabinet** rather than commissioned individually

The date range **cannot be narrowed with certainty** from available sources, but the simultaneous dating of multiple Kampman & Steitz instruments suggests they were part of a **single collection** that Teylers later acquired, possibly from a private physics cabinet or lecture-demonstration collection.

### 5. PHYSICAL PRINCIPLES DEMONSTRATED

The catalog states merely "Toestel ter demonstratie van de beweging langs het hellend vlak" (device for demonstrating motion along the inclined plane). The apparatus actually demonstrates several fundamental principles:

1. **Resolution of gravitational force:** A gravitational force mg can be decomposed into a component parallel to the incline (mg sin θ) and a component perpendicular to it (mg cos θ)
2. **Mechanical advantage of the simple machine:** The inclined plane is one of Archimedes' five simple machines — a small force applied over a longer distance can balance a larger gravitational force
3. **Newton's Second Law applied to inclines:** The net acceleration of a body on a frictionless incline is a = g sin θ
4. **Galileo's law of acceleration:** Building on Galileo's famous 1603 inclined plane experiments (published 1638 in *Discorsi*), the distance traveled is proportional to the square of the elapsed time: d ∝ t²
5. **Equilibrium conditions:** By balancing the hanging weight against the incline component, the instrument demonstrates static equilibrium and the conditions under which motion begins

### 6. CONNECTION TO TEYLERS MUSEUM: THE FYSISCH KABINET

The "FK" prefix stands for **Fysisch Kabinet** (Physics Cabinet), Teylers' collection of scientific instruments. Key historical context:

- **Pieter Teyler van der Hulst** (1702–1778), a wealthy Haarlem merchant-banker, bequeathed his fortune to establish the **Teylers Stichting** (foundation)
- **Martinus van Marum** (1750–1837) became the first director of Teylers Museum in **1784** and founded three collections: the **Fysisch Kabinet** (Physics Cabinet), the Paleontological-Mineralogical Cabinet, and the Library
- Van Marum assembled approximately **350 demonstration models** for the physics collection
- He purchased instruments from various sources, including Amsterdam makers and entire private collections
- Van Marum gave public physics lectures from **1777 to 1803**
- The most famous instrument he commissioned was the **large electrostatic generator** (1784), built by John Cuthbertson, an Amsterdam-based English instrument maker
- Later, **Hendrik Lorentz** (Nobel Prize in Physics, 1902) served as Curator of Teylers Physics Cabinet from **1910 to 1928**
- The instruments are displayed in the **Instrument Room**, built 1880–1885 as an extension to the original Oval Room (1784)

The Kampman & Steitz instruments predate the museum's founding (1784), suggesting Van Marum acquired them from a pre-existing collection.

### 7. MUSEUM BOERHAAVE COMPARISON

Museum Boerhaave (Rijksmuseum Boerhaave) in Leiden holds the original **Leiden Physics Cabinet** — the collection that began with Burchard de Volder's Theatrum Physicum (1675) and was vastly expanded when Leiden University purchased 's Gravesande's private instrument collection in **1742** (the year of his death). Key comparison:

- The Leiden collection was cataloged by **Claude August Crommelin** (1878–1965) in **1926**, with a revised English edition in **1951**
- A comprehensive modern catalog was published by **Peter de Clercq**: *The Leiden Cabinet of Physics: A Descriptive Catalogue* (Museum Boerhaave Communication 271), describing approximately **295 instruments** dated ca. 1660–1800
- Museum Boerhaave explicitly displays models of **Archimedes' simple machines including the inclined plane**, and the 's Gravesande/Musschenbroek room is a highlight of the museum
- The original 's Gravesande instruments were built by **Jan van Musschenbroek** (1687–1748) in Leiden, who worked in extraordinarily close collaboration with 's Gravesande — designing and manufacturing instruments to 's Gravesande's specifications

**Critical distinction:** The Teylers instrument (FK 0027) is a **later copy** made in Amsterdam by Kampman & Steitz, not an original by Jan van Musschenbroek from 's Gravesande's own collection. The Boerhaave instruments are the *originals* (or very early examples); the Teylers instruments are *reproductions* based on the published plate engravings in 's Gravesande's textbook. This is common — 's Gravesande's plates were explicitly intended to allow instrument makers across Europe to reproduce his apparatus. The Kampman & Steitz instruments represent the **dissemination of Leiden's experimental physics tradition** to other Dutch cities and institutions.

### 8. THE MAKER RELATIONSHIP: MUSSCHENBROEK vs. KAMPMAN & STEITZ

The Teylers catalog does not explain why 's Gravesande designed the instrument but Kampman & Steitz built it. The historical chain is:

1. **'s Gravesande designed** the demonstration apparatus in collaboration with **Jan van Musschenbroek** in Leiden (ca. 1717–1742)
2. **'s Gravesande published** the designs as copper-engraved plates in *Physices Elementa Mathematica* (1720 and later editions)
3. The published plates served as **open-source blueprints** for any skilled instrument maker to reproduce
4. **Kampman & Steitz in Amsterdam** reproduced the designs (1750–1774), likely as a complete set for a physics demonstration cabinet
5. **Teylers Museum** acquired the instruments, probably through Martinus van Marum after 1784

This represents the 18th-century equivalent of technology transfer through published literature.
