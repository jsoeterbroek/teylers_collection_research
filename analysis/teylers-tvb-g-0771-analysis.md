## Object Description

This is a portrait etching by Karel Dujardin (c. 1626–1678), depicting the Amsterdam poet and glazier Jan Vos (1612–1667) half-length, facing slightly right, holding a rolled paper or parchment in his left hand. The print was created as the frontispiece portrait for the second volume of Vos's collected poems, *Alle de gedichten* (Amsterdam, 1662). Below the portrait is a four-line laudatory poem by Joost van den Vondel. The print exists in at least 3–4 states; later states include biographical text about Vos's role in the 1648 Peace of Munster celebrations.

## Visual Analysis of the Print

The image shows Jan Vos in a half-length portrait, turned slightly to the right. He has long, dark, curly hair falling past his shoulders. He wears a dark mantle or cloak over a white collar. In his left hand he holds a rolled document (paper or parchment), a standard attribute for poets in 17th-century portraiture. A small dark cap (calotte/keppeltje) appears to sit atop his head, partially obscured by the voluminous hair. The background is crosshatched.

At top left, the artist's signature reads: **"KDu Jardin F"** (Karel Dujardin fecit).

Below the portrait image, the Vondel poem reads in full:

> *Zoo spant Natuur door Vos een regenboogh van dichten,*
> *Of bogen van triomfe, en leert tooneelen stichten.*
> *Wat hoortge, als hy een wijs op vrede en oorlogh zet?*
> *Een stem, gewrongen door een boghtige trompet.*
> *J. v. Vondel.*

Translation: "Thus Nature, through Vos [Fox], spans a rainbow of poems, / Or triumphal arches, and teaches to establish theaters. / What do you hear, when he sets a tune to peace and war? / A voice, wrung through a curved trumpet. — J. v. Vondel."

Below this, biographical text in a later state reads: "He was appointed on June 5, 1648 to design the tableaux vivants for the Peace of Munster, and died in July 1667. The portrait of the Poet Vos."

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. CREATOR BIRTH YEAR INCORRECT: 1622 should be c. 1626

Teylers lists Karel Dujardin as "(1622-1678)". Current art-historical scholarship has revised his birth year to **c. 1626** (baptized 27 September 1626 in Amsterdam). The National Gallery London, the Rijksmuseum, and Encyclopaedia Britannica all use c. 1626. The old date of 1622 comes from outdated 19th-century sources. This error affects the production date range as well.

### 2. TECHNIQUE PARTIALLY ANACHRONISTIC: "stippelgravure" did not exist yet

Teylers catalogs the technique as **"ets en stippelgravure" (etching and stipple engraving)**. Stipple engraving as a distinct, named printmaking technique was not developed until the **mid-18th century** (pioneered by Francesco Bartolozzi in the 1760s). Karel Dujardin died in 1678. While some dot-work and stippling effects appear in the print (common in 17th-century etching for tonal modeling of skin), applying the term "stippelgravure" to a 1662 print is anachronistic. The Rijksmuseum correctly catalogs this simply as **"ets" (etching)**.

### 3. PRODUCTION DATE RANGE MISLEADINGLY BROAD

Teylers gives "1642-1678, ca." — essentially Dujardin's entire career span. The print was created as the frontispiece for the second volume of Jan Vos's *Alle de gedichten*, published in Amsterdam in **1662**. The Rijksmuseum dates it **1652–1662**; the Philadelphia Museum of Art dates it **c. 1661–1662**. A date of c. 1661–1662 is far more precise and historically accurate. Additionally, if Dujardin was born c. 1626, a start date of 1642 (age 16) is improbably early.

### 4. "Graveur" role description is inaccurate

Teylers lists Dujardin's role as **"graveur" (engraver)**. He was an **etser (etcher)**, not an engraver. Etching and engraving are fundamentally different intaglio techniques. Dujardin was known primarily as a painter and etcher. His 52 cataloged prints are etchings, not engravings.

### 5. Hollstein and Bartsch references missing

This print is documented in the standard reference catalogs as **Hollstein Dutch 52** and **Bartsch 52**. These references are absent from Teylers' record. The Rijksmuseum further specifies it as Hollstein Dutch 52-a-2(3) for state 2 of 3.

### 6. State not identified

The print exists in at least **3–4 states**. The presence of the biographical text about the Peace of Munster and Vos's death ("Hij wierd den 5e juny 1648...") suggests this is a **later state** (state 3 or later), as this information was added after the initial publication as frontispiece. The Rijksmuseum holds state 2 (without biographical text) and state 4 (a later impression from the old plate). Teylers does not identify which state this impression represents.

### 7. Context as frontispiece not mentioned

The print was created specifically as the **frontispiece portrait for volume 2 of *Alle de gedichten van den poëet Jan Vos*** (Amsterdam, Jacob Lescailje, 1662). This essential publication context is entirely absent from Teylers' record.

### 8. The Vondel poem attribution deserves elaboration

The inscription is a poem by **Joost van den Vondel** (1587–1679), the greatest Dutch poet of the Golden Age. The fact that Vondel — who represented a very different theatrical aesthetic (classical restraint vs. Vos's spectacle-driven style) — wrote a laudatory verse for Vos's portrait is culturally significant. Vondel also wrote a separate, better-known poem about Vos: "Bezie de gaauwste Vos, en d'yvrigst' in zijn plicht..." which praises Vos as self-taught.

### 9. "Glazenier" keyword is imprecise

Teylers uses **"glazenier"**, which in modern Dutch can imply a stained-glass artist. Jan Vos was a **glazemaker** (glazier/window-fitter) — he fitted and repaired windows. From 1652 he served as **stadsglazenmaker** (municipal glazier) of Amsterdam, providing all windows for the new City Hall on the Dam (now the Royal Palace). He was not a stained-glass artist. Period sources consistently use "glazemaker" or "glaslooder."

### 10. Jan Vos's full biography adds context

Jan Vos (baptized 4 March 1612, Amsterdam — buried 11 July 1667, Amsterdam) was far more than a "dichter" (poet):

- **Glazemaker** on Kalverstraat (inherited family workshop)
- **Stadsglazenmaker** of Amsterdam (from 1652)
- **Head of the Amsterdam Schouwburg** (theater) for ~19 years (1647–1652, 1653–1667)
- **Wijnroeier** (wine measurer, from c. 1655)
- **Playwright**: *Aran en Titus* (1641), *Klucht van Oene* (1642), *Medea* (1667)
- Connected to Amsterdam's regent elite: Joan Huydecoper van Maarsseveen (patron), the De Graeff and Bicker families

### 11. The Peace of Munster inscription is historically accurate

The biographical text states Vos was appointed on 5 June 1648 to design the "vertooningen" (tableaux vivants) for the Peace of Munster celebrations. This is confirmed: the Peace of Munster was proclaimed on 5 June 1648, and Jan Vos (alongside Samuel Coster and Geeraardt Brandt) organized the theatrical celebrations on the Dam. The death date "July, 1667" is also confirmed (buried 11 July 1667).

### 12. Teding van Berkhout collection provenance

The "TvB" prefix identifies this print as part of the collection of **Jhr. Hendrik Teding van Berkhout** (1879–1969), a museum director and art collector. The collection comprised approximately 5,000 prints and 770 watercolors/drawings, donated to Teylers in 2001 by the Stichting Jhr. H. Teding van Berkhout (via S.F. barones van Hoëvell-Teding van Berkhout).
