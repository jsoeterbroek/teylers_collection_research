## Object Description

Oil painting on canvas (52.1 × 41.4 cm, signed and dated 1825) by Dutch painter and actor **Johannes Rienksz. Jelgerhuis** (1770–1836). The painting depicts the **choir of the Nieuwe Kerk (New Church) in Delft**, looking toward the monumental mausoleum of **Willem I, Prince of Orange** (William the Silent, 1533–1584), designed by **Hendrick de Keyser** (1614–1621) and completed by his son **Pieter de Keyser** in 1623. The painting belongs to the tradition of Dutch topographical church-interior painting stretching from the 17th-century Delft School (Houckgeest, De Witte, Van Vliet) to 19th-century Romanticism. Staffage figures include a man and young boy with a dog. The canvas was painted in the year Jelgerhuis exhibited in Haarlem. The Rijksmuseum holds a related chalk/watercolor drawing dated 1824 (RP-T-00-1820) that appears to be a direct preparatory study for this painting, and a second related work from July 1819 (RP-T-1921-334).

## Key Findings

### 1. CREATOR NAME INCOMPLETE — CONTRADICTS THE PAINTING'S OWN INSCRIPTION

The catalog records the creator as "Johannes Jelgerhuis" — omitting the patronymic **Rienksz.** (son of Rienk). The painting's own inscription reads *"Jelgerhuis / **Rz** / 1825"* — the "Rz" being the abbreviation of Rienksz. The catalog is thus inconsistent with the object it describes. Every authoritative source (Scheen's *Lexicon*, Rijksmuseum, Wikipedia, Wikidata) uses **Johannes Rienksz. Jelgerhuis** or "Johannes Jelgerhuis Rz." as the standard form. The catalog omits a part of the artist's name that is literally written on the canvas.

### 2. NO PROVENANCE OR ACQUISITION INFORMATION

No prior ownership, acquisition date, acquisition method, or price is recorded. Teylers began collecting contemporary Dutch paintings in 1821. Jelgerhuis exhibited in Haarlem in 1825 — the same year this canvas is dated — making a direct acquisition at or after that exhibition plausible. The complete absence of any acquisition record is a significant gap for a public museum catalog.

### 3. NO DESCRIPTION OF WHAT THE PAINTING DEPICTS

No description field. A researcher consulting only the catalog record cannot determine what part of the Nieuwe Kerk is shown, that the dominant subject is the Hendrick/Pieter de Keyser mausoleum of William the Silent, or that there are staffage figures. The subject tags partially compensate but a prose description is entirely absent.

### 4. MAUSOLEUM DESIGNER NOT RECORDED

The painting's primary subject — the mausoleum of William the Silent — was designed by **Hendrick de Keyser** (commissioned 1614, work begun 1615, De Keyser died 1621) and completed by his son **Pieter de Keyser** in 1623. This is the most prominent object in the composition yet its designer, date, and context (one of the most important funerary monuments in Dutch art history) are entirely absent from the catalog record.

### 5. NO EXHIBITION HISTORY

No exhibition history recorded despite the work appearing in at least six art-historical publications. Jelgerhuis exhibited regularly in Haarlem, Amsterdam, and The Hague between 1814–1834 (per Scheen's *Lexicon*).

### 6. Related preparatory drawings at Rijksmuseum not cross-referenced

The Rijksmuseum holds a chalk/watercolor drawing of the same subject by Jelgerhuis dated **1824** (RP-T-00-1820) — a year before this painting — which appears to be a direct preparatory study. A second Rijksmuseum work dated July **1819** (RP-T-1921-334) shows the same view. These related works are not cross-referenced in the catalog.

### 7. Creator's career as actor entirely omitted

Jelgerhuis was one of the most celebrated actors of his generation in the Netherlands, a leading performer at the Amsterdam Stadsschouwburg from 1808, and the author of the influential *Theoretische lessen over de Gesticulatie en Mimiek* (1827). This dual identity as painter-actor is a significant biographical fact entirely absent.

### 8. Art-historical context absent

The 17th-century Delft School (Houckgeest, De Witte, Van Vliet) repeatedly depicted this same view of the Nieuwe Kerk's choir with William's tomb. Jelgerhuis's 1825 canvas is a Romantic revival of that tradition. Neither the 17th-century precedent nor the 19th-century Romantic context is mentioned.

### 9. Duplicate subject tags for William the Silent

The catalog lists both "Willem de Zwijger" and "Willem I Prins van Oranje" as separate subject tags — these are the same person (1533–1584). Neither tag gives his dates.

### 10. Bibliography entries lack authors, dates, and page numbers

Six bibliography titles listed but no author, date, publisher, or page references. For example, "Honderd Jaren Nederlandsche Schilder- en Teekenkunst: De Romantiek" is presumably Jan Veth et al., The Hague, 1925, but the catalog supplies nothing to enable verification.
