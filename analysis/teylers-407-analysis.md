## Object Description

This is the founding catalog of what would become **Rijksmuseum Boerhaave** — the Netherlands' national museum of science history. Written by Claude August Crommelin (1878–1965), adjunct-director of the Kamerlingh Onnes Laboratory in Leiden, it systematically documents the historical collection of 18th-century physics instruments that had been displaced by Heike Kamerlingh Onnes's modern cryogenics equipment. The catalog describes demonstration apparatus from the famous **'s Gravesande–Musschenbroek collection** — instruments used to introduce Newtonian physics to continental Europe. Published in the year of Kamerlingh Onnes's death (1926), it directly led to the founding of the Nederlandsch Historisch Natuurwetenschappelijk Museum in 1928, which Albert Einstein visited in its opening year (1931).

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. THIS CATALOG FOUNDED THE NETHERLANDS' NATIONAL SCIENCE MUSEUM

The Teylers record gives no indication of this publication's significance. Crommelin's 1926 catalog was the **foundational act** of what became Rijksmuseum Boerhaave:

- 1926: Crommelin publishes this catalog, documenting instruments languishing in laboratory storage
- **30 August 1928**: He founds the *Nederlandsch Historisch Natuurwetenschappelijk Museum*
- 1931: The museum opens its doors; **Albert Einstein** visits in the opening year
- Crommelin serves as director until 1947/1949
- Today the collection is housed in Rijksmuseum Boerhaave in Leiden

Without this catalog, the 's Gravesande–Musschenbroek collection might have been forgotten.

### 2. CLAUDE AUGUST CROMMELIN: BIOGRAPHY NOT PROVIDED

**Claude August Crommelin** (1878–1965) was far more than a cataloger:

- Studied physics at Leiden University from 1898; doctorate in 1910 on the equation of state of argon
- **Assistant to Kamerlingh Onnes** from 1903; conservator (curator) from 1907; adjunct-director from 1924
- Published 50+ scientific articles on gas physics (1910–1925)
- Secretary-treasurer (1907) then **director** (1938–1945) of the Leidse Instrumentmakersschool (Instrument Makers School), founded by Kamerlingh Onnes in 1901
- Played chamber music in a trio with **Paul Ehrenfest** (piano) and **Albert Einstein** (violin) whenever Einstein visited Leiden
- Part of the Crommelin Huguenot family (related to astronomer Andrew Crommelin, after whom Comet 27P/Crommelin is named)

### 3. THE 'S GRAVESANDE–MUSSCHENBROEK INSTRUMENTS

The catalog documents the historical physics instruments assembled by two pivotal Leiden professors:

- **Willem Jacob 's Gravesande** (1688–1742): Pioneer of demonstration experiments in academic physics teaching; introduced Newtonian physics to the Netherlands and continental Europe
- **Petrus van Musschenbroek** (1692–1761): Inventor of the **Leyden jar** (the first electrical capacitor), the most important electrical instrument of the 18th century

These instruments represent the material foundation of Enlightenment experimental physics. A revised English edition was published in 1951 as *Descriptive catalogue of the physical instruments of the 18th century (including the Collection 's Gravesande–Musschenbroek)*, held by 35 libraries worldwide.

### 4. THE KAMERLINGH ONNES CONNECTION

The catalog exists *because* of Kamerlingh Onnes. When he became professor in 1882, he transformed the Leiden laboratory into a world-leading cryogenics facility:

- **10 July 1908**: First liquefaction of helium (reaching 4.2 K / −269°C)
- **1911**: Discovery of superconductivity
- **1913**: Nobel Prize in Physics

His modernization displaced the older 18th-century instruments into storage. Crommelin, as Onnes's conservator and right-hand man, recognized their historical value and documented them — precisely in the year Onnes died (21 February 1926). The publication date marks a pivotal moment: the formal preservation of the laboratory's past.

### 5. THE LORENTZ–TEYLERS LINK

The catalog connects directly to Teylers Museum through **Hendrik Lorentz** (Nobel Prize 1902):

- Lorentz was professor at Leiden University, colleague of Kamerlingh Onnes and Crommelin
- In **1910**, he became **Curator of Teylers Physics Cabinet** (Fysisch Kabinet) in Haarlem
- He maintained a working laboratory at Teylers Museum from 1909 until his death in 1928
- His successor at Teylers was **Adriaan Fokker**, also a Leiden physicist (photographed at the Kamerlingh Onnes Laboratory in 1926)

Teylers and the Leiden laboratory were thus linked by the same personnel: Lorentz moved between both institutions. This catalog documents the collection that complemented Teylers' own 18,000+ scientific instruments.

### 6. THE 11 PLATES

The catalog's 11 photographic plates document historically significant 18th-century instruments. These images have scholarly value for the history of scientific instrumentation. The catalog does not describe their content.

### 7. ENGLISH EDITION AND LATER SCHOLARSHIP

- **1951**: Crommelin published an expanded English edition (held by 35 WorldCat libraries)
- **1997**: Peter de Clercq published the definitive modern catalog of the same collection: *The Leiden Cabinet of Physics* (Museum Boerhaave Communication 271), describing 295 instruments dated c. 1660–1800

These cross-references are absent from the Teylers record.
