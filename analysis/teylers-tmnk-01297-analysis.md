## Object Description

TMNK 01297 is a large silver engraved medal commemorating one of the most dramatic survival stories in VOC history: the disaster of the ship De Gouden Buys (The Golden Pipe) in 1693. The medal was made in 1695, two years after the event, when one of the two survivors — Lourens Thyszoon Veyselaer — had already died on his return voyage. The other survivor, Daniël Silleman, had published an account of the ordeal in Enkhuizen in 1695, which appears to have directly prompted the medal's commission.

The Gouden Buys departed Enkhuizen on 4 May 1693, bound for the Dutch East Indies (the VOC's trading empire in Asia). During the passage through the tropics, the ship was becalmed for two months near the equator. The combination of heat, thirst, and disease — most likely scurvy and dysentery — killed the great majority of the crew. When the ship finally reached the coast of southern Africa and anchored in Helenabay (St. Helena Bay, approximately 100 km north of what is now Cape Town) on 19 October 1693, only about 12 men remained fit for duty. Of the crew of approximately 190, around 178 had died at sea. Captain Baanman was bedridden. Seven men went ashore in a longboat to seek help. Five of them died in the African wilderness. Two survived: Lourens Thyszoon Veyselaer, who was taken in by the Khoikhoi people and eventually led to the Dutch settlement at the Cape; and Daniël Silleman.

Governor Simon van der Stel organized a rescue mission with four ships. The salvage vessel *Dageraad* recovered treasure chests from the stricken Gouden Buys (which had dragged her anchors and grounded in shallow water) and took Silleman aboard. But the *Dageraad* then foundered in a storm off Robben Island, with the loss of over 200 more lives. Silleman survived by swimming ashore. Both Veyselaer and Silleman ultimately made their way back to the Cape together. Veyselaer died on the homeward voyage. Silleman returned to the Netherlands, where his account was published and the medal was struck in his honor.

The medal is engraved rather than struck — its technique (smooth with file marks and engraved lines) makes it a unique pictorial object rather than a mass-produced coin-type piece. The inscriptions were composed by the Amsterdam jeweler-poet Dirk Schelte (baptized Amsterdam 4 October 1639, buried Amsterdam 26 March 1715). The commissioning body was the VOC Chamber of Enkhuizen — the same chamber from whose port the Gouden Buys had departed. The medal was awarded to Veyselaer (and/or Silleman) as an honor, with a suspension ring for wearing on a chain.

## Key Findings

### 1. OBVERSE AND REVERSE ARE TRANSPOSED

This is a major cataloging error that inverts the medal's primary scene of commemoration.

Teylers describes the **obverse** (front) as the **ship in the bay**, and the **reverse** as the **scene with Veyselaer among the Khoikhoi**.

The Rijksmuseum record for the nearly identical medal (NG-VG-1-4373, a comparable example from the same commission) explicitly identifies:
- **Obverse (voorzijde):** The landscape scene with Veyselaer (a European man) among the Khoikhoi, with a palm tree, elephant, river, and wildlife in the background
- **Reverse (keerzijde):** The VOC sailing ship (De Gouden Buys) and rowing boat in the mountain-ringed bay (Helenabay)

The same assignment appears in the Alamy image catalog description, which describes the "front" as the landscape with Veyselaer and the "back" as the ship.

Visual inspection of the images confirms this identification is consistent with numismatic convention: the **primary narrative scene** — Veyselaer's survival among the Khoikhoi — is the obverse, and the **contextualizing scene** of the ship is the reverse. In Dutch commemorative medals of this period, the more narrative/personal scene typically occupies the obverse, while the maritime/historical scene occupies the reverse.

The Teylers catalog has these sides switched. Every description of the medal's "obverse" and "reverse" in the Teylers record is therefore inverted.

### 2. DIRK SCHELTE MISIDENTIFIED AS THE MAKER OF THE MEDAL

The Teylers catalog lists Dirk Schelte (1639–1715) as the **maker** (primary creator) of the medal. This is incorrect.

Dirk Schelte was an Amsterdam jeweler and poet. His role was to **compose the inscriptions** — the verse texts that run around the circumference of the medal. He did not cut the dies or engrave the metal. The Rijksmuseum explicitly identifies the medal's maker as **"anonymous"** and notes separately that Schelte authored the inscriptional texts.

This distinction is fundamental to medallurgical cataloging. The person who composes inscriptions is an inscription author, not a medailleur. Attributing the medal to Schelte as its maker misrepresents the chain of artistic production and makes it impossible to trace the actual engraver.

### 3. ISSUING AUTHORITY INCORRECTLY IDENTIFIED AS "DUTCH REPUBLIC — HOLLAND PROVINCE"

The Teylers catalog attributes the issuing authority to the Dutch Republic, Holland Province. The correct issuing authority was the **VOC Chamber of Enkhuizen** (Kamer Enkhuizen van de Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie). The Gouden Buys was an Enkhuizen vessel; the survivors returned via Enkhuizen; Silleman published his account in Enkhuizen; and the medal was commissioned by the Enkhuizen chamber.

The "Holland Province" attribution is not entirely wrong — the VOC operated under the States-General and the chambers were provincial bodies — but it misses the specific corporate entity responsible for the commission. The VOC Kamer Enkhuizen is the correct attribution.

### 4. PRODUCTION PLACE "AMSTERDAM (UNCERTAIN)" REQUIRES REASSESSMENT

The catalog gives Amsterdam as the production place with an "uncertain designation." Given that the commissioning body was the VOC Chamber of Enkhuizen, the medal may well have been produced in Enkhuizen rather than Amsterdam. Silleman's account was published in Enkhuizen in 1695. Without documentary evidence of the engraver's location, "Amsterdam" remains unconfirmed. "Enkhuizen (?)" or simply "Netherlands (place uncertain)" would be more accurate.

### 5. INSCRIPTION TRANSCRIPTION — PARTIAL TEXT ONLY

The catalog provides only one of the three circumscription texts. The Rijksmuseum records three separate inscription bands running around the medal's circumference:

1. Birth and departure details: "Louwrens Thijsse geboren den 25 Maart 1673. Met 't Schip de Goude Buijs..." [birth date, departure from Enkhuizen 4 May 1693]
2. The survival at Helenabay: "Heleensbay bergd de Goude Buys, t'wyl God Hem spaard, voor 't wild gespuys..." [the main narrative]
3. The commemoration: "Gedaghtenis van 't Ramp gevaar, Van Lourens Tysse Veijselaar. Daar Hij, en Sill'man 't leeven hielen van tweemaal honderd min tien zielen"

The Teylers transcription appears to conflate or truncate these three separate text bands into a single continuous string. The first inscription band (giving Veyselaer's birth date and departure details) is missing from the Teylers record entirely.

### 6. DEATH TOLL IN INSCRIPTION: "TWEEMAAL HONDERD MIN TIEN ZIELEN" (190 SOULS)

The inscription states that Veyselaer and Silleman survived "van tweemaal honderd min tien zielen" — literally "of two hundred minus ten souls," i.e., 190 people. The Teylers description says approximately 190 deaths. The inscription refers to the crew of the Gouden Buys as 190 souls total, of whom all but two (or a handful) perished. The number does not account for additional deaths in the Dageraad disaster. The catalog does not explain this arithmetic, which is important for understanding the medal's commemorative text.

### 7. TECHNIQUE: "ENGRAVED WITH FILE MARKS" IS UNUSUAL AND SIGNIFICANT

The medal's technique — engraving on a smooth, filed silver disc rather than die-striking — is highly unusual for a commemorative medal of this period. Most Dutch historical medals were struck (geslagen). An engraved medal is essentially a unique or very-small-edition object, more akin to a personal commission than a public issue. This has significant implications for the medal's rarity and the question of how many examples were made. The Teylers record notes the technique but does not comment on its rarity or implications.

### 8. BIOGRAPHICAL DATES FOR VEYSELAER (1673–1695) ARE CONFIRMED BY INSCRIPTION

The Rijksmuseum inscription confirms Veyselaer's birth date as 25 March 1673. His death date of 1695 is consistent with his dying on the homeward voyage from the Cape. These dates in the Teylers record are correct.

### 9. CATALOG REFERENCES ENTIRELY ABSENT

No standard catalog references are provided. The most relevant references would include:

- **Van Loon** (Beschryving der Nederlandsche Historipenningen, 1723–1731) — the standard Dutch medal reference
- **Rijksmuseum NG-VG-1-4373** — the comparable exemplar
- Silleman's published account: *Korte en bondige beschryving van de rampzalige reyse van 't schip De Gouden Buys* (Enkhuizen, 1695)
