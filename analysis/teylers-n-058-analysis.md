## Object Description

A pen-and-ink drawing (*penwerk*) by **Hendrick Goltzius** (Mühlbracht, 1558 – Haarlem, 1617), dated 1588, depicting his own permanently crippled right hand. As an infant of approximately one year, Goltzius fell into a pan of boiling oil; a meddling neighbour removed the splints and bound only the right hand in cloth, causing the fingers to permanently contract into a claw-like configuration. The drawing belongs to Goltzius's celebrated category of "pen works" (*penwerken*) — virtuoso pen-and-ink drawings in which he deliberately reproduced, using a pen, the swelling and tapering burin lines of his engraving technique. The hand is simultaneously a study, a technical demonstration, a trademark, and an indirect **self-portrait**. During his incognito Italian journey (1590–91), the drawing reportedly served as proof of his identity when other artists recognised the famous deformed hand. One of the most celebrated drawings in the entire Teylers collection. Designated one of Teylers' **50 Topstukken**.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. NO DESCRIPTION WHATSOEVER (MAJOR)

The catalog record contains no descriptive text. For one of the most famous drawings in the entire Teylers collection, the absence of any description is a critical deficiency. A visitor searching the online catalog finds:
- No explanation that the hand is Goltzius's own crippled right hand
- No mention of the childhood burn injury (documented by Karel van Mander, *Schilder-boeck*, 1604)
- No identification as a "penwerk" — an established and significant genre in Goltzius's oeuvre
- No mention that it functions as an indirect self-portrait
- No reference to the Italian journey anecdote (1590–91)

### 2. "SELF-PORTRAIT" MISSING FROM SUBJECT TAGS (MAJOR)

The catalog assigns only the subject term **"hand"**. This drawing is universally recognised in scholarship as an indirect self-portrait — it depicts the artist's own permanently damaged hand, his professional trademark, famous across Europe in his lifetime. The absence of "zelfportret" or "self-portrait" as a subject term means the work will not surface in any search for self-portraits in the collection. This is a major cataloguing failure given the work's identity as an indirect self-representation.

### 3. OBJECT NAME FIELD MISUSED (MINOR)

The "Object Name" field reads "pen in inkt" — a technique description, not an object type. Standard museum practice requires this field to contain a typological descriptor such as "tekening" (drawing) or, more precisely, "penwerk" (pen work). The category "penwerk" is art historically significant: it is an established and celebrated genre in Goltzius's output, distinct from ordinary drawings, and was among the most expensive collectible drawings of the late 16th century. The current entry creates redundancy with the technique field and misses an opportunity to classify the work correctly.

### 4. DIMENSIONS DISCREPANCY (MINOR)

The catalog records the paper width as **328 mm**. External sources — including the Alamy photographic archive, Hektoen International, and Metropolitan Museum 2003 retrospective materials — consistently give the paper dimensions as **230 x 322 mm** (height x width). The 6 mm discrepancy in width (328 vs. 322 mm) is outside normal rounding and warrants physical re-measurement.

### 5. NO PROVENANCE (MAJOR)

The catalog lists no provenance. Teylers holds 125 Goltzius drawings, most entering the collection via the landmark 1790 purchase from the heirs of Duke Livio Odescalchi (Rome) — drawings previously in the collection of **Queen Christina of Sweden** (1626–1689). Whether N 058 was part of this Odescalchi/Christina tranche is not stated. For a Topstuk of this importance, the provenance gap is unacceptable.

### 6. NO EXHIBITION HISTORY (MAJOR)

No exhibitions are recorded. This drawing was exhibited in the most important Goltzius retrospective of the modern era:

- **"Hendrick Goltzius, Dutch Master (1558–1617): Drawings, Prints, and Paintings"**, Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York (June–September 2003), then Toledo Museum of Art (October 2003–January 2004). Catalog edited by Huigen Leeflang and Ger Luijten (Waanders Publishers, 2003).

The absence of this from the record means researchers cannot find the most authoritative published analysis of the work.

### 7. CREATOR DESCRIPTION REDUCTIVELY INCOMPLETE (MINOR)

The catalog describes Goltzius only as "tekenaar" (draughtsman). He was one of the most important **printmakers, engravers, painters, and print publishers** of the Northern Renaissance. The "pen works" genre is inseparable from his identity as an engraver — the entire point of the technique is to mimic burin engraving lines using a pen. Standard references describe him as "Dutch printmaker, painter, draughtsman and publisher."

### 8. CATALOG RAISONNÉ ENTRY NUMBERS NOT CITED (MINOR)

The bibliography references Řezníček's *Die Zeichnungen von Hendrick Goltzius* (1961) — the fundamental catalog raisonné — but does not give the specific entry number for N 058. This is the standard scholarly identifier used in all specialist literature and should be cited. Similarly, Bleyerveld/Veldman's *The Netherlandish Drawings of the 16th Century in Teylers Museum* (Primavera Pers, Leiden, 2016) is listed without an entry number.

### 9. STÄDEL COMPANION DRAWING NOT CROSS-REFERENCED (MINOR)

The Städel Museum, Frankfurt (inv. 805 Z), holds *Four Studies of a Right Hand* (c. 1588–89, black and red chalk), in which the lower study depicts Goltzius's damaged right hand — the most directly comparable work to N 058. This cross-reference is absent from the catalog.

### 10. ITALIAN JOURNEY ANECDOTE NOT MENTIONED (MINOR)

Karel van Mander's *Schilder-boeck* (1604) records that when Goltzius traveled incognito to Italy in 1590–91, he was recognised by other artists who had seen this drawing and knew his famous hand. This anecdote is one of the most cited passages in Goltzius scholarship and is directly relevant to N 058.
