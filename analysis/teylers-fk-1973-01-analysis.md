## Object Description

This is a plastic scale model of an **ultracentrifuge for uranium enrichment**, made at the **FOM Laboratory for Mass Spectrography** (later AMOLF) in Amsterdam in 1957. It represents the foundational technology of the **Dutch ultracentrifuge program**, which led to the establishment of **URENCO** — one of the world's largest commercial uranium enrichment companies — via the Treaty of Almelo (1970) with the UK and Germany. The model was designed by **Jacob Kistemaker** (1917–2010), the physicist who founded the Dutch enrichment program and who was simultaneously **conservator (curator) of Teylers' Natuurkundig Kabinet** (Physics Cabinet). The technology descended from this program was infamously stolen by **A.Q. Khan** in 1972–1975 and proliferated to Pakistan, Iran, Libya, and North Korea.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. JACOB KISTEMAKER: PIONEER OF DUTCH NUCLEAR TECHNOLOGY

The catalog says only "naar Kistemaker." **Jacob (Jaap) Kistemaker** (1917–2010) was one of the most important Dutch physicists of the 20th century:

- Born in De Bilt; doctorate at Leiden's **Kamerlingh Onnes Laboratory** (1945) under Hendrik Kramers
- Sent to **Niels Bohr's institute** in Copenhagen (1946) to learn nuclear physics
- Founded the **FOM Laboratory for Mass Spectrography** in Amsterdam (1949)
- In **1953**, produced the first enriched uranium outside the American embargo — Niels Bohr himself visited to see the achievement
- Organized the pivotal **1957 Amsterdam conference** on isotope separation, where Soviet specialist **Gernot Zippe** shared centrifuge knowledge that transformed the program
- Professor at **Leiden University** (1956–1984); member of the **KNAW** (Royal Netherlands Academy of Sciences)
- Director of FOM Laboratory until 1982; a planetoid was named in his honor
- Played chamber music — continuing the tradition of physicist-musicians at Leiden (cf. Crommelin/Einstein/Ehrenfest trio)

### 2. KISTEMAKER WAS TEYLERS' OWN PHYSICS CURATOR

The most remarkable finding: **Kistemaker himself was conservator of Teylers' Natuurkundig Kabinet** during the 1970s, and a member of the **Teylers Tweede Genootschap** (Second Society). This directly explains why a Cold War nuclear technology model entered an 18th-century museum — the inventor donated his own work to the collection he curated. The catalog makes no mention of this connection.

This places Kistemaker in the lineage of illustrious Teylers physics curators: **Martinus van Marum** (1784–1837) → **Hendrik Lorentz** (1910–1928) → **Adriaan Fokker** (1928–1955) → ... → **Jacob Kistemaker** (1970s).

### 3. THE SIGNIFICANCE OF 1957

The catalog gives "1957" without explanation. This was the pivotal year:

- **April 1957**: Kistemaker's Amsterdam conference on isotope separation
- **28 April 1957**: Gernot Zippe, recently released from Soviet captivity, explained Soviet centrifuge principles to Kistemaker in a private two-hour conversation
- **30 April 1957**: The program fundamentally changed direction — from heavy horizontal machines to light, vertical, self-aligning **Zippe-type centrifuges**
- FOM's annual report records the beginning of ultracentrifuge work in collaboration with Werkspoor

The model represents the **very moment** the Dutch centrifuge program became the program we know — the moment theoretical possibility became concrete engineering.

### 4. FOM LABORATORIUM: NOT JUST A MAKER

"F.O.M. Laboratorium" tells the reader nothing. The **Stichting voor Fundamenteel Onderzoek der Materie** (Foundation for Fundamental Research on Matter) laboratory in Amsterdam went through several names:
- 1949: FOM Laboratory for Mass Spectrography
- 1960: FOM Laboratory for Mass Separation
- 1966: FOM Institute for Atomic and Molecular Physics (**AMOLF**)
- 2017: AMOLF (merged into NWO)

This was one of the Netherlands' most important physics research institutions, directly responsible for technology that became a cornerstone of the global nuclear fuel cycle.

### 5. THE A.Q. KHAN PROLIFERATION CONNECTION

The technology represented by this model has direct links to one of the worst nuclear proliferation episodes in history:

- **1972**: Pakistani metallurgist **Abdul Qadeer Khan** began work at FDO, a subcontractor of UCN (Ultra Centrifuge Nederland)
- **1974**: Khan obtained unsupervised access to classified centrifuge blueprints for 16 days
- **15 December 1975**: Khan left for Pakistan with copied designs and ~100 supplier contacts
- Khan's stolen designs became the **P-1 and P-2 centrifuges**, subsequently proliferated to Iran, Libya, and North Korea

The FK 1973.01 model represents the **intellectual ancestor** of the proliferated technology — Kistemaker's original concept that evolved into the commercial designs Khan later stole. This Cold War and proliferation context is absent from the catalog.

### 6. CLASSIFICATION AS "MECHANICS" IS REDUCTIVE

The catalog places this in the "Mechanics" collection. While an ultracentrifuge is technically a mechanical device (a spinning rotor), its significance lies in **nuclear physics, isotope separation, and nuclear fuel cycle technology**. The classification obscures the object's true historical importance. A modern classification would place it under nuclear physics or industrial technology.

### 7. HOW GAS CENTRIFUGE ENRICHMENT WORKS

The catalog provides no explanation of the technology. Gas centrifuge enrichment:
- Uranium hexafluoride (UF₆) gas is fed into a cylindrical rotor spinning at extreme speed in a vacuum
- Centrifugal force pushes heavier U-238 molecules toward the wall; lighter U-235 concentrates near the center
- An axial countercurrent enhances separation
- **50× more energy-efficient** than the competing gaseous diffusion process
- Requires ~30× fewer stages than diffusion
- Has completely replaced gaseous diffusion worldwide
