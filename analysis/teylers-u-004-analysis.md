## Object Description

A large-format drawing on blue-grey paper (497 × 349 mm) attributed to **Peter Paul Rubens** (1577–1640), made as a copy after **Tiziano Vecellio (Titian)** (c.1488–1576). Subject: **Saint Jerome in the Wilderness** (*Heilige Hieronymus in de woestijn*). The drawing is in mixed media: black chalk, white and light yellow heightening, red chalk accents, brown pen (worked up), with a brown pen border line. The inscription on the sheet reads *"PP Rubbens St Hironimus"* — a later collector's attribution annotation, not an autograph signature. Additional inscribed numbers (113, C T 98, 899) are old inventory/sale marks from previous collections. Titian painted Saint Jerome in at least four distinct versions (c.1531, Louvre; c.1552, Brera; c.1575, Escorial; Thyssen-Bornemisza). Rubens studied Titian's works intensively during his Italian sojourn (1600–1608) and at the Spanish court in Madrid (1628–29), where he had direct access to Titian's Saint Jerome at the Escorial.

The catalog bibliography includes two Van Dyck titles specifically concerning Van Dyck's Saint Jerome paintings — indicating a live attribution debate that the record itself entirely conceals.

## Key Findings

### 1. ATTRIBUTION DEBATE WITH VAN DYCK CONCEALED — HIDDEN IN BIBLIOGRAPHY

The most significant issue: the bibliography quietly includes two Van Dyck titles — *"Young Van Dyck = Le jeune Van Dyck"* and *"Meesterwerk of kopie?: Twee versies van Anthonie van Dijcks Heilige Hieronymus met een engel"* — the latter explicitly about a disputed attribution of Van Dyck's Saint Jerome between Museum Boijmans Van Beuningen and the Nationalmuseum Stockholm. These titles indicate that this drawing is implicated in a scholarly attribution debate between the Rubens and Van Dyck traditions of this subject. Yet the catalog record presents the drawing as straightforwardly by Rubens with no caveat, no note on contested authorship, and no explanation of the Van Dyck connection. A researcher finds a "Rubens after Titian" record with a Van Dyck bibliography and no explanation whatsoever.

### 2. NO IDENTIFICATION OF WHICH TITIAN ORIGINAL WAS COPIED

The catalog states only "kopie naar Tiziano Vecellio" without identifying which of Titian's several Saint Jerome paintings was the source. Titian painted Saint Jerome in at least four compositionally distinct versions (c.1531 Louvre, c.1552 Brera, c.1575 Escorial, Thyssen-Bornemisza). Identifying the source is essential for art-historical interpretation and is a major omission.

### 3. NO DATE ASSIGNED TO THE DRAWING

No production date in the catalog. Rubens made copies after Titian in at least three distinct periods: Italian sojourn (1600–1608), Madrid visit (1628–29, with direct access to the Escorial Saint Jerome), and Antwerp. Assigning even an approximate date is standard for Rubens drawings and is addressed systematically in the Corpus Rubenianum XXVI (Jeremy Wood, Brepols). Its absence leaves the drawing unanchored in Rubens's career.

### 4. NO PROVENANCE INFORMATION

No provenance recorded. The largest single acquisition in Teylers Museum's history was the 1790 purchase of ~1,700 drawings from the heirs of Livio Odescalchi (Rome), many formerly belonging to Queen Christina of Sweden (1626–1689). Whether this drawing arrived via that purchase is unrecorded but would be extremely significant context.

### 5. NO ICONOGRAPHIC DESCRIPTION

No description of what is depicted: Saint Jerome's pose, whether a lion is present, the landscape elements, how Rubens interpreted or adapted the Titian source, the quality of the mixed-media technique. For a drawing of this scale and scholarly importance, the absence of any description is a serious gap.

### 6. BIBLIOGRAPHY LACKS AUTHORS, YEARS, PUBLISHERS, AND PAGE REFERENCES

All seven bibliography entries give only bare titles — no author, year, publisher, or page number. This makes them nearly useless for scholarly navigation. Examples of what is missing:
- *Die Handzeichnungen von Peter Paul Rubens*: Gustav Glück & Franz Martin Haberditzl, Berlin: Julius Bard, 1928
- *Young Van Dyck = Le jeune Van Dyck*: Alan McNairn, National Gallery of Canada, Ottawa, 1980

### 7. "PP Rubbens St Hironimus" inscription not identified as a later annotation

The inscription is recorded but not identified as a **later collector's attribution annotation** — not an autograph signature. Rubens stopped signing works after c.1614; such inscriptions on old master drawings were commonly added by later collectors or auction houses. This distinction is not made in the catalog.

### 8. Titian birth year lacks "circa" qualifier

"1488/90" is recorded without a "circa" qualifier. The National Gallery, London deliberately declines to give a birth year for Titian, noting the lack of documentary evidence. The standard scholarly notation is "c.1488–1576." The slash notation implies false precision.
