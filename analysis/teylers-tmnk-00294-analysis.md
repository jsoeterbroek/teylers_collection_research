## Object Description

This is a silver triumph medal commemorating one of the most celebrated military exploits in Dutch history — the **Capture of Breda on 4 March 1590** using the "Turfschip van Breda" (Peat Ship of Breda), often compared to the **Trojan Horse**. Approximately 68–75 Dutch and English soldiers under **Charles de Héraugière** hid inside a peat barge, sailed unchecked past Spanish guards into the castle, and captured it at dawn. The medal was struck by order of the States-General just **16 days** after the event (resolution of 20 March 1590), with dies engraved by **Gerard van Bylaer** at the Dordrecht mint. It comes from the personal collection of **Pieter Teyler van der Hulst** — the museum's founder — demonstrating his active interest in Dutch historical medals.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. THE TURFSCHIP STORY: THE DUTCH TROJAN HORSE

The catalog title names the event but provides no description. This is one of the most famous stories in Dutch history:

- **February 1590**: Peat skipper **Adriaen van Bergen** from Leur proposed the plan to Prince Maurice — his barge was never inspected by the Spanish garrison
- **Charles de Héraugière** reconnoitered the defences disguised as a fisherman
- **25 February**: 68–75 soldiers concealed beneath peat blocks in the barge's hold
- Voyage delayed by extreme cold and ice on the River Mark
- **Night of 3–4 March**: Barge reaches the castle; at dawn soldiers emerge
- Spanish garrison taken completely by surprise; castle captured with minimal casualties
- **Breda** — ancestral seat of the House of Nassau-Orange — restored to Dutch control

This was Maurice's **first spectacular military victory** and launched his legendary career as the military genius who revolutionized European warfare.

### 2. INSCRIPTIONS NOT TRANSLATED

The catalog quotes partial Latin text without translation:

**Obverse:** *INVICTI ANIMI PRAEMIVM PARATI VINCERE AVT MORI 4 MARTII*
= **"The reward of an unconquered spirit: prepared to conquer or to die. 4 March."**

**Reverse:** *BREDA A SERVITVTE HISPANA VINDICATA DVCTV PRINCIPIS MAVRITII A NASSAVIA AO MDXC*
= **"Breda liberated from Spanish servitude under the leadership of Prince Maurice of Nassau. Year 1590."**

These are powerful propagandistic texts that should be translated for the visitor.

### 3. CHARLES DE HÉRAUGIÈRE (1556–1601): BIOGRAPHY ABSENT

The catalog lists "Charles de Héraugnières" without context. He was:
- Born 1556 in **Cambrai** (Walloon/southern Netherlands)
- Nobleman and experienced military officer
- Led the covert assault force concealed in the peat barge
- Appointed **governor of Breda** as reward
- Received a gold medal, pewterware, and a gold-plated model of the turfschip
- The Rijksmuseum holds his portrait (SK-A-575, anonymous, 1590)
- Died 1601

### 4. ADRIAEN VAN BERGEN: THE PEAT SKIPPER

The catalog lists "Adriaen van Bergen" without context. He was:
- A peat barge skipper from **Leur** (now Etten-Leur) who regularly delivered peat to the Spanish garrison
- **Devised the original plot** and approached Prince Maurice
- On the day of the operation, he overslept or fell ill — his **two nephews** piloted the barge
- Honored with a statue "De Turfschipper" in Breda and the Van Bergenplein in Leur
- His grandson **Adriaen van der Donck** later played a key role in the colonization of **New Netherland** (New York)

### 5. VAN LOON REFERENCE NOT CITED

This medal is described in **Van Loon I, pp. 408–409** (*Beschryving der Nederlandsche Historipenningen*, 1723–1731), the standard reference for Dutch historical medals. Also cataloged as **Dugniolle 3255**. The Rijksmuseum holds comparable examples (NG-VG-1-482 at 18.23 g and NG-VG-3-726 at 7.93 g). These standard references are absent from the catalog.

### 6. COMMISSIONED BY THE STATES-GENERAL

The catalog does not mention that this medal was struck **by resolution of the States-General on 20 March 1590** — just 16 days after the capture. This makes it an **official state commemoration**, not a private issue. Van Bylaer cut dies for all States-General triumph medals.

### 7. PIETER TEYLER'S MENNONITE CONNECTION TO VAN BYLAER

The catalog notes the provenance from Pieter Teyler's collection but does not observe an interesting connection: both **Pieter Teyler** (1702–1778) and **Gerard van Bylaer** (c. 1553–1617) were **Mennonites**. Teyler was a wealthy Mennonite silk merchant; Van Bylaer is documented as Mennonite. The medal connects the museum's founder to a craftsman of the same religious community across two centuries.

### 8. MAURICE OF ORANGE NOT CONTEXTUALIZED

The catalog lists "Maurice of Orange (1567–1625)" without context. Maurice:
- Was the son of William the Silent (assassinated 1584)
- **Revolutionized European warfare** by reorganizing the Dutch army based on Roman military principles
- The capture of Breda was his first major victory, launching a string of conquests
- **Tragic irony**: Breda was lost again to Spain under Spinola just before Maurice's death in 1625, immortalized by Velázquez's *The Surrender of Breda* (1634–35)

### 9. VAN BYLAER BIRTH DATE INCONSISTENCY

The catalog gives Van Bylaer's dates as "±1540–1617." Multiple sources give his birth as **c. 1553** in Barneveld. This is the same inconsistency noted in TMNK 00272 (the prinsendaalder), which lists the same dates. If the birth year is wrong, it is systematically wrong across multiple objects.
