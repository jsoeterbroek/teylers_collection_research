## Object Description

Despite its name, this is **not a weather instrument**. An "electric barometer" is a demonstration device for the phenomenon of **barometric light** — the mysterious glow produced when mercury moves inside an evacuated glass tube. First observed by **Jean Picard** in 1675, this phenomenon is now understood as **triboelectric gas discharge / electroluminescence**: mercury sliding against glass generates static charge, which excites residual gas molecules in the vacuum, causing them to emit light. It is the same fundamental mechanism as a **neon lamp** — making this instrument a direct ancestor of electric lighting, created 90 years before Edison. Made in 1790, it belongs to the research programme of **Martinus van Marum**, who operated the world's largest electrostatic generator at Teylers and had just established the first physics laboratory in the Netherlands that same year.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. THIS IS NOT A BAROMETER — IT DEMONSTRATES ELECTROLUMINESCENCE

The title "Elektrische barometer" without explanation will mislead any reader into thinking this is a pressure-measuring instrument. It is a **demonstration device for barometric light** — evacuated glass tube(s) containing mercury, which glow when tilted or shaken.

The closest known comparable is at the **Museo Galileo in Florence**: a set of six mercury friction tubes (inv. 3765), described as evacuated glass tubes with mercury used "to demonstrate that the friction of the mercury against the glass of the tubes in a vacuum produced the electric glow."

### 2. THE BAROMETRIC LIGHT PHENOMENON: A CENTURY OF DISCOVERY

The catalog provides no context for this instrument's scientific significance. Barometric light was one of the most investigated electrical phenomena of the 17th–18th centuries:

| Year | Scientist | Discovery |
|------|----------|-----------|
| **1675** | **Jean Picard** | First observed the glow while carrying a barometer from the Paris Observatory at night |
| 1700 | **Johann Bernoulli** | Studied the phenomenon at the University of Groningen; demonstrated to the French Academy |
| **1703–09** | **Francis Hauksbee** | Crucial breakthrough: showed that mercury was irrelevant — any friction in a vacuum produces the glow; built a glowing evacuated glass globe bright enough to read by |
| 1749 | **Abbé Nollet** | Created the "oeuf électrique" (electric egg) — evacuated glass with electrodes, producing visible discharge; precursor to neon tubes |
| **1790** | **Teylers FK 1159** | **This instrument** |

The direct line runs: barometric light (1675) → Hauksbee's glowing globe (1706) → Nollet's electric egg (1749) → Geissler tubes (1857) → Crookes tubes (1870s) → **neon lamps** (1910) and **X-ray tubes** (1895). FK 1159 sits squarely in this lineage.

### 3. VAN MARUM'S ELECTRICAL RESEARCH PROGRAMME

The 1790 date places this instrument at the peak of **Martinus van Marum's** electrical research at Teylers:

- **1784**: World's largest electrostatic generator installed (1.65 m glass disks, 330,000 volts, sparks over 2 meters)
- **1785**: First observation of ozone during electrical discharge
- **1787**: First to liquefy ammonia
- **1790**: **Physics laboratory established** — the first in the Netherlands. **FK 1159 made this year.**
- **1791**: Final modifications to the electrostatic generator

Van Marum studied electrical discharge in various media. The electric barometer complements other discharge instruments in the collection, notably the **aurora globe** (FK 1153) by John Cuthbertson — a partially evacuated glass globe connected to the electrostatic generator that produces luminous bands simulating the aurora borealis.

### 7. MODERN SCIENTIFIC UNDERSTANDING

The catalog provides no explanation of the physics. Modern understanding:
- Mercury sliding against glass generates **triboelectric charge** (static electricity from friction)
- When mercury separates from the glass surface, accumulated electrons are released into the low-pressure gas remaining in the tube
- These electrons collide with gas molecules, exciting them
- The excited molecules emit **photons** — visible light
- This is fundamentally the same process as in a **neon lamp, fluorescent tube, or plasma globe**
- The phenomenon requires only partial vacuum — complete vacuum actually prevents it (no gas molecules to excite)
