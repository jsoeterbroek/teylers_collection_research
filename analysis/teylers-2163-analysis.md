## Object Description

This is a multi-volume octavo work by Georges Cuvier (1769–1832) surveying the progress of the natural sciences from the year of the French Revolution (1789) to the mid-1820s. It represents the expanded book form of Cuvier's official reports on scientific progress, originally commissioned by Napoleon and later continued under the Bourbon Restoration monarchs Louis XVIII and Charles X. Published by Baudouin Frères in Paris between 1826 and 1828, the work is a landmark in the historiography of science, written by the man who was simultaneously the most powerful scientific administrator in France and one of its greatest naturalists.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. GEORGES CUVIER: FAR MORE THAN "G. CUVIER"

The catalog lists the author as "G. Cuvier" without further identification. Jean Léopold Nicolas Frédéric Cuvier, known as Georges Cuvier (23 August 1769, Montbéliard – 13 May 1832, Paris) was one of the most important scientists of the 19th century and arguably the most powerful figure in French science during his lifetime. His significance includes:

- **Founder of comparative anatomy** as a scientific discipline, developing the principle of "correlation of parts" — that every organ in an animal's body is functionally related to all others
- **Founder of vertebrate paleontology** — he was the first to systematically reconstruct extinct animals from fossil bones and firmly established the fact of species extinction
- **Architect of modern zoological classification** — his *Le Règne animal* (1817) organized animals into four embranchements (vertebrates, mollusks, articulates, radiates), breaking from the Linnaean linear scale
- **Proponent of catastrophism** — the theory that Earth's history was shaped by sudden, violent upheavals that destroyed species
- **Opponent of evolution** — he publicly rejected the transformist theories of Lamarck (1809) and engaged in the famous 1830 public debate with Étienne Geoffroy Saint-Hilaire at the Académie des Sciences
- **Permanent Secretary (secrétaire perpétuel) of the Académie des Sciences** from 1803 until his death — a position of enormous power over French scientific life
- **Senior statesman** — served as Imperial Inspector of Public Instruction under Napoleon, State Councillor under Louis XVIII, was created Baron Cuvier in 1819, and served as Chancellor of the University of Paris. He was a member of the Conseil d'État and held numerous political appointments across four successive French regimes (Revolution, Napoleon, Restoration, July Monarchy)

His full title at the time of this publication would have been "Baron Cuvier." The catalog should reflect this and note his extraordinary significance.

### 2. THE ORIGIN AND NATURE OF THIS WORK

The catalog provides no context for what this work actually is. It is not simply a book Cuvier decided to write — it has a specific institutional and political origin:

**The 1808 Rapport historique (precursor):** In 1802 (13 ventôse an X), the French government commissioned the Institut de France to produce reports on the state of all sciences since 1789. Cuvier, as permanent secretary of the Class of Physical and Mathematical Sciences, was charged with producing the report on the natural sciences. He presented this *Rapport historique sur les progrès des sciences naturelles depuis 1789, et sur leur état actuel* to Emperor Napoleon in his Council of State on 6 February 1808. It was printed by the Imprimerie impériale in 1810. Cuvier later complained this single report "cost him a full year of labor."

**The 1826–1828 expanded work:** The *Histoire des progrès des sciences naturelles depuis 1789 jusqu'à ce jour* is the expanded, updated, multi-volume version of that original report. As permanent secretary, Cuvier continued to compile reports on scientific progress. The 1826–1828 publication gathers these into a comprehensive survey covering nearly four decades of scientific advances, organized in two periods:
- **Première période** (First period): 1789 to approximately 1808
- **Seconde période**: 1809 to 1827

The complete work eventually comprised **5 volumes** published between 1826 and 1836 (volumes published after 1828 were issued posthumously or by different publishers, as Cuvier died in 1832). The Teylers copy, dated 1826–1828, likely represents the first 3 or 4 volumes of this five-volume set.

### 3. THE SIGNIFICANCE OF 1789 AS STARTING DATE

The catalog does not explain why the work begins at 1789. This date is not arbitrary — it marks the **French Revolution**, which fundamentally transformed French scientific institutions. The choice of 1789 was originally Napoleon's: he wanted to demonstrate that scientific progress had accelerated under his regime compared to the Ancien Régime. The commission explicitly required documenting progress "since 1789" — that is, since the revolutionary era. The date thus has both political and institutional significance:

- 1789 saw the abolition of the old royal academies
- The revolutionary and Napoleonic periods created new institutions: the Muséum national d'Histoire naturelle (1793), the École Polytechnique (1794), the Institut de France (1795)
- Cuvier himself arrived in Paris in 1795, so the period covered essentially coincides with his entire scientific career

### 4. PUBLISHER "BAUDOUIN" IS BAUDOUIN FRÈRES — A SIGNIFICANT FIRM

The catalog lists only "Baudouin" as publisher. The full imprint is **Baudouin Frères**, an important Parisian publishing house active during the Restoration (1816–1834). The firm was run by twin brothers **Charles Baudouin** (1791–186?) and **Alexandre Baudouin** (1791–1859?), sons of the revolutionary-era printer François-Jean Baudouin (1759–1835), who had been the official printer of the National Assembly during the Revolution. Their younger brother Hippolyte joined before 1828.

The Baudouin Frères firm was politically significant:
- They were shareholders in the liberal newspaper *Le Constitutionnel*
- They were prosecuted and convicted in 1824 and 1828 for publishing Béranger's politically subversive *Chansons*
- Honoré de Balzac collaborated with them as a printer (1826–1828)
- They published the *Collection des mémoires relatifs à la Révolution française*

The publisher's full name "Baudouin Frères" should be recorded rather than simply "Baudouin."

### 5. CUVIER'S DIRECT CONNECTION TO TEYLERS MUSEUM

The catalog makes no mention of the remarkable fact that Cuvier had a **direct, personal connection to Teylers Museum**. In **1811**, Cuvier visited Teylers Museum in Haarlem (then part of the French Empire under Napoleon) to examine the famous fossil known as *Homo diluvii testis* ("Man, witness of the Deluge"). This specimen, originally described by Johann Jakob Scheuchzer in 1726 as the skeleton of a human who drowned in the biblical Flood, had been purchased by Teylers' director **Martinus van Marum** in 1802 from Scheuchzer's grandson in Zürich.

Cuvier carefully prepared the fossil by clearing away surrounding rock, uncovered the forelimbs, and definitively identified it as **a giant salamander** — not a human at all. He declared it "nothing but a salamander, or rather a proteus of gigantic dimensions and of an unknown species." This was a triumph of Cuvier's comparative anatomical method and one of the most famous corrections in the history of paleontology. The specimen (later named *Andrias scheuchzeri*) remains on display at Teylers Museum to this day, in its original showcase.

Furthermore, Cuvier was elected a **correspondent of the Royal Institute of the Netherlands** (Koninklijk Instituut) in 1812, and became a full foreign member in 1827 — during the very period this book was being published. Teylers' director Martinus van Marum also reorganized the museum's mineral collection according to Cuvier's methods.

This connection between the author and the museum that holds this book is historically significant and should be noted in the catalog.

### 7. RELATED WORKS BY CUVIER — RISK OF CONFUSION

There are **three distinct works** by Cuvier with similar titles that should not be confused:

1. ***Rapport historique sur les progrès des sciences naturelles depuis 1789*** (1810) — the original single-volume report presented to Napoleon in 1808, printed by the Imprimerie impériale. This is the precursor.

2. ***Histoire des progrès des sciences naturelles, depuis 1789 jusqu'à ce jour*** (1826–1836, 5 vols.) — the expanded multi-volume version, published by Baudouin Frères. **This is the work in the Teylers catalog.**

3. ***Histoire des sciences naturelles depuis leur origine jusqu'à nos jours, chez tous les peuples connus*** (1841–1845, 5 vols.) — Cuvier's posthumously published lectures from the Collège de France (1829–1832), compiled by Magdeleine de Saint-Agy. This is a much broader work covering the entire history of natural sciences from antiquity.

The catalog should clarify which of these works this copy represents.

### 8. VOLUME COUNT AND EDITIONS

The catalog date range "1826–1828" suggests this copy contains the volumes published during Cuvier's lifetime by Baudouin Frères. The complete work eventually ran to 5 volumes (1826–1836), with later volumes published after Cuvier's death in 1832 by the publisher Roret (who succeeded Baudouin). The Teylers copy likely contains 3 to 4 volumes. The catalog should specify how many physical volumes are present.

### 9. WORLDCAT HOLDINGS

The work is recorded in WorldCat under OCLC number **24037522**. It is held by research libraries worldwide, though it is not an extremely common work. Its presence in the Teylers collection is consistent with the museum's historical commitment to acquiring current scientific literature, and its special connection to Cuvier makes this copy particularly fitting.
