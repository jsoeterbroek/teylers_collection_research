## Object Description

This is the official expedition report by Otto Herz on the recovery of the **Beresovka mammoth** — one of the most famous paleontological discoveries in history. Published by the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg in 1902, it documents the 1901 expedition to excavate a nearly complete woolly mammoth carcass preserved in Siberian permafrost for approximately 44,000 years. The mammoth was found with undigested food still in its stomach and grass between its teeth, indicating sudden death during summer. The mounted specimen remains on display at the Zoological Museum in St. Petersburg.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. THIS IS A LANDMARK PRIMARY SOURCE IN PALEONTOLOGY

The catalog gives no indication of this publication's significance. It is the **first-hand account by the expedition leader** of the most thoroughly documented mammoth recovery of the 19th/early 20th century. The Beresovka mammoth is one of the most famous fossils ever found:

- Discovered in **1900** by Lamut hunter Semen Tarabykin on the Berezovka River (tributary of the Kolyma), northeastern Siberia
- A male mammoth, aged 45–50 years, preserved in permafrost
- Found in an upright sitting position with broken hips, legs, and ribs — suggesting it fell off a cliff and suffocated
- **Sedges, grasses, and buttercups** were lodged between its teeth
- Approximately **14 kg of undigested plant material** in its stomach
- Retained skin, flesh, and internal organs
- Radiocarbon dated to approximately **44,000 years old**

### 2. OTTO HERZ: BIOGRAPHY NOT PROVIDED

**Alfred Otto Herz** (1856–1905) was a German entomologist (specialist in butterflies and beetles) based at the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg. Despite his entomological specialty, he was selected to lead the mammoth expedition due to his extensive field experience in remote regions (Transcaucasia, Persia, Kamchatka, China, Korea, Japan, Siam). He was accompanied by zoologist **Eugen Pfizenmayer**.

### 3. THE EXPEDITION (1901)

The catalog provides no details of the expedition:
- Departed St. Petersburg **3 May 1901**; reached the site **9 September 1901** (4 months of travel)
- Excavated in freezing conditions, recovering ~130 kg of flesh, ~230 kg of skin, and the complete head
- Dissection completed **11 October 1901**
- Remains packed in **27 cases on 10 sleighs** drawn by reindeer
- Return journey in temperatures reaching **−48°C**
- Report presented to the Academy **27 February 1902** — just 9 days after return

### 4. THE 10 PLATES

These contain photographs of the excavation and the mammoth carcass in situ — historically significant images now widely reproduced in paleontology literature. The catalog does not describe their content.
