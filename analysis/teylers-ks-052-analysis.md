## Object Description

Oil painting on canvas (98.1 × 120.1 cm, signed and dated 1838) by Dutch painter **Nicolaas Pieneman** (1809–1860). The painting depicts the immediate aftermath of the assassination attempt on William I, Prince of Orange ("William the Silent") by **Juan de Jáuregui** in Antwerp on 18 March 1582. The scene shows the wounded Willem I in a chair, his wife Charlotte of Bourbon attending him, the dead body of Jáuregui on the floor, and the young **Prince Maurits** (age 14–15) directing soldiers to examine the assassin's corpse. A 19th-century Dutch nationalist history painting acquired by Teylers in or around 1838 — the year the museum's First Painting Gallery opened. A steel engraving after this painting was made by Johann Wilhelm Kaiser (I) in 1853–54 for the *Vereeniging ter Bevordering van Beeldende Kunsten* (Teylers KG 08154).

## Key Findings

### 1. TITLE OMITS THE CENTRAL PROTAGONIST — MISLEADING SUBJECT DESCRIPTION

The catalog title *"De aanslag door Jean Jauregui op Prins Willem I"* (The attack by Jean Jauregui on Prince Willem I) does not describe what is actually depicted. The painting shows the **aftermath** of the attack, with **Prince Maurits** at center commanding soldiers to examine Jáuregui's corpse. The full scholarly title, documented in the 1978 Rijksmuseum exhibition catalogue *Het vaderlandsch gevoel* (DBNL), is:

> *Prins Maurits laat het lijk van Jauregui onderzoeken, oogenblikkelijk nadat deze een aanslag op het leven van Willem I had volvoerd, te Antwerpen, 18 Maart 1582*

The Teylers title omits the **narrative protagonist** (Maurits) and the key depicted action (his command). It reduces a complex multi-figure history painting centred on Maurits's decisive response to a mere reference to the attack.

### 2. DATE TYPO "1852" INSTEAD OF "1582" IN RELATED OBJECT RECORD

The related object KG 08154 (steel engraving after this painting) is titled in the Teylers record as "Aanslag door Jean de Jauregui op prins Willem I, Antwerpen 18 maart **1852**." The historical event occurred in **1582**. This is a digit-transposition typographical error embedded in the catalog record — factually wrong.

### 3. NO PROSE DESCRIPTION OF THE DEPICTED SCENE

The description field is empty. This is a significant omission for a complex multi-figure history painting with at least six identifiable subjects (Willem I wounded, Charlotte of Bourbon, Maurits commanding, soldiers with halberds, dead Jáuregui, a dog). The narrative meaning — Maurits's strategic initiative at age 14–15 to expose the Spanish conspiracy — is entirely absent.

### 4. NO PROVENANCE OR ACQUISITION HISTORY

No record of how or when Teylers acquired the painting. Given it was made in 1838 and the First Painting Gallery opened that same year, it is likely a near-contemporary acquisition — possibly a direct commission — but nothing is documented.

### 5. Creator roles incomplete

The catalog records Pieneman as "kunstschilder en lithograaf." Multiple authoritative sources (Wikipedia, Wikidata, Bravefineart) consistently describe him as also an **art collector** and **sculptor** — roles omitted from the catalog.

### 6. Assassin's name rendered in French form without context

"Jean Jauregui" is a French-language transliteration used in 16th-century pamphlets. The correct modern scholarly name is **Juan de Jáuregui**, a Biscayan from Bilbao. Using the French form without noting his Basque/Spanish origin is misleading about his nationality.

### 7. Maurits's age and nationalist significance not noted

At the time of the 1582 event, Maurits was approximately **14–15 years old**. Pieneman centred the composition on young Maurits's initiative — a deliberate nationalist programme for the newly consolidated Dutch state. This art-historical context is unaddressed.

### 8. Charlotte de Bourbon's death not contextualised

Charlotte of Bourbon (c.1546 – 5 May 1582) **died seven weeks after the assassination attempt**, of exhaustion from nursing Willem. This is directly relevant to the scene depicted and was commonly noted in 19th-century accounts.

### 9. No exhibition history

The 1978 Rijksmuseum exhibition *Het vaderlandsch gevoel* catalogued this work. No exhibition history is recorded.
