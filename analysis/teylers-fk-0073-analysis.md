## Object Description

This is an 18th-century physics demonstration instrument that embodies two of the most elegant discoveries in the history of mathematics: the **brachistochrone** (curve of fastest descent) and the **tautochrone** (curve of equal-time descent) — both of which turn out to be the same curve: the **cycloid**. The apparatus has an adjustable straight incline (to compare against a cycloidal track) and a cycloidal pendulum (demonstrating that a weight swings in equal time regardless of amplitude). It was built by **J. Kampman** in the workshop of **Adam Steitz** in Amsterdam, following the instrument designs of **Willem Jacob 's Gravesande** (1688–1742), the foremost promoter of Newtonian physics on the European continent. The instrument connects three giants of 17th-century science: **Christiaan Huygens** (who discovered the tautochrone property in 1673), **Johann Bernoulli** (who posed the brachistochrone challenge in 1696), and **Isaac Newton** (who solved it in a single evening). It is part of a coordinated suite of 's Gravesande-style mechanics apparatus at Teylers, all from the Kampman/Steitz workshop.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. NO DESCRIPTION OF WHAT THE INSTRUMENT DOES

The catalog provides a title — "Cycloidal track with adjustable straight incline and cycloidal pendulum" — but no description of what it demonstrates or why it matters. This instrument demonstrates two profound mathematical discoveries:

**The Brachistochrone (curve of fastest descent):** A ball released simultaneously on the straight incline and the cycloidal track reaches the bottom **faster on the cycloid**, even though the straight path is shorter. The cycloid is the fastest possible curve between two points under gravity — counterintuitively faster than a straight line.

**The Tautochrone (curve of equal time):** On the cycloidal pendulum, a weight released from any height along the curve reaches the bottom in **exactly the same time**. This is the property Huygens exploited to design theoretically perfect pendulum clocks.

The catalog should explain these demonstrations — they are among the most beautiful results in classical mechanics.

### 2. THE BRACHISTOCHRONE PROBLEM: A FOUNDING MOMENT OF MATHEMATICS

In June 1696, **Johann Bernoulli** posed a challenge in the *Acta Eruditorum*: find the curve along which a body falls fastest between two points. Five of the greatest mathematicians in history solved it:

1. **Isaac Newton** — reportedly solved it in a single evening; Bernoulli recognized the anonymous solution saying *"tanquam ex ungue leonem"* ("I know the lion by his claw")
2. **Jakob Bernoulli** — Johann's brother, provided a rigorous independent proof
3. **Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz** — co-inventor of calculus
4. **Ehrenfried Walther von Tschirnhaus**
5. **Guillaume de l'Hôpital**

The answer: the cycloid. Jakob Bernoulli's solution method led directly to the **calculus of variations** — one of the most important branches of applied mathematics, later formalized by Euler and Lagrange. This instrument physically demonstrates the problem that gave birth to an entire field of mathematics.

### 3. CHRISTIAAN HUYGENS AND THE CYCLOIDAL PENDULUM (1673)

The cycloidal pendulum component of this instrument demonstrates a discovery by **Christiaan Huygens** (1629–1695), published in his *Horologium Oscillatorium* (1673) — considered one of the three most important works on mechanics in the 17th century, alongside Galileo's *Two New Sciences* (1638) and Newton's *Principia* (1687).

Huygens discovered that an ordinary pendulum is **not** perfectly isochronous — its period varies with amplitude. He proved that only a cycloid produces truly equal-time oscillations. His ingenious solution: mounting cycloidal "cheeks" at the pivot point so the string wraps against them, forcing the bob to trace a cycloidal arc. His surprising result was that the cheeks themselves must also be cycloids — because the cycloid is its own **evolute**.

Huygens attempted to use cycloidal pendulum clocks to solve the **longitude problem** — determining position at sea. Sea trials (1660, 1685, 1686, 1690) all failed because the pendulum was too sensitive to ship motion. The solution ultimately came from **balance springs** (also invented by Huygens) and John Harrison's marine chronometers. But the cycloidal pendulum remains one of the most elegant ideas in the history of science.

### 4. 'S GRAVESANDE NOT IDENTIFIED

The catalog does not mention 's Gravesande, though the instrument is built after his designs. **Willem Jacob 's Gravesande** (1688–1742):

- Professor at the University of Leiden
- Published *Physices Elementa Mathematica, Experimentis Confirmata* (1720) — the first continental textbook teaching physics through Newtonian experimental demonstration, with 127 engraved plates of apparatus
- Revolutionized physics pedagogy: systematic experimental demonstration rather than theoretical lecture
- His instrument designs were reproduced by workshops across Europe for physics cabinets
- His original instrument collection at Leiden is now at **Museum Boerhaave** (transferred from Leiden University, most recently in February 2025 with 3,000+ objects)
- His famous *vis viva* experiment (brass balls dropped on clay) supported Leibniz's concept of kinetic energy as *mv²*

The FK 0073 cycloidal track follows 's Gravesande's published design. The catalog should state this connection.

### 5. KAMPMAN AND STEITZ NOT IDENTIFIED

The catalog lists "J. Kampman, Adam Steitz Amsterdam" without context:

**Adam Steitz** was an Amsterdam instrument maker active c. 1750, the more established of the two. He independently built a **Gregorian reflector telescope** for Amsterdam merchant Jacobus van der Wall, later held at the Leiden Observatory. He also built a centrifugal machine after 's Gravesande (FK 0053 at Teylers). Both makers are listed in the DWC (Digitaal Wetenschapshistorisch Centrum) database of the Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences.

**J. Kampman** was likely a specialist craftsman within Steitz's workshop. The consistent attribution "J. Kampman, Adam Steitz Amsterdam" across multiple instruments suggests Kampman made the instruments in Steitz's workshop.

No birth/death dates or biographical details are publicly known for either maker.

### 8. COMPARABLE INSTRUMENTS IN OTHER COLLECTIONS

Similar cycloidal/brachistochrone demonstration instruments survive at:

- **Museo Galileo, Florence**: Inv. 966, by Francesco Spighi (2nd half 18th c.) — wood, brass, ivory, 2395×420×910 mm. Very similar concept with cycloidal and straight channels.
- **Harvard Collection of Historical Scientific Instruments**: Object 16776, "Inclined plane with cycloid curve" by Watkins & Hill, London.
- **Museum Boerhaave, Leiden**: Holds 's Gravesande's original instrument collection from Leiden University, likely including a prototype of this design.

The Teylers specimen is among the finest surviving examples of this type of instrument, in high-quality mahogany with boxwood and brass fittings.
