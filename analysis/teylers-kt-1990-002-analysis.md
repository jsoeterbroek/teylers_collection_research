## Object Description

A deeply personal mixed-media drawing by **Marlene Dumas** (b. 1953, Cape Town), made in 1989 during the final days of her pregnancy with her daughter **Helena** (born 11 April 1989). The work shows Dumas' heavily pregnant body in a deliberately raw, unromanticized manner — a programmatic subversion of the idealized mother-and-child imagery that dominates the rest of Teylers' Old Master collection. Dumas is widely regarded as **one of the most important living figurative painters**. In May 2025, her painting *Miss January* sold at Christie's for **$13.63 million** — the record for the most expensive work by a living woman artist at auction. In November 2025, she became the **first contemporary woman artist to enter the Louvre's permanent collection**.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. DUMAS' EXTRAORDINARY STATURE NOT INDICATED

The catalog lists "Marlene Dumas (1953)" without context. She is:

- One of the most important living painters in the world
- Auction record: **$13.63 million** (Christie's New York, May 2025) — most expensive work by a living woman at auction
- **First contemporary woman artist in the Louvre's permanent collection** (November 2025, nine-painting series *Liaisons*)
- Major retrospective *open-end* at Palazzo Grassi, Venice (2022–2023) — first woman shown in the Pinault Collection's Venice venues
- Held by MoMA, Tate, Centre Pompidou, Stedelijk Museum Amsterdam, LACMA, Art Institute of Chicago, and SFMoMA

### 2. AUTOBIOGRAPHICAL SIGNIFICANCE NOT EXPLAINED

The inscription "The last days. 1989" is transcribed but not explained. The work was made in the final days before the birth of Helena, Dumas' only child with painter **Jan Andriesse** (1953–2020, cousin of her dealer Paul Andriesse). This is one of a series of pregnancy works including *Pregnant Image* (1988–90) and *Pregnant woman as a baby* (1989). The drawing is based on a self-made photograph — Dumas never works from live models, only from photographic and printed sources. This photographic mediation is characteristic and significant.

### 3. OLD MASTER DIALOGUE NOT ARTICULATED

The catalog does not explain why Teylers — a museum renowned for Michelangelo, Rembrandt, and Raphael drawings — holds contemporary work. Pieter Teyler's founding charter (1778) mandated the encouragement of **"living art"** alongside the historical collection. This acquisition is consistent with a 200+ year tradition. The technique (red and brown chalk on brown paper) directly echoes Old Master *trois crayons* methods — connecting Dumas' drawing to the Rubens and Watteau tradition that Teylers also holds. The museum itself frames the work as a deliberate counterpoint to its "many classical drawings and prints on mother-and-child themes."

### 4. TITLE PHRASE "AS SEEN" NOT GLOSSED

The parenthetical "(as seen before giving birth)" implies mediation — "as seen" in a photograph, not in a mirror. This is a key interpretive point: Dumas' entire practice is based on working from photographs, never from life. The catalog transcribes the inscription but does not explain this distinction.

### 5. MEDIUM DESCRIPTION SLIGHTLY AMBIGUOUS

The catalog states "Brush in grey; red and brown chalk" — it is unclear whether "grey" refers to ink wash or diluted chalk. The Dutch phrasing "Penseel in grijs, Krijt, rood en bruin" is standard museum telegraphic notation but could be more explicit for non-specialist readers.
