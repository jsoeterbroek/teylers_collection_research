## Object Description

TMNK 02269 is a large gold medal — one of the most imposing in the Teylers numismatic collection — awarded posthumously by the Staten-Generaal (States-General of the Dutch Republic) to the family of Wolter Jan Gerrit baron Bentinck (1745–1781), in recognition of his conduct at the Battle of the Doggersbank (5 August 1781). Bentinck commanded the 54-gun ship *Batavier* in the Dutch squadron under Vice-Admiral Johan Arnold Zoutman during this engagement, which took place in the North Sea during the Fourth Anglo-Dutch War (1780–1784). He died of his wounds on 24 August 1781, nineteen days after the battle.

The Battle of the Doggersbank was fought between a Dutch convoy escort and a British squadron under Vice-Admiral Sir Hyde Parker. Both sides suffered heavy casualties; neither achieved a decisive tactical victory, but the Dutch merchant convoy successfully reached port. In the Netherlands, the battle was celebrated as a vindication of Dutch naval honour after decades of decline. Zoutman became a national hero. The States-General awarded a small number of large gold honor medals on gold chains — known as the "Erepenning van de Staten-Generaal" — to the most senior officers: Zoutman, several captains-at-sea (Dedel, Van Braam, Van Kinsbergen), and posthumously Bentinck.

This medal is distinct from the oval "Doggersbank-medaille" (Betts-588; Van Loon Supplement 563) designed by Franciscus Hemsterhuis and engraved by Johann Heinrich Schepp, which was awarded more widely to all officers and petty officers present at the battle. The Teylers piece is the rarer, heavier States-General type — a prestige award on a gold chain.

The gold medal is screwed (it unscrews, presumably to allow it to be hung on a chain or worn as a decorative object). At 90.58 mm diameter and 811 g, it is an exceptionally large and heavy gold object — one of the largest and heaviest single medals in Dutch eighteenth-century medallurgy.

## Key Findings

### 1. FATAL ATTRIBUTION ERROR: NICOLAAS VAN SWINDEREN DIED IN 1760 — 21 YEARS BEFORE THIS MEDAL WAS MADE

This is the most significant error in the catalog record. The Teylers catalog attributes this 1781 medal to **Nicolaas van Swinderen (1705–1760)** as medailleur. This is **chronologically impossible**: Van Swinderen died on 3 July 1760, more than twenty-one years before the Battle of the Doggersbank took place. He cannot have made a medal commemorating events of August 1781.

Nicolaas van Swinderen was the leading medal engraver working for the Stadtholder's court in The Hague during the 1730s–1750s, producing medals for William IV and his family. He was active and important in his time. But he was dead when this medal was struck.

The actual maker of the related Doggersbank commemorative medal — the oval captain's type (Betts-588; Van Loon Supplement 563) — was **Johann Georg Holtzhey** (14 August 1726 – 20 February 1808), the foremost Dutch medal engraver of the second half of the eighteenth century, working in Amsterdam as mint master and medal cutter. The Royal Museums Greenwich (MEC0255) and the Rijksmuseum (KOG-MP-2-0399) both attribute the 1781 Doggersbank medal type to **Johan George Holtzhey**.

The Teylers record may represent a confusion arising from the use of older dies or a misread archival attribution in earlier provenance documentation. It is also possible that the gold States-General type (TMNK 02269) was struck from different dies than the silver captain's type, and that its maker has not been definitively established. However, **Van Swinderen is definitively ruled out** by his death date. The catalog should either attribute this medal to Johan Georg Holtzhey (if documentation supports it) or list the maker as "anonymous" or "uncertain" pending further research.

This error, if accepted, would fundamentally misidentify the object's place in Dutch medallurgical history.

### 2. TYPOGRAPHICAL ERROR IN EDGE INSCRIPTION: "BELOONINIG" IS A MISSPELLING

The catalog transcribes the edge inscription as:

> "TER **BELOONINIG** VAN DE DAPPERHEIT..."

"Belooninig" is not a word in Dutch. The correct word is **"beloning"** (reward/remuneration). This appears to be either:
- A transcription error by the cataloger, or
- An actual misspelling that exists on the physical medal itself

Visual inspection of the medal images confirms the edge inscription text exists, but the image resolution does not allow confident letter-by-letter verification. If the error is on the medal itself, that is a significant historical fact about the object and should be noted explicitly (e.g., "sic" with explanation). If it is a cataloger's transcription error, it must be corrected. Either way, the current record does not flag this as either a known error or a confirmed reading.

### 3. ISSUING AUTHORITY INCORRECT: STATES-GENERAL, NOT "DUTCH REPUBLIC — HOLLAND PROVINCE"

The catalog attributes the issuing authority to "Republiek der Zeven Verenigde Nederlanden, Holland." The medal was awarded by the **Staten-Generaal** (States-General of the Dutch Republic) — this is explicitly stated in the medal's own title as recorded by Teylers ("Beloningspenning van de **Staten-Generaal**..."). The States-General was the federal assembly of all seven provinces, not a Holland provincial body. The award was made at the national level, not provincially, to honor Bentinck's services during a nationally significant naval engagement.

Listing "Holland" as the issuing authority is inconsistent with the medal's own title and misrepresents the constitutional significance of the award.

### 4. WEIGHT OF 811 GRAMS REQUIRES VERIFICATION AND CONTEXT

The stated weight of **811 grams** for a gold medal of 90.58 mm diameter is extraordinary. For reference:
- The Rijksmuseum's comparable Doggersbank medal (KOG-MP-2-0399) is 45 mm diameter and weighs 30.68 g (silver, not gold)
- A solid gold disc of 90 mm diameter and the approximate thickness of a decorative medal would weigh roughly 300–500 g depending on thickness
- 811 grams of gold at 2026 prices represents approximately €65,000–70,000 in bullion value alone

The weight is plausible if the medal is thick and made of high-carat gold, or if it includes the attached chain. The Teylers topstukken page confirms it is a "heavy medal on a chain of golden links" — this is the traditional form of the States-General erepenning (honor medal). If the 811 g figure includes the chain, this must be stated in the catalog. If it is the medal alone, it is an unusually large figure and its basis (weighed on what scale, with or without ring/chain) should be documented.

The catalog does not address this at all.

### 5. REVERSE INSCRIPTION CONTAINS ORDER OF THE GARTER MOTTO

The reverse bears the motto **"HONI · SOIT · QUI · MAL · Y · PENSE"** — the motto of the English Order of the Garter, surrounding the Orange-Nassau arms. This is because the Prince of Orange (William IV and subsequently William V) held the Order of the Garter. The display of the Orange-Nassau arms within the Garter chain on a Dutch Republic medal is iconographically specific and historically significant: it reflects the constitutional position of the Stadtholder-Prince as a holder of foreign orders, and the medal visually links Dutch naval victory under Orange leadership to the prestige of the English royal order — an irony given that this was a medal for fighting the English.

The Teylers catalog transcribes the Garter motto but offers no explanation of it. A reader unfamiliar with English heraldry would have no context for why a Dutch Republic military medal features a French-language English royal motto.

### 6. BENTINCK'S RANK CONFUSED: CAPTAIN AT THE BATTLE, POSTHUMOUSLY ELEVATED

The Teylers catalog title refers to Bentinck as "bevelhebber" (commander). At the Battle of the Doggersbank, Bentinck held the rank of **Captain** (kapitein ter zee), commanding the ship *Batavier*. He was promoted to **Schout-bij-Nacht** (Rear-Admiral) and also appointed Adjudant-Generaal to the Prince of Orange posthumously or in his final days — the gravestone inscription at the Nieuwe Kerk in Amsterdam gives his rank as "Schout bij nacht en Adjudant Generaal van Z.D. Hoogheid als Admiraal Generaal" (Rear-Admiral and Adjutant-General of His Highness as Admiral-General). The title "bevelhebber" is generic and does not specify his actual rank. The RMG portrait caption confirms: "Schout bij nacht van Holland en Westvriesland, Adjudant Generaal van Z.D. Hoogheid als Admiraal Generaal. Overleden aan zyne wonden..." The medal was awarded in recognition of his conduct as captain; his posthumous promotion to rear-admiral is a distinct fact.

### 7. THE MEDAL WAS AWARDED POSTHUMOUSLY

The catalog does not explicitly state that this medal was awarded **posthumously**. Bentinck died on 24 August 1781; the medal was received by his family. The posthumous nature of the award is historically significant — it gives the medal a memorial character beyond a simple reward for valor — and should be noted.
