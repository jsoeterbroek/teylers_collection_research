## Object Description

A gouache on paper (272 x 360 mm) by **Cornelis Troost** (Amsterdam, 1696 – Amsterdam, 1750), dated **1742**, depicting a crowd assembled before the inn **'t Bokje** in the **Haarlemmerhout** to witness an elaborate prank orchestrated by writer-satirist **Jacob Campo Weyerman** (1677–1747) and associates in 1739: a figure exposes painted buttocks arranged to mimic a face in a first-floor window, with blackened trumpeters and mock-ceremonial fanfare, parodying public fascination with the Moroccan ambassador. This is one of at least four treatments Troost made of this subject. Acquired by Teylers in **1780 for 100 guilders** — a considerable sum for a drawing at the time. Designated one of Teylers' **50 Topstukken**.

## Key Findings Not in Teylers' Record

### 1. ARTIST BIRTH YEAR WRONG (MAJOR)

The catalog states **Cornelis Troost (1697–1750)**. The correct birth year is **1696**. Troost was born on **8 October 1696** in Amsterdam. This is confirmed by every authoritative source: the Rijksmuseum, Getty Museum, Art UK, the RKD, and — most definitively — the standard catalogue raisonné, **J.W. Niemeijer, *Cornelis Troost 1696–1750*** (Assen, 1973), whose very title carries the correct year. The error appears in both the AdLib database and the public Topstukken page.

### 2. DATE RANGE "CIRCA 1717 – VOOR 1750" IS FACTUALLY IMPOSSIBLE (MAJOR)

The catalog gives the date as **"circa 1717 – voor 1750."** This is demonstrably wrong. The prank depicted at 't Bokje in the Haarlemmerhout took place in **1739** — the earliest conceivable date for this composition. The drawing itself is consistently dated to **1742** by the Niemeijer catalogue raisonné (cat. no. **890T**), the DBNL scholarly article (Lange-van der Meulen, 2016), and the Librariana research survey. "Circa 1717" appears to be a generic career-start placeholder (Troost's earliest paintings date from c.1717–1720) erroneously applied to this specific work. The correct date is **1742**.

### 3. TECHNIQUE DESCRIBED AS "WATERVERF" — SHOULD BE GOUACHE (MAJOR)

The catalog records the medium as **"penseel in waterverf"** (brush in watercolor). Multiple independent sources — including the Teylers Museum's own public Topstukken page, the Librariana article, and the DBNL scholarly text — consistently describe the work as **gouache** (opaque watercolor: *penseel in dekkende waterverf*). This distinction is not trivial: gouaches were valued specifically as *papierconst* (painting on paper) by 18th-century collectors, explaining the high 1780 acquisition price of 100 guilders. The Librariana article explicitly notes: *"De gouache in het Teylers Museum... geschilderd met penseel en dekkende verf op papier."* Calling it "watercolor" is both technically incorrect and historically misleading. Additionally, the support is **blue paper**, not mentioned in the catalog.

### 4. VERSO INSCRIPTION ENTIRELY ABSENT (MAJOR)

The catalog lists four inscriptions (C.Troost fc; T 68; Teyler; Troost T.29) — all inventory and ownership marks. It entirely omits the significant **verso inscription** documented in scholarly literature: **"Vertooning van den Ambassadeur van Maroccen in den hout"** (Display of the Ambassador of Morocco in the woods), followed by additional lines (later illegible). This is effectively Troost's own title for the work and the most direct evidence for interpreting its satirical meaning. Its omission from the inscriptions field is a major scholarly gap.

### 5. NIEMEIJER CATALOGUE RAISONNÉ ABSENT FROM BIBLIOGRAPHY (MAJOR)

The bibliography cites only the 1904 Scholten catalogue, without specifying an entry number. The standard modern scholarly reference — **J.W. Niemeijer, *Cornelis Troost 1696–1750*** (Assen: Van Gorcum, 1973), cat. no. **890T** — is entirely absent. This is the primary reference for all Troost works and is cited by every major institution holding Troost drawings (Rijksmuseum, Mauritshuis, auction houses). Its absence makes the record unusable for scholarly cross-referencing.

### 6. NO PROVENANCE IN DATABASE RECORD (MAJOR)

The database record contains no provenance. The acquisition facts (purchased **1780, 100 guilders**) appear only on the public-facing Topstukken website, not in the AdLib catalog. No pre-1780 ownership chain is given. Contextually important: **Cornelis Ploos van Amstel** (1726–1798) — Troost's son-in-law (married to Troost's daughter Elisabeth) and the major collector of Troost's work — owned the related 1748 gouache now in the Hermitage. The Pelletier engraving referenced in the catalog was made after Ploos van Amstel's drawing, not the Teylers version. This network is entirely undocumented.

### 7. HISTORICAL EVENT NOT IDENTIFIED (MINOR)

The description notes a "groteske figuur" without explaining the event depicted. The prank was organized by **Jacob Campo Weyerman** (1677–1747) — Dutch painter, journalist, and satirist — who staged a mock-ceremonial presentation of a "Moroccan ambassador" to mock Dutch public fascination with Moroccan diplomacy following the 1736 Dutch embassy to Morocco. The subject tag "toneelscene" (theater scene) is misleading: this was a real public prank, not a theatrical performance.

### 8. RELATED VERSIONS NOT CITED (MINOR)

The catalog makes no reference to the other known treatments of this subject by Troost:
- Drawing (1739) — Koninklijk Oudheidkundig Genootschap, Amsterdam (Niemeijer 891T) — earliest version
- Oil on canvas (c. 1739–1750) — Rijksmuseum Amsterdam (SK-A-4089; Niemeijer 892S) — large-scale monumental version, 157 × 104 cm
- Gouache (1748) — State Hermitage Museum, St. Petersburg (Niemeijer 893T) — source for Pelletier engraving

### 9. PELLETIER PRINT NOTE AMBIGUOUS AND POTENTIALLY MISLEADING (MINOR)

The note **"dans le cabinet Ploos van Amstel"** could be read as implying the Teylers drawing was in Ploos van Amstel's cabinet. In fact, Pelletier's engraving (c. 1757–1764) was made after the **1748 Hermitage gouache** (Ploos van Amstel's drawing), not after the Teylers version. The note needs clarification.

### 10. "HOTEL 'T BOKJE" IS ANACHRONISTIC (MINOR)

The description reads "Exterieur van hotel 't Bokje." Every scholarly and contemporary source — the Rijksmuseum, the DBNL article, local history sources — correctly calls it **"herberg 't Bokje"** (inn/tavern). "Hotel" is an anachronistic modernism inconsistent with historical usage.
